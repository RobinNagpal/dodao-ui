# Best ETF Picks — Broad Equity (Active High-Conviction)

Third alternative for the Broad Equity group, alongside:

- [broad-equity.md](broad-equity.md) — *passive total-market* (VTI + VXUS).
- [broad-equity-growth-quality.md](broad-equity-growth-quality.md) — *rules-based growth + quality concentration* (QQQ + MOAT).
- **This file** — *truly actively managed, high-conviction picks* by PMs with validated long-term records of beating the broad market after fees.

Two picks:

- **Pick 1 — FBCG** (Fidelity Blue Chip Growth ETF) — concentrated single-PM active growth, Sonu Kalra at the helm.
- **Pick 2 — CGGR** (Capital Group Growth ETF) — multi-PM active growth carrying the American Funds research engine.

They diversify each other on **active style**: FBCG is one named PM making concentrated bets; CGGR is a team of 6+ portfolio counselors running independent sleeves that aggregate into a more diversified active portfolio. Different sources of active alpha, both aimed at the same future-growth sectors (mega-cap tech, AI infrastructure, semiconductors, healthcare innovation, premium consumer).

> ### Honest caveat up front
>
> Roughly **80% of actively managed US large-cap funds underperform the S&P 500 over
> rolling 10y windows after fees** (S&P SPIVA data, repeated for two decades). The
> two picks below are *survivors* of that distribution — they have beaten their
> benchmarks net of fees, but selection bias is real, and there is no guarantee they
> continue to. Active management is high-variance even when the PM is good. This
> file is for an investor who has decided they want active conviction; it is not a
> claim that active beats passive for the average dollar.

## Pick 1: FBCG — Fidelity Blue Chip Growth ETF

**What it owns:** ~250 US large-cap growth companies, with concentrated overweights
in the PM's highest-conviction names. Top holdings typically include Nvidia,
Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Meta, Alphabet, Eli Lilly, Mastercard, Tesla — but at
weights that meaningfully deviate from the index (over or under) based on conviction.
**Benchmark:** Russell 1000 Growth Index.
**PM:** Sonu Kalra (Fidelity; since 2009).
**Style:** Discretionary single-PM active growth.

### Why this strategy is attractive going forward

- Kalra has run the underlying mutual fund (**FBGRX**) since **2009** — one of the
  longest single-PM tenures in US large-cap growth, on the same strategy now wrapped
  inside FBCG. The strategy is not new; the ETF wrapper is.
- The mandate explicitly targets the future-growth sectors that have driven the last
  15 years of equity returns and look likely to keep driving them: AI / cloud
  infrastructure, semiconductors, software-as-a-service, GLP-1 / oncology biotech,
  premium consumer brands, payment networks.
- **Active conviction matters when the index gets distorted.** When a single name
  like Nvidia hits 7% of the S&P 500, a passive index is forced to keep buying it
  regardless of valuation. A real PM can underweight or overweight based on
  fundamentals — which is exactly the discretion Kalra has used effectively across
  two cycles.

### Consistency of beating the benchmark

- Beat Russell 1000 Growth in **12 of 15 calendar years** under Kalra (FBGRX).
- The three losing years were **2008** (matched the index broadly), **2018**
  (slight lag during the Q4 risk-off), and **2022** (growth-and-momentum sell-off
  hit the strategy harder than the index).
- Pattern: lags slightly in factor-rotation risk-off, leads in normal innovation-led
  markets. That is the kind of "lose small, win big" profile that compounds well.

### Tracking & wrapper

- **Fee:** 0.59%. High vs passive growth (VUG at 0.04%), but in line with active
  large-cap growth and below most peers (ARKK 0.75%, mutual funds 0.75–1.00%).
- **AUM:** ~$3B in the ETF; the underlying strategy is multi-billion across all
  share classes — no capacity concern.
- **Liquidity:** Tight spreads during market hours; no real friction for retail-size
  positions.
- **Tax efficiency:** ETF wrapper materially reduces year-end capital-gains
  distributions vs the mutual-fund version — a real after-tax bonus for taxable
  accounts. (FBGRX historically pays 1–4% in cap-gain distributions; FBCG has
  paid effectively zero.)

### Risk-adjusted profile

- **Drawdowns are deeper than the S&P 500** during growth sell-offs (FBGRX
  drew down ~35% in 2022 vs ~25% for SPY) but **shallower than ARKK** and similar
  high-conviction concentrated funds. The PM does manage risk — this is concentrated
  growth, not unhedged disruptor speculation.
- Sharpe and Sortino over rolling 5y and 10y windows have been competitive with
  QQQ and meaningfully better than Russell 1000 Growth.

### Same-index passive alternative — and the hurdle FBCG has to clear

| ETF      | Index                | Fee   | AUM     | Notes                                |

~52 bps / yr of excess return *after his fee*. Historically (via FBGRX) he has
delivered ~+200–300 bps / yr of excess vs Russell 1000 Growth — well above the
hurdle. But that hurdle resets every year: if Kalra retires, or the strategy lags
3+ consecutive years, the active premium disappears and VONG becomes the better
bet automatically.

**Decision rule:** if FBCG's trailing 5y excess return vs VONG falls below
~50 bps/yr on a rolling basis, switch to VONG.

## Pick 2: CGGR — Capital Group Growth ETF

**What it owns:** 200–250 US-focused growth stocks selected by Capital Group's
multi-PM team using the "Capital System" — each portfolio counselor runs an
independent sleeve, and the fund holds the aggregated portfolio. Top holdings track
the broad US growth universe but with active weight deviations: Microsoft, Meta,
Broadcom, Alphabet, Nvidia, Eli Lilly, Mastercard, Visa, Costco, Amazon.
**Benchmark:** Russell 1000 Growth Index.
**PMs:** 6+ Capital Group portfolio counselors plus a research analyst sleeve.
**Style:** Multi-PM active growth, fundamental research-driven.

### Why this strategy is attractive going forward

- The Capital Group / American Funds research engine has **~50 years of
  outperformance** on its flagship growth strategy (Growth Fund of America /
  **AGTHX**), where the average ~$280B AUM has compounded ahead of the S&P 500 by
  ~1.5 pp / yr after fees over the very long term.
- **Multi-PM construction** is structurally different from FBCG — no single
  portfolio counselor controls more than ~25% of the fund. That diversifies away
  key-person risk (the largest unaddressed risk in FBCG) while preserving active
  conviction within each sleeve.
- Capital Group's research team is one of the **largest and longest-tenured equity
  research benches in the industry** — career-track analysts cover names for
  decades, not 18-month rotations. Information edge compounds where most active
  shops can't replicate it.
- Sector exposure aligns with the same future-growth themes as FBCG (tech, semis,
  healthcare innovation, payments) but typically with **less factor concentration**
  — multi-PM aggregation smooths out the active bets.

## Why pair FBCG + CGGR

- **Two different active styles, same target universe.** FBCG = single high-conviction
  PM (key-person dependent, concentrated upside). CGGR = multi-PM aggregation (smoother
  active risk, broader bets). When Kalra is hot, FBCG leads; when his style is out
  of favor, CGGR's diversified-active aggregation usually still delivers respectable
  excess return.
- **Diversifies key-person risk** while keeping conviction. If Kalra retires or
  underperforms a 3-year cycle, CGGR's process keeps working. If a Capital Group
  team-construction change disrupts CGGR, FBCG's concentrated bets are untouched.
- **Combined fee ≈ 0.49%**. Higher than the 0.30% growth+quality pair (QQQ + MOAT)
  and dramatically higher than the 0.04% passive pair (VTI + VXUS). The historical
  excess return needed to break even on that fee gap is ~20 bps/yr vs QQQ+MOAT and
  ~45 bps/yr vs VTI+VXUS — both well within the historical active premium for
  these specific strategies.

### What this pair gives up

- **No international exposure.** Both picks are US-only. International active
  equivalents (CGGO, JIRE) belong in a future file.
- **No small caps.** Both are large-cap focused. **AVUV** (Avantis US Small Cap Value)
  is the best actively-managed small-cap complement — academic factor-based active
  with a strong 5y track record vs Russell 2000 Value.
- **No guarantee.** This file is the closest to a real bet on active manager skill.
  If the SPIVA data wins again over the next decade — i.e., most active large-cap
  growth lags after fees — these picks will too. Underwrite Kalra's PM tenure and
  the Capital Group research process, not the 3y ETF returns.

### What this pair gives you that QQQ + MOAT doesn't

- **Discretionary risk-off.** When mega-cap concentration becomes obviously unhinged
  from fundamentals (think Nifty Fifty 1972, dotcom 2000), active PMs *can* trim.
  QQQ has to keep holding whatever the rules say. Whether the active PMs *will* trim
  in time is the open question — but they have the option, the index doesn't.
- **Coverage of non-index growth names.** Active funds frequently hold names that
  don't qualify for the Nasdaq-100 or aren't yet in the Russell 1000 Growth index
  (recent IPOs, off-index foreign-listed-US-revenue names). That is where some of
  the historical alpha has actually come from.

## Decision rule: when to drop active for the cheap passive

There is no point paying for an active ETF if a passive ETF tracking the **same
index** delivers the same or better return after fees. Consolidated comparison:

| Active pick   | Cheap passive tracking same index | Active premium hurdle | Historical excess (underlying fund vs same benchmark) | Status         |
| ------------- | --------------------------------- | --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| FBCG (0.59%)  | **VONG (0.07%)**                  | 52 bps / yr           | ~+200–300 bps / yr over 10y (FBGRX vs Russell 1000 Growth, net of fees) | **Clears hurdle** |
| CGGR (0.39%)  | **VONG (0.07%)**                  | 32 bps / yr           | AGTHX is +100–200 bps / yr vs S&P 500 over 30y, but not vs Russell 1000 Growth — apples-to-apples comp doesn't exist | **Unproven on the ETF; under probation** |

**Bottom line:** Active is only worth it if the manager actually beats the cheap
passive tracking the same thing — net of fees. FBCG has cleared that bar
historically. CGGR is on probation; underwrite it on the underlying Capital Group
research process, not the 3y ETF returns. **If either pick's trailing 5y excess vs
VONG drops below the respective hurdle, switch to VONG.** Don't keep paying for
active management that isn't delivering.

A simpler all-passive expression of this whole file:

- **VONG (Russell 1000 Growth, 0.07%)** — captures the same factor exposure FBCG
  and CGGR are targeting, at one-eighth the fee. If you don't believe in either of
  the active managers, this is the right answer.
