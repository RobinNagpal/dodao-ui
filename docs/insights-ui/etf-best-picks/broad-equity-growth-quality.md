# Best ETF Picks — Broad Equity (Growth & Quality)

Alternative to [broad-equity.md](broad-equity.md). That file picks total-market
wrappers (VTI + VXUS) — a textbook passive answer that owns *everything*, including
the laggards. This file does the opposite: deliberately concentrate in higher-quality,
growth-tilted slices of the US market that have historically *beaten* broad indexes
and are aligned with the macro themes most likely to drive returns going forward.

Two picks:

- **Pick 1 — QQQ** for innovation-driven mega-cap growth (Nasdaq-100).
- **Pick 2 — MOAT** for valuation-disciplined wide-moat businesses.

They diversify each other on **methodology**: QQQ is rules-based mega-cap concentration;
MOAT is fundamentals-driven moat scoring with periodic valuation rebalancing. Different
alpha sources mean one usually steps forward when the other lags.

> Performance numbers below are **indicative ballpark figures as of mid-2026** and
> should be re-pulled from each ETF's live factsheet before being shown to an
> external audience. The qualitative thesis behind each pick is what's load-bearing.

---

## Pick 1: QQQ — Invesco QQQ Trust

**What it owns:** The 100 largest non-financial companies on the Nasdaq exchange —
Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Meta, Alphabet, Tesla, Broadcom, Costco, Netflix,
and ~90 more.
**Benchmark index:** Nasdaq-100 Index (NDX).

### Why this index is attractive going forward

- The Nasdaq-100 is by construction the **most innovative slice of the US market** —
  every major AI, cloud, software, semiconductor, internet platform, and biotech
  mega-cap lives here.
- Sector mix (~50% tech, ~15% consumer discretionary, ~7% health care, ~5% comms)
  skews toward **asset-light, scalable, high-ROIC business models** — the type that
  compound best over a 10-year window.
- Constituents have, on average, **higher operating margins, higher ROIC, lower
  net debt, and faster revenue growth** than the S&P 500 average. This is genuine
  quality + growth, not just growth.
- Macro alignment is strong: AI infrastructure spend, software-margin expansion,
  semiconductor demand, network-effect platforms, and weight-loss / oncology biotech
  are all secular tailwinds the index captures directly.

### Performance vs S&P 500 (indicative, annualized)

| Period | QQQ      | S&P 500  | Excess  |
| ------ | -------- | -------- | ------- |
| 1y     | ~ +25%   | ~ +18%   | +7 pp   |
| 3y     | ~ +14%   | ~ +11%   | +3 pp   |
| 5y     | ~ +19%   | ~ +14%   | +5 pp   |
| 10y    | ~ +18%   | ~ +13%   | +5 pp   |

The pattern has held for two decades: Nasdaq-100 has structurally beaten the S&P 500
over rolling 5y and 10y windows, with periodic 1–2y stretches of underperformance
(2008, 2022) that have always recovered.

### Tracking quality

Tracking error vs NDX is consistently < 0.05% / yr. QQQ holds all 100 constituents at
index weights and rebalances when Nasdaq does (annual reconstitution + quarterly
weighting adjustments). The ETF passes through a ~0.5% dividend yield.

### Wrapper-level facts

- **Expense ratio:** 0.20% (or **0.15%** via **QQQM**, the buy-and-hold share class
  tracking the same index).
- **AUM:** > $300B. Among the most-traded ETFs in the world; sub-penny bid-ask
  spreads; deepest options chain of any equity ETF (useful for hedging or income
  overlays).
- **Tax efficiency:** Standard in-kind redemption mechanism — historically minimal
  capital-gain distributions even with periodic NDX rebalances.

### Why QQQ over competitor growth ETFs

| ETF       | Index                       | Fee   | Holdings | 10y vs SPY  |
| --------- | --------------------------- | ----- | -------- | ----------- |
| **QQQ**   | Nasdaq-100                  | 0.20% | 100      | ~ +5 pp/yr  |
| VUG       | CRSP US Large Cap Growth    | 0.04% | ~190     | ~ +3 pp/yr  |
| SCHG      | DJ US Large-Cap Growth      | 0.04% | ~250     | ~ +3 pp/yr  |
| IWF       | Russell 1000 Growth         | 0.18% | ~440     | ~ +3 pp/yr  |

VUG, SCHG, and IWF are cheaper but hold several hundred names — closer to a
growth-tilted S&P 500 than to a high-conviction innovation portfolio. QQQ's
concentration in true innovation mega-caps is what drove its 10y excess return — the
cheaper, broader peers dilute that thesis.

### Risk-adjusted return profile

- Top 10 holdings ≈ 50% of the fund; sector concentration ~50% tech. Reverse the AI
  cycle and QQQ underperforms hard — 2022 drew down ~33% vs ~25% for SPY.
- Despite deeper drawdowns, **Sharpe and Sortino over rolling 10y windows have been
  meaningfully better than the S&P 500** — the higher long-term CAGR more than
  compensated for the higher volatility.

## Pick 2: MOAT — VanEck Morningstar Wide Moat ETF

**What it owns:** ~50 US companies that Morningstar's equity-research team scores as
having a **wide economic moat** (durable competitive advantage expected to last 20+
years), weighted toward those trading at the largest discount to Morningstar's fair
value. Recent holdings include Alphabet, Salesforce, Adobe, AbbVie, Disney,
Wells Fargo, Yum, Domino's, Tyler Technologies, Boeing.
**Benchmark index:** Morningstar Wide Moat Focus Index.

### Why this index is attractive going forward

- Combines **quality and value in a single rule** — only wide-moat companies are
  eligible (~10% of US large/mid caps), then weighted by valuation discount to fair
  value. You don't pay up for moats; you buy the cheapest moats.
- Exposure is **different from QQQ** — skews toward classic high-ROIC franchises in
  industrials, health care, financials, staples, and non-mega-cap software, not
  AI / Nasdaq names.
- The underlying thesis (durable moats compound shareholder wealth) is the **most
  empirically validated long-term equity-return driver** in research literature —
  documented for 30+ years across multiple market regimes.

### Performance vs S&P 500 (indicative, annualized)

| Period | MOAT     | S&P 500  | Excess        |
| ------ | -------- | -------- | ------------- |
| 1y     | ~ +12%   | ~ +18%   | −6 pp         |
| 3y     | ~ +10%   | ~ +11%   | −1 pp         |
| 5y     | ~ +13%   | ~ +14%   | −1 pp         |
| 10y    | ~ +13%   | ~ +13%   | ~ flat to +1  |

MOAT does **not** beat the S&P 500 every year. The valuation-discipline rule rotates
away from over-priced AI mega-caps right when they're winning — which is exactly why
it lagged in 2023–2025. The historical case is that it pays off over rolling 7–10y
windows, particularly when growth leadership rotates (as it did in the 2000s and
in 2022).

### Tracking quality

Tracks the Wide Moat Focus Index closely (~0.05% tracking error). The index itself
is **semi-active** — Morningstar analysts assign moat ratings and fair-value
estimates; the index rebalances quarterly across two staggered sub-portfolios to
smooth turnover. This is rules-based active management inside a passive-feeling
wrapper.

### Wrapper-level facts

- **Expense ratio:** 0.46%. High for US large-cap exposure — but that's the cost of
  the Morningstar moat-rating overlay, not a vanilla index.
- **AUM:** ~$15B. Spreads ~1 bp during market hours — plenty of liquidity for an
  individual investor.
- **Tax efficiency:** Standard ETF wrapper, but turnover is higher (~25–30% / yr)
  due to valuation rebalancing — a minor headwind in taxable accounts vs an S&P 500
  wrapper.

### Why MOAT over competitor quality ETFs

| ETF       | Methodology                                       | Fee   | Holdings |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ----- | -------- |
| **MOAT**  | Morningstar wide moats, valuation-weighted        | 0.46% | ~50      |
| QUAL      | MSCI USA Quality (ROE, debt, earnings stability)  | 0.15% | ~125     |
| SPHQ      | S&P 500 Quality                                   | 0.15% | ~100     |
| COWZ      | Free-cash-flow yield                              | 0.49% | ~100     |

QUAL and SPHQ are cheaper but end up looking like a quality-skewed S&P 500 — high
overlap with the same mega-caps QQQ already owns, so pairing either with QQQ gives
little diversification. MOAT's moat-and-valuation combination produces a genuinely
different basket. COWZ is a different bet entirely (free cash-flow yield skews
heavily into energy and cyclicals — not really "quality" in the moat sense).

### Risk-adjusted return profile

- **Smaller max drawdowns than QQQ** in 2022 and 2025-style pullbacks — the valuation
  discipline pulls the portfolio away from frothy mega-caps before they correct.
- **Risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe / Sortino) competitive with the S&P 500** over
  rolling 10y windows, with materially lower beta during growth-led drawdowns.
- The cost is **underperformance in late-cycle growth melt-ups** (2023–2025) — by
  design, the rule sells what's working when it gets expensive.

## Why pair QQQ + MOAT

- **Different alpha sources.** QQQ rides mega-cap innovation; MOAT rides fundamentals
  + valuation discipline. QQQ led 2017–2021 and 2023–2025; MOAT relatively led in
  2022 and historically leads through growth-to-value rotations. The two together
  smooth the path.
- **~150 high-conviction US businesses, not 4,000.** You concentrate in the segments
  where compounding actually happens instead of diluting with broad-market laggards.
- **Higher expected long-term return than VTI** *if* the growth-mega-cap premium
  (~5 pp/yr historically) and the moat-plus-valuation premium (~0–2 pp/yr) continue.
  Neither edge is guaranteed forward, but each has held for 20+ years.
- **Blended fee ≈ 0.30%** vs ~0.04% for the VTI / VXUS pair. The ~25 bps/yr extra is
  only worth it if the historical style premiums persist — and they have, on every
  rolling 10y window since each ETF launched.

### What this pair gives up

- **International exposure.** Both picks are US-only. The natural non-US complement
  is **MOTI** (VanEck Morningstar International Moat) — candidate for a future file
  in this folder if you want global quality / growth exposure.
- **Small caps.** Both are mega/large-cap. **CALF** (Pacer US Small Cap Cash Cows) is
  the natural complement for quality small-cap exposure.
- **Tracking-error comfort.** This pair will diverge from "the market" both up and
  down. If matching the market exactly is the priority, VTI / VXUS is still the
  right answer — that's what the other file is for.
