# Bedsheets / Towels from India — Detailed Tariff & Cost Reduction Guide

> **For beginners:** Read [basic-concepts.md](./basic-concepts.md) first if you don't know words like HTS code, MFN, FTZ, drawback, AD/CVD, etc. This guide assumes you understand those terms.

---

## 1. The Product

- **What it is:** Cotton bedsheets, pillowcases, bath towels, hand towels, and other home textiles ("home linens").
- **Country of origin:** India.
- **Why India:** India is the **largest cotton producer in the world** and the **#1 source of U.S. cotton home textiles**. Home textile manufacturing is concentrated in Tamil Nadu (Karur, Erode), Gujarat (Surat), and Haryana (Panipat).

## 2. The HTS Code

Home textiles classification depends on:

1. **Type of item** (sheets vs. towels vs. quilt covers vs. duvet covers)
2. **Fiber composition** (cotton vs. blends vs. synthetic)
3. **Construction** (woven vs. terry vs. knit)
4. **Finish** (printed vs. plain, bleached vs. unbleached, embroidered or not)

| HTS Code | Description | MFN Duty |
|---|---|---|
| **6302.31.9020** | Cotton bed linen, plain woven, not printed | **6.7%** |
| 6302.31.9040 | Cotton bed linen, printed | 6.7% |
| 6302.21 | Printed cotton bed linen, woven | 6.7% |
| **6302.60.0020** | Cotton terry bath/hand towels | **9.1%** |
| 6302.60.0010 | Cotton terry kitchen/dish towels | 9.1% |
| 6304.91 | Cotton furnishing articles (other) | varies |
| 5701.10.1300 | Knotted carpets, of wool | varies |

**Key insight:** Towels (9.1%) have a higher MFN rate than bedsheets (6.7%). Same room of the house, different duties.

| Component | Rate | Why it applies |

> **AD/CVD note:** India does have active U.S. AD/CVD orders on **other textile categories** (e.g., polyester staple fiber, PET film, certain yarns). These do NOT apply to basic cotton bedsheets or terry towels — but **always verify your specific HTS code** against the [trade.gov AD/CVD search](https://www.trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties).

## 4. Landed Cost Calculation on $10,000 of Cotton Towels

```
Product value (FOB):              $10,000.00
MFN duty (9.1%):                  $   910.00
Section 122 (10%):                $ 1,000.00
MPF (0.3464%, min $33.58):        $    34.64
HMF (0.125%, ocean only):         $    12.50
                                  -----------
Total LANDED COST:                $11,957.14
Effective tariff rate:            19.57%
```

For **bedsheets** (6.7% MFN):

```
MFN duty (6.7%):                  $   670.00
Section 122 (10%):                $ 1,000.00
MPF + HMF:                        $    47.14
                                  -----------
Total LANDED COST:                $11,717.14
Effective tariff rate:            17.17%
```

(India → U.S. ocean shipping for a small container shipment is roughly **$1,500–$3,000**, not included.)

## 5. Detailed Ways to Reduce the Tariff Cost

### Option A: Time the Imports — Wait for Section 122 to Expire

**This is the simplest, biggest, no-effort lever for India home textiles right now.**

The Section 122 10% surcharge **expires around July 24, 2026** unless Congress extends it.

#### Math on $10,000 of towels:

- Import today (April 2026): $1,910 in duty.
- Import after July 24, 2026: $910 in duty.
- **Saving: $1,000.**

#### How to actually do this:

1. **Bonded warehouse strategy.** Ship the goods to the U.S. now, but route them straight into a bonded warehouse. Don't clear customs. When Section 122 expires in July, clear the goods at the new (lower) rate.
2. **Foreign-Trade Zone strategy.** Same idea — keep goods inside an FTZ until July 24, then clear.
3. **Delay shipment from India.** If your inventory permits, just don't ship until June (so it lands after July 24).

#### Risks:

- **Congress could extend Section 122.** It's only a 150-day tariff under current law, and Congress has the power to extend. Watch the news.
- **A new tariff regime could replace Section 122** — possibly at a higher rate.
- **Inventory carrying cost.** Storing in a bonded warehouse for 3–4 months costs ~$0.30–$0.50 per cubic foot per month.

For most importers, even with carrying costs, the $1,000 saving on $10,000 outweighs the storage fees.

### Option B: First Sale Rule

India home-textile supply chains usually go through buying agents:

```
Indian mill (Karur, Panipat, etc.)  →  Buying agent (Dubai/Singapore)  →  U.S. importer  →  U.S. retailer
   ($3.50)                              ($5.00)                           ($7.00)              ($14.99)
```

If you declare on the **first sale** ($3.50) instead of the **last sale** ($5.00 or $7.00), you cut your duty base.

#### Math on $10,000 (first sale ~70% of last sale):

- Last-sale duty: 19.1% × $10,000 = $1,910.
- First-sale duty: 19.1% × $7,000 = **$1,337**.
- **Saving: $573.**

#### Practical setup:

1. Get the **mill's invoice** (price they sold to the agent).
2. Get the **agent's invoice** (price you paid).
3. Confirm goods were destined for the U.S. (purchase orders, U.S. shipping addresses).
4. Use a customs broker familiar with first-sale claims.

#### Hurdles:

- Buying agents in Dubai/Singapore guard their margins and may resist sharing the mill invoice.
- Fix: Some big buyers (Bed Bath & Beyond, Target, Walmart) require open-book vendor agreements.

### Option C: Tariff Engineering for Home Textiles

#### Tactic 1: Bedsheet vs. Comforter Cover vs. Quilt

- **HTS 6302.31** (bed linen) at 6.7%
- **HTS 9404.90** (quilts, comforters with filling) at 11.4%
- **HTS 6304.92** (furnishing articles, cotton) at 6.3%

A "duvet cover with insert" might fit different categories than a "comforter" — small product changes can move you to lower-duty headings.

#### Tactic 2: Terry vs. Flat Weave

- Terry towels: 9.1% (HTS 6302.60)
- Flat-woven cotton "huck" towels (e.g., kitchen flour-sack towels): often classified differently
- Microfiber towels (synthetic): different HTS, different rate

For premium "non-terry" guest towels or kitchen towels, classification can be more favorable.

#### Tactic 3: Embroidery thresholds

- "Embroidered" home textiles fall under different sub-headings than plain textiles.
- An embroidered bedsheet (HTS 6302.21 vs. 6302.31) may have slightly different duty.
- For highly decorated items, the embroidery classification can be either better or worse — verify.

#### Tactic 4: Fiber blend just over/under thresholds

- 100% cotton: 6.7% (sheets)
- 50% cotton + 50% poly blend: classified as the **"chief weight" fiber** — if cotton is heavier, still 6.7%; if poly is heavier, jumps to 6304.93 territory with potentially different rates.
- Double-check fabric specs — accidentally crossing into "synthetic majority" can increase your duty.

#### Tactic 5: Set vs. individual items

- A "sheet set" (fitted + flat + 2 pillowcases) sold together is classified as a set.
- Sets are valued at the **"essential character"** item's HTS rate.
- Sometimes selling separately and pricing carefully changes the classification math.

| China | 9.1% | 0% (post-SCOTUS) | 10% (+Section 301 if applicable) | 19.1%+ | Reduced share |

**India is currently the cheapest source for terry towels** even with the 19.1% duty. Pakistan is an alternative but quality varies. Mexico has limited home-textile capacity but USMCA gives 0% duty if you can find a maker.

#### When to consider moving:

- If you can find a Mexican mill (rare but they exist for hospitality grades).
- If your retail customers value short lead times.

### Option E: Bonded Warehouse / FTZ

As discussed in Option A, these are the **best tools to wait out the Section 122 surcharge**.

For long-term use beyond July 2026:

- **Cash flow:** Pay duty only when sheets/towels leave the warehouse for sale.
- **Returns / re-exports:** No duty on goods that leave the U.S. again.
- **Slow-moving SKUs:** If you over-ordered, you can re-export to off-price retailers internationally.

### Option F: Duty Drawback (and the Re-Export Reality Check)

#### **Re-Export Reality Check for India Cotton Towels:**

Let's say you want to sell in **Canada**.

**Option 1: India → U.S. → Canada (with drawback)**
- India → U.S. ocean: ~$0.50/towel
- U.S. duty: 19.1% × $5 = $0.96
- U.S. → Canada trucking: ~$0.20/towel
- Canada import duty on cotton towels from India: **~17%** (Canada doesn't have the U.S.'s GSP gap on India)
- Drawback refund: -$0.95 (99% of $0.96)
- Net duty/freight cost: ~$0.71/towel

**Option 2: India → Canada directly**
- India → Canada ocean: ~$0.55/towel
- Canada duty: 17% × $5 = $0.85
- Net duty/freight cost: $1.40/towel

Hmm — surprisingly, **drawback can sometimes save money for Canada** because:
- Canada has its own duties on Indian textiles (~17%).
- The U.S. drawback recovers most of the U.S. duty.
- The double-shipping cost is small.

**But** still cheaper to ship direct in most cases when you account for Canada-side processing fees and customs broker fees.

#### For the EU (a big buyer of Indian home textiles):

EU has GSP+ for India. Duty on Indian cotton towels to EU = **~0%** under GSP+ for most items.

- **India → EU directly:** $0.55 freight + $0 duty = $0.55/towel
- **India → U.S. → EU:** $0.50 freight + $0.96 duty + $0.40 air freight + $0 EU duty + drawback refund (-$0.95) = $0.91/towel + admin

**Direct shipping to EU is much cheaper.** EU's preferential treatment of Indian textiles makes routing through the U.S. economically wasteful.

#### Conclusion:

For India home textiles, drawback is useful when:
- You're a U.S.-based brand with U.S. as primary market.
- A small portion of inventory naturally ends up exported.
- You want to recover duty on returns or off-price re-sales.

It's not a strategy to actively pursue.

### Option G: Verify HTS Classification Carefully

Home textiles have **many close-but-different HTS codes**. A mistake can cost (or save) you 3–5 percentage points of duty.

Common misclassification examples:

- "Bedspreads" (HTS 6304.91) vs. "bed linen" (HTS 6302.31) — different rates.
- "Beach towels" (potentially HTS 6302.60) vs. "swimwear cover-ups" (different chapter).
- "Dish towels" vs. "kitchen linens" — small product details matter.

**File a CBP Binding Ruling for any product with ambiguous classification.** It's free, takes ~30 days, and protects you legally.

## 7. The Best Practical Option for India Home Textiles

### **Recommendation: Time imports (bonded warehouse strategy until July 2026) + First Sale Rule for the long term**

**Why:**

- The **Section 122 expiry** is a one-time, near-future event giving a clean **$1,000 saving on $10,000** with minimal effort. Just store goods until late July 2026.
- **First Sale Rule** is the durable lever — it works regardless of what happens with Section 122 and saves another $500+ per $10,000 ongoing.
- **No design changes needed.** No factory moves needed.
- These are the two highest-ROI strategies for any India home-textile importer in 2026.

**Implementation order:**

1. **Now (April–July 2026):** Set up a bonded warehouse arrangement. Ship India goods into bond. Wait for July 24.
2. **Now in parallel:** Get factory invoices from your Indian mill. Set up first-sale documentation.
3. **August 2026:** Clear bonded inventory at the new (lower) rate. Continue with first-sale on new shipments.
4. **Ongoing:** File CBP binding rulings for ambiguous products.

**When to skip:**

- If your import volume is small (<$50k/year) — bonded warehouse fees may eat the savings.
- If your buyer requires immediate stock availability (no time to wait).
