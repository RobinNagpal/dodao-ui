# Wooden Furniture from Indonesia — Detailed Tariff & Cost Reduction Guide

> **For beginners:** Read [basic-concepts.md](./basic-concepts.md) first if you don't know words like HTS code, Section 232, FTZ, drawback, USMCA, etc. This guide assumes you understand those terms.

---

## 1. The Product

- **What it is:** Wooden bedroom and living-room furniture (non-upholstered) — beds, dressers, dining tables, chairs, bookcases.
- **Country of origin:** Indonesia.
- **Why Indonesia:** Indonesia is the world's **#3 furniture exporter** behind China and Vietnam, and the **#1 source of teak and rattan** furniture. The industry is concentrated in Jepara (Central Java) and Surabaya.

## 2. The HTS Code

Furniture HTS codes depend on:

1. **Material** (wood, metal, plastic, rattan, bamboo)
2. **Use** (bedroom, dining, office, kitchen, outdoor)
3. **Construction** (assembled, knock-down/flat-pack)
4. **Whether upholstered**

| HTS Code | Description | MFN Duty |
|---|---|---|
| **9403.50.9080** | Wooden bedroom furniture, other | **0%** |
| **9403.60.8093** | Wooden furniture, other (living/dining) | **0%** |
| 9403.40.9060 | Wooden kitchen cabinets/vanities | 0% |
| 9403.91.0080 | Parts for wooden furniture | 0% |
| 9401.61 | Upholstered wooden seating | 0% |
| **9403.81** | **Furniture of bamboo or rattan** | **0%** |
| 9403.20 | Other metal furniture | 0% |

**Key insight:** Furniture is **MFN duty-free**. Almost all the duty cost comes from **Section 232** (the new wood-furniture tariff) and **Section 122** (the temporary surcharge).

## 3. Current Tariff Stack (April 2026)

| Component | Rate | Why it applies |
|---|---|---|
| MFN | **0%** | Furniture is duty-free |
| Section 301 | 0% | Indonesia (not China) |
| **Section 232 wood furniture** | **25%** | All countries; in effect since late 2025; rate stays at 25% until at least Jan 1, 2027 |
| Section 122 | **10%** | All countries, expires ~July 24, 2026 |
| AD/CVD | None active for Indonesia furniture | Indonesia has AD/CVD on certain other goods (e.g., uncoated paper) but not wood furniture |
| **Total all-in duty** | **35%** | |

> **Section 232 details:** The 25% wood furniture tariff applies broadly to "wooden furniture, parts thereof" and was originally scheduled to rise to 30% on January 1, 2026. **Trump delayed the increase to January 1, 2027** in early 2026. So 25% is the rate through 2026.
>
> **Important exemption to watch:** The Section 232 proclamation allows exemptions for countries that reach a **national-security agreement**. As of April 2026, no country has been exempted yet, but Mexico (USMCA) and Vietnam are most likely candidates.

## 4. Landed Cost Calculation on $10,000 of Wooden Furniture

```
Product value (FOB):              $10,000.00
MFN duty (0%):                    $     0.00
Section 232 (25% — wood):         $ 2,500.00
Section 122 (10%):                $ 1,000.00
MPF (0.3464%, min $33.58):        $    34.64
HMF (0.125%, ocean only):         $    12.50
                                  -----------
Total LANDED COST:                $13,547.14
Effective tariff rate:            35.47%
```

(Indonesia → U.S. ocean shipping for furniture is bulky — a 20-foot container costs ~$3,000–$5,000. Per-piece freight on a $10,000 shipment can be ~5–10% of value, **not included** above.)

## 5. Detailed Ways to Reduce the Tariff Cost

### Option A: Switch to Bamboo / Rattan Furniture (BIGGEST SAVING)

**This is the most powerful single move you can make for Indonesian furniture.**

The Section 232 wood-furniture tariff covers **wooden** furniture. Bamboo and rattan furniture fall under **HTS 9403.81 / 9403.82** ("Furniture of cane, osier, bamboo, or similar materials"), which is **NOT on the Section 232 list**.

#### Math on $10,000 of furniture:

- Wooden furniture: 0% MFN + 25% Section 232 + 10% Section 122 = **35% duty = $3,500**
- Bamboo/rattan furniture: 0% MFN + 0% Section 232 + 10% Section 122 = **10% duty = $1,000**
- **Saving: $2,500.**

#### Practical reality:

- **Indonesia is the world capital of bamboo and rattan furniture.** Major producers: Bandung, Cirebon, Jepara.
- Designs are widely available — modern minimalist, mid-century, tropical, traditional.
- Common substitutions:
  - Wooden dining chair → rattan/bamboo dining chair
  - Wooden bed frame → bamboo bed frame with wood mattress slats
  - Wooden bookshelf → rattan-fronted bookshelf with wood frame
- For products that **MUST be solid wood** (e.g., farmhouse dining tables), this doesn't work — but for many bedroom and living-room categories, bamboo/rattan can be a genuine substitute.

#### Pitfalls to watch:

- A "bamboo-look" product made from real wood with bamboo veneer is still **wood furniture**.
- Mixed-material furniture: classification depends on the **"essential character"** rule. A frame of wood with rattan inserts may classify as wood. Genuine bamboo/rattan must dominate.
- Get a **CBP Binding Ruling** to confirm classification before mass production.

### Option B: Source from a USMCA Country (Mexico)

Mexico has wood-furniture manufacturing (Jalisco, Mexico City). Under USMCA, Mexico-origin furniture enters the U.S. at **0% duty** — and **may be exempt from the Section 232 wood tariff** under USMCA's national-security exemption framework.

#### Math on $10,000:

- Indonesia: $3,500 in duty.
- Mexico (if Section 232-exempt under USMCA): **$0 in duty.**
- **Saving: $3,500.**

#### Practical reality:

- Mexico's per-piece labor cost is **~3× higher than Indonesia** ($8–10/hour vs. $2–3/hour).
- Solid wood furniture from Mexico costs **~25–40% more per piece** than equivalent Indonesian furniture.
- BUT, you save 35% in tariff + ocean freight + 8 weeks of lead time.
- For a $300 retail piece of furniture:
  - Indonesia: $100 FOB + $5 freight + $35 duty = **$140 landed**
  - Mexico: $135 FOB + $2 trucking + $0 duty = **$137 landed**
- **Mexico can be cost-competitive** plus you get faster turnaround.

#### When Mexico wins:

- Higher-end furniture where labor cost is a smaller share.
- Products that benefit from short lead times (custom orders, replenishment).
- Brands selling primarily in the U.S./Canadian market.

#### When Mexico loses:

- Tropical hardwood furniture (teak, mahogany) — the wood doesn't grow there.
- Very low-priced furniture where labor cost dominates.

---

### Option C: Wait for a Section 232 Exemption Deal

The Section 232 proclamation says countries can negotiate exemptions. **Watch for Indonesia/Vietnam ART-style deals that include wood furniture exemption.**

#### What to do now:

- Subscribe to USTR press releases.
- Have your customs broker monitor USTR and CBP guidance weekly.
- Be ready to **shift orders to a country that gets exempted** — for example, if Vietnam negotiates an exemption (likely candidate due to large furniture industry), orders shift.

#### Risk:

- No guarantee any exemption happens during 2026.
- Even if it does, the exemption may have quantitative limits (TRQ-style).

### Option D: First Sale Rule

Indonesian furniture supply chains often go through trading houses:

```
Indonesian factory  →  Jakarta/Singapore trading agent  →  U.S. importer  →  U.S. retailer
   ($60)               ($90)                                ($130)            ($299)
```

Declaring duty on the **first sale ($60)** instead of the **last sale ($90)** saves a meaningful amount when the duty rate is 35%.

#### Math on $10,000 (first sale ~70% of import price):

- Last-sale duty: 35% × $10,000 = $3,500.
- First-sale duty: 35% × $7,000 = **$2,450**.
- **Saving: $1,050.**

#### Practical setup:

1. Get the **factory's invoice** to the trading agent.
2. Get the **agent's invoice** to you.
3. Confirm goods were destined for the U.S. from the start.
4. Use a customs broker familiar with first-sale claims.

#### Hurdles for furniture specifically:

- Many Indonesian manufacturers sell **directly** to U.S. importers (no agent in the middle), which means no first-sale opportunity.
- For those that go through agents, the savings are real and worth pursuing.

### Option E: Knock-Down (Flat-Pack) Furniture & Component Imports

Some furniture parts have **different classifications and (potentially) different Section 232 treatment** than finished furniture.

#### Example:

- Finished wooden bed frame (HTS 9403.50): 0% MFN + 25% Section 232.
- Furniture parts (HTS 9403.91): currently **also 0% MFN + 25% Section 232** under the proclamation (parts are included in the scope).

So just **shipping in flat-pack** doesn't help with Section 232. **However:**

- **Veneer panels and raw lumber components** (HTS chapters 44 — wood) may have different treatment.
- A bookcase imported as "wood panels + hardware" may classify under chapter 44 with different (sometimes lower) Section 232 rates.

#### Practical setup:

1. Import **wood panels, dowels, hardware** separately.
2. Assemble in the U.S. (using a domestic assembly partner).
3. Sell as "Made in USA" or "Assembled in USA" furniture.

#### When this works:

- Higher-end brands where U.S. assembly is part of the brand story.
- Products where flat-pack shipping has structural sense (bookshelves, desks, bed frames).
- Brands with U.S. assembly capacity (or willing to invest in one).

### Option F: Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ)

For furniture importers, FTZ is **uniquely valuable** because:

1. **Section 232 deferral.** You don't pay the 25% until furniture leaves the FTZ for U.S. sale.
2. **No duty if re-exported.** If you sell to Canada/Mexico/Caribbean, no U.S. tariff at all.
3. **Inverted tariff potential.** Components (wood panels, hardware) sometimes have different rates than finished furniture. Assemble in the FTZ, pay finished-furniture rate.

#### When FTZ pays off:

- Importing $1M+/year in furniture.
- Some inventory naturally sells to Canada/Mexico.
- Long inventory cycles (furniture sits 4–8 weeks before retail sale).

#### Top furniture FTZs:

- Long Beach / Los Angeles (CA)
- Houston (TX)
- Savannah (GA)
- Charleston (SC)

---

### Option G: Bonded Warehouse

Same logic as FTZ but simpler. Defer the 35% duty for up to 5 years. Useful for **seasonal furniture** (patio sets, holiday-themed decor) and for **inventory you may re-export**.

### Option H: Duty Drawback (and the Re-Export Reality Check)

#### **Re-Export Reality Check for Indonesian Wood Furniture:**

If you import to the U.S. and re-export to **Canada**:

**Option 1: Indonesia → U.S. → Canada (drawback)**
- Indonesia → U.S. ocean: $200 per furniture piece (it's bulky)
- U.S. duty: 35% × $400 FOB = $140
- U.S. → Canada trucking: $100/piece
- Canada import duty on Indonesian wood furniture: ~9.5% MFN (Canada has not adopted Section 232 wood tariffs) = $38
- Drawback refund: -$139 (99% of $140)
- Net cost: $339/piece

**Option 2: Indonesia → Canada directly**
- Indonesia → Canada ocean: $250/piece
- Canada duty: 9.5% × $400 = $38
- Net cost: $288/piece

**Direct shipping to Canada is ~$50/piece cheaper.** Canada's MFN tariff on Indonesian furniture is much lower than the U.S.'s, and double-shipping eats the drawback benefit.

#### For Mexico:

Mexico has a **Mexico-Indonesia partial trade agreement** and reasonable MFN rates. Direct shipping is cheaper.

#### For Australia / Japan:

Both have **comprehensive economic partnership agreements** with Indonesia (RCEP, IA-CEPA). Indonesian furniture enters at very low or 0% duty. **Direct shipping is dramatically cheaper.**

#### Conclusion:

For Indonesian furniture, drawback is mostly useful when:

- You're a U.S.-based furniture brand with U.S. as primary market.
- You handle returns or have B2B distribution to Canada.
- A small portion of inventory naturally crosses the border.

It is **not** a strategy to actively pursue.

### Option I: Product Substitution — Different Materials, Same Product

Beyond bamboo/rattan, consider:

- **Metal furniture** (HTS 9403.20): 0% + 10% Section 122 = 10% (no Section 232).
- **Plastic furniture** (HTS 9403.70): 0% + 10% Section 122 = 10%.
- **Glass-top tables with metal frame:** Can classify under HTS 9403.20 if metal is the chief material.

For modern/industrial design lines, metal furniture from Indonesia is widely available and **avoids the 25% Section 232**.

## 7. The Best Practical Option for Indonesian Wooden Furniture

### **Recommendation: Switch to Bamboo / Rattan Furniture from the Same Indonesian Suppliers**

**Why:**

- **$2,500 saving on $10,000** — far the biggest single saving available for Indonesian furniture.
- **Indonesia is already the global leader** in bamboo/rattan furniture, so you keep your supply chain.
- **No factory move, no FTZ setup, no complicated paperwork.**
- The product genuinely looks different but is often what consumers want anyway (bamboo and rattan are on-trend in 2026).

**Implementation:**

1. **Talk to your existing Indonesian suppliers.** Most wood furniture makers in Jepara also do rattan/bamboo.
2. **Redesign your line** — a bamboo dining chair instead of a wood one, a rattan headboard instead of wood.
3. **File a CBP Binding Ruling** to confirm that your specific product classifies under HTS 9403.81/82 (cane/bamboo/rattan), not HTS 9403.50/60 (wood).
4. **Document the material composition** — keep mill certificates and supplier letters proving genuine bamboo/rattan content.

### **Alternative recommendation if your line MUST be solid wood:**

**Combine: First Sale Rule + Watch for Section 232 exemption deals**

- First Sale Rule cuts the duty base ($1,050 saving).
- Monitor USTR for Vietnam/Indonesia/Mexico exemption deals.
- If Vietnam gets a Section 232 exemption first, switch sourcing to Vietnam.

### **When to skip these and just absorb the duty:**

- If your business is small and the redesign cost is too much.
- If your customers specifically want solid wood from a specific tropical hardwood (teak, mahogany) — you're stuck with the 35% for now.
