# U.S. Import Tariff Report — 5 Product Examples ($10,000 Each)

## Important Context (Read First — As of April 2026)

The U.S. tariff landscape changed dramatically in February 2026:

- **Feb 20, 2026:** The U.S. Supreme Court (in *Learning Resources v. Trump* and *Trump v. V.O.S. Selections*) ruled 6-3 that the President cannot use IEEPA to impose tariffs. This struck down the "reciprocal" tariffs from 2025 (e.g., 20% on Vietnam, 26% on India, 32% on Indonesia, etc.).
- **Feb 24, 2026:** All IEEPA tariffs terminated. A new **Section 122 surcharge of 10%** was applied to most imports as a temporary replacement (legally limited to 150 days, expiring around **July 24, 2026** unless Congress extends).
- **Still in effect:** Section 301 (China), Section 232 (wood/furniture, steel, aluminum, autos), and traditional antidumping/countervailing duties (AD/CVD).

**Standard fees that apply to almost every import (for $10,000 shipments):**
- **Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF):** 0.3464% of value (FY2026 min $33.58, max $651.50). On $10,000 = **$34.64**
- **Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF):** 0.125% of value (ocean freight only). On $10,000 = **$12.50**

Shipping cost is NOT included below unless flagged separately.

## 1. Toys from China

- **Product:** Plastic toys / general toys
- **Country:** China
- **Likely HTS code:** 9503.00.0073 (other toys, put up in sets) — MFN base 0%
- **Normal duty rate:** 0% (toys are duty-free under standard MFN)
- **Extra tariff or country-specific duty:**
  - Section 301 List 4A: **7.5%** (still in effect — not struck down by SCOTUS)
  - Section 122 surcharge: **10%** (until ~July 24, 2026)
  - **Combined extra: 17.5%**
- **Estimated cost on $10,000 import:**
  - Product value: $10,000.00
  - MFN duty (0%): $0.00
  - Section 301 (7.5%): $750.00
  - Section 122 (10%): $1,000.00
  - MPF: $34.64
  - HMF: $12.50
  - **Final landed cost: ≈ $11,797.14**
- **Simple explanation:** Toys are normally free to import. But because they come from China, a 7.5% extra duty (Section 301) was added years ago in the trade war, and that's still active. On top of that, every country pays a temporary 10% surcharge right now.
- **Ways to reduce tariff/import cost:**
  1. **Move sourcing to Vietnam, Mexico, or India** — same toys would skip the 7.5% Section 301. Mexico under USMCA could even drop the 10% Section 122 in many cases. Add ~$0.30–$0.80/unit in shipping vs. China, but you save 7.5% of product value.
  2. **Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ):** Bring toys into a U.S. FTZ. You only pay duty when goods leave the zone for U.S. sale. If you re-export some, you pay no U.S. duty on those. Good for importers who also export.
  3. **Bonded warehouse:** Similar idea but shorter term — defer duty up to 5 years. Helps cash flow.
  4. **First Sale Rule:** If your factory sells to a middleman who sells to you, you can often declare duty on the *first sale* (factory → middleman) price, which is lower. Saves duty on the markup.
  5. **Tariff engineering:** Re-design the product (e.g., add educational/instructional features) so it can correctly classify under a different HTS that may have a different Section 301 list assignment.
- **Best practical option:** **Move sourcing to Vietnam or India.** Toy manufacturing has already shifted there, and the savings (~$750 on $10,000) usually beat the extra freight. Foreign-Trade Zones are great for big importers, but for a $10,000 shipment they're overkill.
- **Sources used:** [USITC HTS](https://hts.usitc.gov/), [USTR Section 301](https://ustr.gov/issue-areas/enforcement/section-301-investigations), [Toys & Games Tariffs 2026](https://www.tariffstool.com/tariffs-on-toys-games)

## 2. Shoes / Sneakers from Vietnam

- **Product:** Athletic sneakers with textile upper and rubber sole
- **Country:** Vietnam
- **Likely HTS code:** 6404.11.9020 (athletic footwear, textile upper, rubber/plastic outer sole, valued >$12/pair)
- **Normal duty rate:** **20%** (one of the highest MFN rates in the entire HTS — footwear tariffs are notoriously high)
- **Extra tariff or country-specific duty:**
  - Reciprocal 20% tariff: **eliminated Feb 24, 2026** (struck down by Supreme Court)
  - Section 122 surcharge: **10%** (temporary, until ~July 24, 2026)
  - No AD/CVD on Vietnamese sneakers
- **Estimated cost on $10,000 import:**
  - Product value: $10,000.00
  - MFN duty (20%): $2,000.00
  - Section 122 (10%): $1,000.00
  - MPF: $34.64
  - HMF: $12.50
  - **Final landed cost: ≈ $13,047.14**
- **Simple explanation:** Sneakers have always carried a high 20% U.S. duty regardless of country. Vietnam used to face an extra 20% reciprocal hit, but that's gone. Right now you only pay the 20% MFN + the temporary 10% surcharge.
- **Ways to reduce tariff/import cost:**
  1. **Tariff engineering — change material composition.** Shoe duty depends heavily on upper material. Leather sneakers (6403.x) often pay 8.5–10%, while textile uppers pay 20%. Substituting more leather can cut duty by half.
  2. **Adjust the value above/below thresholds.** HTS 6404.11 has rate breakpoints (under $3, $3–$6.50, $6.50–$12, over $12 per pair). Pricing tiers and product mix can move you to a lower-duty bracket — fully legal so long as the declared FOB price is the actual transaction price.
  3. **First Sale Rule:** Big margin product like sneakers (factory $30 → brand $60 → importer $80) — declaring duty on the factory price saves a lot.
  4. **Foreign-Trade Zone:** Especially good if you re-export to Canada/Latin America afterward — no U.S. duty on those re-exports.
  5. **Duty drawback:** If you import sneakers and later export some (e.g., to Canada or as e-commerce returns), you can recover up to **99% of the duty paid**.
- **Best practical option:** **First Sale Rule.** Footwear typically has 2-3 layers of resale before reaching the U.S. importer, and the duty base (20%) is high — so reducing the dutiable value by 20–30% via first sale gives a real, repeatable saving. Tariff engineering helps too but requires product redesign.
- **Sources used:** [USITC HTS Chapter 64](https://hts.usitc.gov/), [White & Case — IEEPA Tariffs Terminated](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/united-states-terminates-ieepa-based-tariffs-following-supreme-court-decision), [Vietnam Tariff Rates](https://www.tariffcentral.org/tariffs/vietnam)

## 3. T-shirts / Basic Cotton Clothes from Bangladesh

- **Product:** Men's cotton knit T-shirts
- **Country:** Bangladesh
- **Likely HTS code:** 6109.10.0012 (T-shirts, singlets — cotton, knitted, men's)
- **Normal duty rate:** **16.5%** (cotton knit T-shirts is one of the higher apparel rates)
- **Extra tariff or country-specific duty:**
  - Reciprocal 35% (Bangladesh's old rate): struck down Feb 2026
  - Section 122 surcharge: **10%**
  - U.S.-Bangladesh ART (Feb 2026) created a **zero-tariff TRQ (tariff-rate quota)** for limited apparel volumes made with U.S.-origin cotton/man-made fibers
  - No GSP (the GSP program has been expired since end of 2020 and Bangladesh wasn't fully eligible for apparel anyway)
- **Estimated cost on $10,000 import:**
  - Product value: $10,000.00
  - MFN duty (16.5%): $1,650.00
  - Section 122 (10%): $1,000.00
  - MPF: $34.64
  - HMF: $12.50
  - **Final landed cost: ≈ $12,697.14**
- **Simple explanation:** Apparel has high U.S. duties everywhere — 16.5% on cotton T-shirts even before any extra tariffs. Bangladesh's old 35% extra reciprocal tariff is gone, but the temporary 10% surcharge still applies. There's a special new zero-duty deal but only for shirts made from American cotton.
- **Ways to reduce tariff/import cost:**
  1. **U.S.-Bangladesh ART zero-tariff TRQ.** If your T-shirts are made with U.S.-origin cotton yarn/fabric and meet the certification + TRQ allocation, your reciprocal portion drops to **0%**. You still pay the 16.5% MFN duty though, so this isn't a full bypass — but it removes the 10% surcharge.
  2. **First Sale Rule.** Apparel goes through agents/buyers/brands — declaring duty on the factory's first-sale price typically saves 15–25% of dutiable base.
  3. **Tariff engineering — fabric blend.** A T-shirt that's >50% synthetic (e.g., 55/45 polyester/cotton) classifies under 6109.90, which has a different (often higher) rate, BUT specific knit constructions or "tank tops" (other HTS) can have lower rates. Verify with a CBP binding ruling first.
  4. **Foreign-Trade Zone:** Useful if you also export some shirts; you don't pay duty on shirts that leave the U.S. again.
  5. **Move to a different country with lower combined rate.** Right now India (10% Section 122 only on top of MFN) and many smaller countries can be cheaper than Bangladesh once you factor everything. But Bangladesh has the lowest production cost per shirt — usually the per-unit savings stays with Bangladesh even with duties.
- **Best practical option:** **Use the U.S. cotton TRQ under the new Bangladesh agreement** if you can make the supply chain work. For brands ordering at scale, buying U.S. cotton + certifying origin is genuinely viable. For smaller importers without that infrastructure, **First Sale Rule** is the most practical lever.
- **Sources used:** [White House — US-Bangladesh Joint Statement](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2026/02/joint-statement-on-framework-for-united-states-bangladesh-agreement-on-reciprocal-trade/), [USITC HTS 6109](https://hts.usitc.gov/), [Just-Style coverage](https://www.just-style.com/news/us-bangladesh-trade-deal/)

## 4. Bedsheets / Towels from India

- **Product:** Cotton terry bath towels (and cotton bedsheets)
- **Country:** India
- **Likely HTS codes:**
  - Towels: **6302.60.0020** (toilet/kitchen linen, terry, cotton)
  - Bedsheets: **6302.31.9020** (cotton bed linen, not knitted/crocheted, not printed)
- **Normal duty rate:**
  - Towels (6302.60.0020): **9.1%**
  - Bedsheets (6302.31): **6.7%**
- **Extra tariff or country-specific duty:**
  - Reciprocal 18% rate on India: struck down Feb 2026
  - Section 122 surcharge: **10%**
  - No active U.S. AD/CVD order on cotton bed linen or terry towels from India (the well-known antidumping case was the EU's, not the U.S.). India *does* have AD/CVD on other categories (PET film, polyester staple fiber, etc.), so verify your exact product.
- **Estimated cost on $10,000 import (using towels at 9.1%):**
  - Product value: $10,000.00
  - MFN duty (9.1%): $910.00
  - Section 122 (10%): $1,000.00
  - MPF: $34.64
  - HMF: $12.50
  - **Final landed cost: ≈ $11,957.14**
- **Simple explanation:** India was hit hard with reciprocal tariffs in 2025 (18%+), but those are now gone. You only pay the normal cotton-linen duty (~7–9%) plus the temporary 10% surcharge. There's no antidumping duty on basic cotton sheets/towels from India today.
- **Ways to reduce tariff/import cost:**
  1. **Wait it out / time your imports.** The Section 122 10% surcharge expires ~July 24, 2026. If you can delay shipments via a bonded warehouse until after that date, you save 10% outright. This is the single biggest practical lever right now.
  2. **First Sale Rule.** Indian textile exports often go through trading houses → U.S. buyers. Declaring duty on the first (factory) sale shaves 10–20% off the dutiable base.
  3. **Bonded warehouse / FTZ.** Defer duty for up to 5 years; pay only when goods enter U.S. commerce. Pairs perfectly with strategy #1.
  4. **Verify HTS classification carefully.** Bedsheets (6.7%) vs. "other cotton furnishing" (different rates) vs. printed vs. plain — small product differences move you across rate lines. Get a binding ruling from CBP if unsure.
  5. **Duty drawback.** If you re-export any portion (e.g., to Canada via your e-commerce business), recover 99% of duties paid on the exported portion.
- **Best practical option:** **Time imports + use a bonded warehouse to clear after July 24, 2026** if your inventory plans allow. That's a clean ~$1,000 savings on a $10,000 shipment with no legal complexity. After that date, **First Sale Rule** becomes the steady-state lever.
- **Sources used:** [USITC HTS Chapter 63](https://hts.usitc.gov/), [White House India Joint Statement](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2026/02/united-states-india-joint-statement/), [CBP Ruling N234008](https://rulings.cbp.gov/ruling/N234008), [Trade.gov AD/CVD home](https://www.trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties)

## 5. Wooden Furniture from Indonesia

- **Product:** Wooden bedroom/living-room furniture (non-upholstered)
- **Country:** Indonesia
- **Likely HTS code:** 9403.60.8093 (other wooden furniture) or 9403.50 (wooden bedroom furniture)
- **Normal duty rate:** **0%** (wooden furniture is normally MFN duty-free!)
- **Extra tariff or country-specific duty:**
  - Section 232 wood-furniture tariff: **25%** (in effect; the planned increase to 30% on Jan 1, 2026 was delayed by Trump until Jan 1, 2027)
  - Section 122 surcharge: **10%**
  - Indonesia's old 19% ART reciprocal: legal status uncertain after SCOTUS ruling; for the calculation below I use the 10% Section 122 (the ART agreement was IEEPA-based and was struck down with the rest)
- **Estimated cost on $10,000 import:**
  - Product value: $10,000.00
  - MFN duty (0%): $0.00
  - Section 232 wood furniture (25%): $2,500.00
  - Section 122 (10%): $1,000.00
  - MPF: $34.64
  - HMF: $12.50
  - **Final landed cost: ≈ $13,547.14**
- **Simple explanation:** Wooden furniture used to be duty-free. But in late 2025 the U.S. added a big 25% Section 232 "national security" tariff on wood and furniture imports — that hits everyone, not just Indonesia. So even though Indonesia's reciprocal deal is gone, this 25% wood-specific tariff is the main cost driver. Plus the temporary 10% Section 122 surcharge.
- **Ways to reduce tariff/import cost:**
  1. **Switch to materials not covered by Section 232.** Furniture made primarily of metal, rattan, bamboo, or composite (non-wood) often falls outside the wood-furniture Section 232 list. Bamboo/rattan furniture from Indonesia/Vietnam can avoid the 25% entirely. This is real "tariff engineering."
  2. **Source from a Section 232 exempt country.** The proclamation allows exemptions for countries that reach a deal addressing "national security concerns." Watch for Vietnam, Mexico, Canada (USMCA), or the EU getting carve-outs. Mexico is the strongest near-term candidate — wood furniture from Mexico under USMCA could be 0% all-in.
  3. **First Sale Rule.** Indonesian factory → trading house → U.S. importer is a common chain. Declaring on the factory price drops the dutiable base meaningfully because Section 232 of 25% is high.
  4. **Foreign-Trade Zone.** Big furniture importers stage inventory in FTZs and pay duty as needed. Cash-flow benefit + zero duty on re-exports.
  5. **Duty drawback on re-exports.** If you sell to Canada/Mexico after import, recover 99% of the Section 232 + Section 122 duties.
  6. **Knock-down (KD) shipping + tariff engineering.** Some furniture parts (e.g., raw wood components) classify differently than finished furniture and may face different duty treatment. Combined with FTZ assembly, this can lower duty.
- **Best practical option:** **Switch to bamboo/rattan or source from Mexico/Vietnam** — on a $10,000 furniture order, the 25% Section 232 saving alone is **$2,500**, far bigger than freight differences. If you must keep Indonesian wood furniture, **First Sale Rule** + monitoring for Section 232 exemptions is the next-best lever.
- **Sources used:** [Section 232 Wood Furniture — RVIA](https://www.rvia.org/news-insights/section-232-tariffs-wood-products-announced), [Home Furnishings Association](https://myhfa.org/blog/new-section-232-tariff-changes-could-impact-furniture-import-costs/), [USITC HTS Chapter 94](https://hts.usitc.gov/), [White House Section 232 Proclamation](https://www.whitehouse.gov/)

## General Cross-Cutting Tips (Apply to All 5)

1. **First Sale Rule** is the single most underused legal lever for any importer with a multi-tier supply chain. It often saves 15–25% of duty base across all five categories.
2. **The 10% Section 122 surcharge expires ~July 24, 2026** — if you can defer customs entry until after that date (via bonded warehouse or FTZ), you save 10% of $10,000 = $1,000 per shipment, on every product type above.
3. **Get binding rulings (CBP Form 19 CFR §177)** before locking in a tariff engineering strategy — they're free and protect you legally.
4. **For each product, the duty engine differs**: Section 301 (China only), Section 232 (wood furniture from everywhere), AD/CVD (specific products from specific countries), and Section 122 (everywhere, temporary).
