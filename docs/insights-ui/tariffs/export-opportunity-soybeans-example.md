# Example Report — Export Market Opportunity: U.S. Soybeans

> This is an **example/illustrative report** for the proposed "Export market opportunity comparison" feature in [`optional_tariff_features.md`](./optional_tariff_features.md).
> Numbers are an April 2026 snapshot for example purposes. Always confirm rates with a licensed customs broker and trade attorney before signing real export contracts.
> For tariff term definitions, see [`../tariffs/basic-concepts.md`](../tariffs/basic-concepts.md).

---

## 1. Why Soybeans?

- **One of the largest U.S. exports.** The U.S. is the world's #2 soybean exporter (after Brazil) and the #1 producer some years; soybeans are typically a top-3 U.S. agricultural export by value.
- **Almost all U.S. soybean farms are export-dependent.** About **half of the U.S. soybean crop is exported.** Farmers' income depends directly on which destination markets are open.
- **Many real destination markets.** China, Mexico, the EU, Japan, Indonesia, Egypt, Vietnam, Taiwan, and others — making it a clean example for "which market is best to export to?"
- **Easy for any user to understand.** Bushels, dollars per bushel, and tonnes per shipload — all standard, familiar units.

## 2. The Product and HTS Code

| HTS Code (Schedule B export) | Description |
|---|---|
| **1201.90.0095** | Soybeans, other than seed |
| 1201.10.0000 | Soybeans, seed |
| 1208.10.0000 | Soybean flour and meal (processed) |
| 2304.00.0000 | Soybean oil-cake / meal (after oil extraction) |

**Key point:** The U.S. side has no export tariff. The cost difference across destinations comes from **the buyer country's import tariff, freight cost, demand strength, and non-tariff barriers** (e.g., GMO rules in the EU).

## 6. Net Margin Comparison — Worked Example

Selling **1 metric tonne** (about 36.7 bushels) of U.S. #2 yellow soybeans, FOB Gulf, indicative April 2026 numbers.

```
Cost basis:
  U.S. farm-gate + elevator + barge to Gulf: ~$380/tonne
  Export elevator + FOB loading:             ~$15/tonne
                                              -------
  FOB U.S. Gulf cost:                         ~$395/tonne

China (with 3% MFN + assumed 0% retaliatory active):
  Buyer pays at landed:                        $470/tonne
  Less: ocean freight (Gulf -> China):         $ 55
  Less: 3% Chinese MFN duty:                   $ 14
  Less: insurance + handling:                  $  6
                                                ----
  Net to U.S. exporter:                        $395/tonne (breakeven)
  Margin per tonne:                            ~$0   (in this scenario)

Mexico (USMCA, 0% duty):
  Buyer pays at landed:                        $475/tonne
  Less: rail freight (Gulf/Midwest -> MX):     $ 35
  Less: 0% duty:                               $  0
  Less: handling:                              $  4
                                                ----
  Net to U.S. exporter:                        $436/tonne
  Margin per tonne:                            ~$41

Japan (food-grade, IP non-GMO):
  Buyer pays at landed:                        $560/tonne
  Less: ocean freight (Gulf -> Japan):         $ 65
  Less: 0% MFN duty:                           $  0
  Less: insurance + IP handling:               $ 10
                                                ----
  Net to U.S. exporter (food-grade premium):   $485/tonne
  Margin per tonne:                            ~$90

EU (Netherlands):
  Buyer pays at landed:                        $490/tonne
  Less: ocean freight (Gulf -> Rotterdam):     $ 45
  Less: 0% MFN duty:                           $  0
  Less: insurance + handling:                  $  6
                                                ----
  Net to U.S. exporter:                        $439/tonne
  Margin per tonne:                            ~$44
```

## 7. What This Tells the Exporter

- **China is the largest buyer but the lowest margin.** The U.S. is a price-taker into China, and any retaliatory tariff swing wipes out the margin. Volume is huge but profit per tonne is the worst.
- **Mexico is the most reliable medium-margin market.** USMCA gives the U.S. a structural advantage Brazil and Argentina cannot match for the foreseeable future. Freight is also short and stable.
- **Japan and Taiwan pay the best per tonne for food-grade.** Identity-preserved non-GMO soybeans for tofu and miso get premium prices. Volume is smaller, paperwork is more.
- **The EU is steady but Brazil is the price-setter.** U.S. soybeans into the EU are competitive only when Brazilian supply is short or when buyers want non-GMO/identity-preserved.
- **Indonesia, Vietnam, and Bangladesh are growing animal-feed markets.** Margin is thin but volume is rising.

## 8. Decision Matrix — Who Should Export to Where

| Exporter type | Best fit market(s) |
|---|---|
| Large grain co-op (volume-first) | China + Mexico |
| Mid-size shipper (margin-first) | Mexico + EU |
| Identity-preserved / non-GMO grower | Japan, Taiwan, EU |
| Organic soybean grower | Japan, EU |
| Risk-averse exporter (avoid LC trouble) | Mexico, EU, Japan |
| Growth-market chaser | Vietnam, Indonesia, Bangladesh |

## 9. Caveats and Risks (Be Honest)

- **Soybean prices swing 20–40% in a year.** Weather in Brazil/Argentina, currency shifts, and trade-war headlines move prices fast.
- **China retaliatory tariffs are political, not economic.** A China tariff on U.S. soybeans can appear or disappear in days; this is the #1 risk to the largest market.
- **Brazil is structurally cheaper** thanks to lower farm cost and a closer south-Atlantic route to China. The U.S. cannot compete on raw price; the U.S. competes on reliability, traceability, and non-GMO/identity-preserved quality.
- **Non-tariff barriers can be larger than tariffs.** GMO rules in the EU, halal in Indonesia, food-grade specs in Japan. None of these are in the table above.
- **Currency and payment risk** in Egypt, Pakistan, and Bangladesh can erase the margin even when tariffs look fine.
- **Freight is the single biggest variable cost** after farm-gate price. A Panama Canal drought or a Red Sea problem can change the ranking.
