# Basic Concepts — A Plain-English Glossary for U.S. Import Tariffs

This file explains every important word and idea you'll see in the tariff reports. If you are new to importing, **read this file first**. Each product report (toys, sneakers, T-shirts, bedsheets, furniture) will use these words and you can come back here whenever you get stuck.

For the **how-to detail** on tariff reduction methods (First Sale Rule, FTZ, Bonded Warehouse, Drawback, Tariff Engineering, Country Switching), see [common-strategies.md](./common-strategies.md).

---

## 1. HTS Code (Harmonized Tariff Schedule code)

Think of the HTS code as a **product's ID number at the U.S. border**. Every single product in the world has its own number — a cotton T-shirt has one number, a wool T-shirt has a different number, a baby's onesie has yet another.

- The number is **10 digits** long (example: `6109.10.0012` for a men's cotton T-shirt).
- The first 6 digits are international (used by every country in the world).
- The last 4 digits are U.S.-specific.
- The U.S. customs uses this number to know **exactly what duty (tax) to charge**.

**Where to look up an HTS code:** The official tool is at [hts.usitc.gov](https://hts.usitc.gov/). You type a product name, and it shows the matching codes.

**Why it matters:** A wrong HTS code = wrong duty paid = either fines (if too low) or wasted money (if too high). Picking the right one is the first job of every importer.

---

## 2. MFN Duty (Most Favored Nation duty)

This is the **normal, baseline import tax** the U.S. charges on a product. It applies to almost every country in the world that the U.S. has normal trade relations with.

- "Most Favored Nation" sounds like special treatment, but it actually means **normal treatment**. (The name is a leftover from old trade treaty language.)
- Examples: Cotton T-shirts have an MFN rate of 16.5%. Wooden furniture has an MFN rate of 0%.
- This rate is in **Column 1 General** of the HTS Schedule.

**Simple way to remember:** MFN = "the regular tax everyone pays." Extra tariffs (Section 301, Section 232, etc.) are stacked **on top of** the MFN rate.

---

## 3. Section 301 Tariffs

A special law (Section 301 of the **Trade Act of 1974**) that lets the U.S. punish a country for "unfair trade practices."

- The U.S. used Section 301 in **2018–2019** against China for things like forced technology transfer and intellectual property theft.
- Result: extra tariffs (7.5%, 25%, etc.) on thousands of Chinese products. These are organized into **lists** (List 1, List 2, List 3, List 4A).
- Example: Toys are on **List 4A**, which has a **7.5% extra tariff**.
- These tariffs are **still active in 2026** — the Supreme Court did NOT strike them down (because they are based on a different law than IEEPA).

**When does Section 301 apply to you?** Only when you import from **China** (and a few other countries with Section 301 actions).

---

## 4. Section 232 Tariffs

Another special law (Section 232 of the **Trade Expansion Act of 1962**) that lets the U.S. impose tariffs for **"national security"** reasons.

- It does NOT target one country — it targets a **product category** from every country.
- Famous examples: 25% on imported steel, 25% on aluminum, 25% on autos, and as of late 2025, **25% on wood furniture**.
- The idea is "we don't want to depend on imports for things our military or economy needs."

**When does Section 232 apply to you?** When the product itself is on the list — regardless of which country it comes from.

---

## 5. Section 122 Tariffs

A less-famous law (Section 122 of the **Trade Act of 1974**) that lets the U.S. add a **temporary surcharge** on imports if there is a **balance of payments problem** (the country is buying way more than it's selling).

- Section 122 has strict limits:
  - Maximum **15%** rate.
  - Maximum **150 days** without Congress's approval.
- In **February 2026**, the U.S. used Section 122 to put a **10% surcharge on most imports** as a temporary replacement for the IEEPA reciprocal tariffs that the Supreme Court had just struck down.
- It is set to expire around **July 24, 2026** unless Congress passes a new law.

**Simple way to remember:** Section 122 = "the temporary 10% extra tax everyone pays right now."

---

## 6. IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act)

A law from **1977** that lets the President take economic actions during a **national emergency**. Originally meant for things like:
- Freezing the assets of an enemy country.
- Stopping trade with sanctioned regimes.

In **April 2025**, President Trump used IEEPA to declare an emergency over the U.S. trade deficit and impose **"reciprocal tariffs"** on almost every country. (See "Reciprocal Tariffs" below.)

- On **February 20, 2026**, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled **6–3** that IEEPA does **NOT** give the President the power to set tariffs.
- All IEEPA-based tariffs **ended on February 24, 2026**.

**Simple way to remember:** IEEPA = "emergency law misused for tariffs in 2025, struck down by the Supreme Court in 2026."

---

## 7. Reciprocal Tariffs

The word "reciprocal" means "matching" or "equal in return." A reciprocal tariff is when the U.S. says:

> "If your country charges us 25% on our goods, we will charge you 25% on your goods. We are matching whatever you do to us."

- Started in **April 2025** under Trump's "Liberation Day" executive order, using IEEPA.
- Different rates per country: e.g., China 34%, Vietnam 46%, Bangladesh 35%, India 26%, Indonesia 32%, baseline 10% for everyone else.
- Stacked **on top of** the normal MFN duty.
- Some countries later signed "ART" deals (Agreement on Reciprocal Trade) to get lower rates. Examples: Vietnam → 20%, Indonesia → 19%, India → 18%.
- **All ended February 24, 2026** when the Supreme Court struck them down.
- Replaced by the **temporary 10% Section 122 surcharge** (which is set to expire July 24, 2026).

**Simple way to remember:** Reciprocal tariffs were 2025's "punish-everyone" tariffs, killed by the courts in early 2026.

---

## 8. AD/CVD — Antidumping and Countervailing Duties

These are **product-specific extra tariffs** for unfair foreign pricing or subsidies. They are imposed by the U.S. Department of Commerce **only after a long investigation**.

- **Antidumping (AD):** When a foreign company sells in the U.S. **below their own home market price** ("dumping" cheap goods to kill U.S. competitors).
- **Countervailing (CVD):** When a foreign company gets unfair **government subsidies** that let them sell cheaper.
- AD/CVD rates can be **HUGE** (sometimes 100%+).
- They apply only to **specific product + specific country + sometimes specific company** combinations.

**Where to check if your product has AD/CVD:** [Trade.gov AD/CVD search](https://www.trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties)

**Simple way to remember:** AD/CVD = "extra punishment tariffs for specific products from specific countries that played unfair."

## 9. Tariff Reduction Strategies (covered separately)

The main legal methods to reduce U.S. import duty are explained in detail in [common-strategies.md](./common-strategies.md). One-line summary of each:

- **First Sale Rule:** Pay duty on the factory's selling price (not the middleman's price). Saves 15–30% of duty when the supply chain has middlemen.
- **Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ):** A U.S. site that legally counts as "outside" U.S. customs. Defer duty until goods leave for U.S. sale; no duty on re-exports.
- **Bonded Warehouse:** Like an FTZ but simpler — 5-year duty deferral with no manufacturing rights.
- **Duty Drawback:** Get back 99% of duty if you re-export (or export goods made from imported parts).
- **Tariff Engineering:** Legally redesign a product so it fits a lower-duty HTS code (Converse felt-sole sneakers, Columbia zip-out coats).
- **Country Switching:** Move production to a country with a free trade agreement (USMCA, CAFTA-DR) or no Section 301.

Open [common-strategies.md](./common-strategies.md) for when each works, the savings math, setup cost, and common mistakes.

## 10. USMCA (formerly NAFTA)

The **United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement**, in effect since 2020. It replaced NAFTA.

- Most goods that **originate in** Mexico, Canada, or the U.S. cross between these countries with **0% duty**.
- "Originate" has a specific meaning — the goods must be made (or sufficiently transformed) in one of the three countries. Just routing them through Mexico isn't enough.
- USMCA also survived the SCOTUS IEEPA ruling — it's a separate legal structure.

**Why it matters for tariff reduction:** Sourcing from Mexico (instead of China/Vietnam/India/Indonesia) can mean **0% duty** for many product categories. This is huge.

---

## 11. TRQ (Tariff-Rate Quota)

A **two-tier tariff** system. A **limited quantity** of a product can enter at a low (or zero) tariff. Above that quantity, a much higher tariff kicks in.

- Example: The new U.S.–Bangladesh agreement (Feb 2026) allows a certain volume of cotton T-shirts to enter at **0%** if made with U.S. cotton — but only up to a quota set by the U.S. government.
- Sugar, dairy, beef, peanuts all have famous TRQs.

**Simple way to remember:** TRQ = "the first X units come in cheap, the rest pay full price."

---

## 12. MPF (Merchandise Processing Fee)

A small fee that **U.S. Customs charges on every formal entry** to cover the cost of processing your paperwork.

- **Rate:** 0.3464% of the goods' value.
- **FY2026 minimum:** $33.58
- **FY2026 maximum:** $651.50
- For a $10,000 shipment: 0.3464% × $10,000 = $34.64 (just above the minimum).

This is **not** a tariff — it's a fee. It applies even when the duty rate is 0%.

---

## 13. HMF (Harbor Maintenance Fee)

A fee that pays for maintaining U.S. **seaports** (dredging channels, etc.).

- **Rate:** 0.125% of the goods' value.
- **Only on ocean shipments** — not air, not truck, not rail.
- For a $10,000 ocean shipment: 0.125% × $10,000 = $12.50.

If you ship by **air**, you skip HMF entirely (but air freight costs much more, so it's rarely worth it just to avoid HMF).

---

## 14. CBP (U.S. Customs and Border Protection)

The **government agency** that enforces customs rules at the border. They:

- Collect duties and fees.
- Inspect cargo.
- Issue binding rulings on classification.
- Process drawback claims.
- Run FTZs and bonded warehouses.

Website: [cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/)

---

## 15. USTR (United States Trade Representative)

The **government office** that negotiates trade agreements and decides Section 301 actions.

- Headed by a Cabinet-level official (the U.S. Trade Representative).
- Handles things like the U.S.–Bangladesh ART, the U.S.–Indonesia ART, WTO disputes, etc.

Website: [ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/)

---

## 16. USITC (U.S. International Trade Commission)

An **independent government agency** that:

- Maintains the official **Harmonized Tariff Schedule** (the master list of all HTS codes).
- Investigates AD/CVD cases.
- Provides trade research to Congress.

Website: [usitc.gov](https://www.usitc.gov/)

---

## 17. FOB / CIF / Landed Cost

These are **shipping price terms** (called "Incoterms").

- **FOB (Free On Board):** The price up to and including loading the goods on the ship at the foreign port. The U.S. importer pays for ocean freight after that.
- **CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight):** The price including ocean freight and insurance to the U.S. port. The U.S. importer pays nothing more for shipping.
- **Landed cost:** The total cost of the goods after they have arrived in the U.S. and cleared customs. Includes product price + duty + fees + freight + insurance + clearance.

**Why it matters:** U.S. customs typically values goods at the **transaction value (FOB price + freight if specified)**. Some countries use CIF as the duty base; the U.S. uses FOB-style transaction value.

---

## 18. CBP Binding Ruling

A **free, written decision from CBP** that tells you the correct HTS code for your specific product.

- You submit a request describing your product (with samples or photos).
- CBP responds in ~30 days with a legally binding classification.
- This protects you: if CBP later disagrees, they can't fine you for following their own ruling.

How to file: Submit through the [CBP CROSS Rulings system](https://rulings.cbp.gov/).

**Simple way to remember:** Binding ruling = "free legal protection for your HTS code choice."

---

## 19. ART (Agreement on Reciprocal Trade)

The **bilateral trade deals** the U.S. signed in 2025–2026 to reduce reciprocal tariffs in exchange for trade concessions.

- U.S.–Vietnam ART (July 2025): Vietnam reciprocal dropped from 46% → 20%.
- U.S.–Bangladesh ART (Feb 2026): Created a 0% TRQ for textile goods made with U.S. cotton.
- U.S.–Indonesia ART (Feb 2026): Indonesia reciprocal dropped from 32% → 19%, with some commodity exemptions.
- U.S.–India "Interim Agreement" (similar concept): rate dropped from 26% → 18%.

**Important:** Most ART rates were **based on IEEPA**, so when the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA in February 2026, the ART reciprocal rates were also struck down. The agreements themselves still exist as frameworks but the tariff numbers no longer apply directly. Only the temporary 10% Section 122 surcharge applies right now.

---

## 20. GSP (Generalized System of Preferences)

A program where the U.S. lets goods from **developing countries** enter at **0% duty** to support their economies.

- The U.S. GSP program **expired at the end of 2020** and has not been renewed by Congress.
- Even when active, GSP usually **excluded apparel and footwear** (the most labor-intensive goods that developing countries actually export).
- So GSP is mostly **not relevant** for the products in this report (toys, sneakers, T-shirts, sheets, furniture).

Other countries (EU, Canada, UK, Australia, Japan) still have active GSP programs that benefit Bangladesh, India, Indonesia, etc. This matters when you think about **direct shipping to those countries instead of through the U.S.**

---

## 21. Country of Origin

The **country where the product was made** — not where it shipped from.

- A shirt made in Bangladesh, sent to Singapore for packaging, then shipped to the U.S. has origin **Bangladesh** (not Singapore).
- "Substantial transformation" rules decide origin: the product must be **substantially changed** in a country to count as that country's origin.
- Just repackaging, relabeling, or transshipping does NOT change origin.

**Why it matters:** All tariffs (Section 301, Section 122, AD/CVD, etc.) are based on country of origin. Lying about origin is **customs fraud** with serious penalties.

---

## 22. Transshipment (legal vs. illegal)

**Transshipment** means goods pass through one country on their way to another.

- **Legal transshipment:** Vietnamese sneakers pass through Singapore's port on the way to the U.S. Origin is still Vietnam. No problem.
- **Illegal transshipment:** Chinese sneakers are shipped to Vietnam, repackaged with "Made in Vietnam" labels, and then shipped to the U.S. to avoid Section 301. **This is fraud and a federal crime.**

**Simple rule:** If the product was actually made somewhere, that's its origin. Repackaging or relabeling never changes that.

---

## 23. Re-Export Reality (covered separately)

Many tariff guides say "re-export to avoid U.S. duties." Reality is more complicated — the destination country has its own duty, and you pay two legs of freight and two customs clearances. Direct shipping from source country to destination country is almost always cheaper.

For the full framework and per-product worked examples, see [common-strategies.md § 7](./common-strategies.md). Each product report also has a "Re-Export Reality Check" section.

## 25. Where to Check Real Numbers (Official Sources)

If you are about to import for real, verify with these official sites:

- **HTS code + duty rate:** [hts.usitc.gov](https://hts.usitc.gov/)
- **CBP rulings (search past decisions):** [rulings.cbp.gov](https://rulings.cbp.gov/)
- **AD/CVD orders search:** [trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties](https://www.trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties)
- **USTR trade actions and Section 301:** [ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/)
- **CBP general guidance:** [cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/)
- **Federal Register (latest tariff proclamations):** [federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/)

For your real shipment, **always work with a licensed customs broker**. They cost a small fee per shipment but save you from expensive mistakes.
