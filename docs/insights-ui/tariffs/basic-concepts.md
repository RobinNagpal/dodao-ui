# Basic Concepts — A Plain-English Glossary for U.S. Import Tariffs

A short reference for the words and ideas that come up across the tariff reports. For the how-to detail on tariff reduction methods (First Sale, FTZ, Bonded Warehouse, Drawback, Tariff Engineering, Country Switching), see [common-strategies.md](./common-strategies.md).

---

## 1. HTS Code (Harmonized Tariff Schedule code)

A product's 10-digit ID at the U.S. border. First 6 digits are international; last 4 are U.S.-specific. CBP uses this number to set the duty rate. Look up at [hts.usitc.gov](https://hts.usitc.gov/). Wrong code = wrong duty = fines or wasted money.

## 2. MFN Duty (Most Favored Nation duty)

The normal baseline import tax — applied to almost every country with normal trade relations. Listed in Column 1 General of the HTS. Extra tariffs (Section 301, 232, 122) stack on top.

## 3. Section 301 Tariffs

Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 lets the U.S. punish "unfair trade practices." Used in 2018–2019 against China (Lists 1–4A, rates 7.5–25%). Still active in 2026 — based on a different law than IEEPA. Applies only when importing from China.

## 4. Section 232 Tariffs

Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 imposes tariffs for "national security." Targets a product category from every country, not one country. Examples: 25% on steel, aluminum, autos, and (late 2025) wood furniture.

## 5. Section 122 Tariffs

Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 lets the U.S. add a temporary surcharge for balance-of-payments problems. Capped at 15% and 150 days without Congress. In Feb 2026 a 10% surcharge replaced the struck-down IEEPA reciprocal tariffs; set to expire ~July 24, 2026 unless extended.

## 6. IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act)

A 1977 emergency law. In April 2025 it was used to impose "reciprocal tariffs" on most countries. On Feb 20, 2026 the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs; all IEEPA tariffs ended Feb 24, 2026.

## 7. Reciprocal Tariffs

The 2025 "Liberation Day" tariffs — country-specific rates on top of MFN (China 34%, Vietnam 46%, Bangladesh 35%, India 26%, Indonesia 32%, 10% baseline). Some lowered later via ART deals. All struck down Feb 24, 2026; replaced by the Section 122 10% surcharge.

## 8. AD/CVD — Antidumping and Countervailing Duties

Product-specific extra tariffs imposed by the Department of Commerce after a long investigation. **AD** punishes dumping (selling below home-market price); **CVD** punishes unfair government subsidies. Rates can be 100%+. Apply to specific product + country (sometimes specific company). Check at [trade.gov AD/CVD search](https://www.trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties).

## 9. Tariff Reduction Strategies (covered separately)

One-line summaries — see [common-strategies.md](./common-strategies.md) for full detail:

- **First Sale Rule:** Pay duty on the factory price, not the middleman price.
- **Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ):** A U.S. site that legally counts as outside customs. Defer duty until U.S. sale; no duty on re-exports.
- **Bonded Warehouse:** Simpler FTZ-cousin — 5-year duty deferral, no manufacturing.
- **Duty Drawback:** Recover up to 99% of duty on goods later exported.
- **Tariff Engineering:** Legally redesign a product into a lower-duty HTS code.
- **Country Switching:** Move production to a country with an FTA or no Section 301.

## 10. USMCA (formerly NAFTA)

The U.S.–Mexico–Canada Agreement (in effect since 2020). Most goods that **originate** in the three countries cross at 0% duty. Just routing through Mexico isn't enough — the goods must be made or substantially transformed there. USMCA was untouched by the 2026 SCOTUS IEEPA ruling.

## 11. TRQ (Tariff-Rate Quota)

Two-tier tariff: a limited quantity at low (or 0%) duty, higher rate above the quota. Example: the U.S.–Bangladesh ART (Feb 2026) created a 0% TRQ for cotton T-shirts made with U.S. cotton. Sugar, dairy, beef, peanuts also have famous TRQs.

## 12. MPF (Merchandise Processing Fee)

Customs paperwork fee on every formal entry. Rate 0.3464%; FY2026 min $33.58, max $651.50. Charged even when duty is 0%.

## 13. HMF (Harbor Maintenance Fee)

Fee for U.S. seaport upkeep. Rate 0.125% — only on ocean shipments (not air, truck, or rail).

## 14. CBP (U.S. Customs and Border Protection)

The agency that enforces customs at the border. Collects duties, inspects cargo, issues binding rulings, processes drawback, runs FTZs and bonded warehouses. [cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/)

## 15. USTR (United States Trade Representative)

The Cabinet-level office that negotiates trade agreements and decides Section 301 actions. [ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/)

## 16. USITC (U.S. International Trade Commission)

Independent agency that maintains the official Harmonized Tariff Schedule and investigates AD/CVD cases. [usitc.gov](https://www.usitc.gov/)

## 17. FOB / CIF / Landed Cost

Shipping price terms (Incoterms):

- **FOB** — price up to loading on the ship at the foreign port.
- **CIF** — price including ocean freight + insurance to the U.S. port.
- **Landed cost** — total post-clearance cost (goods + duty + fees + freight + insurance + clearance).

The U.S. values goods at FOB-style transaction value for duty purposes.

## 18. CBP Binding Ruling

A free, written CBP decision telling you the correct HTS code for your product. ~30-day turnaround. Legally binding — protects you from later reclassification penalties. File at [rulings.cbp.gov](https://rulings.cbp.gov/).

## 19. ART (Agreement on Reciprocal Trade)

The bilateral deals signed in 2025–2026 to lower reciprocal tariffs (U.S.–Vietnam, U.S.–Bangladesh, U.S.–Indonesia, U.S.–India interim). Most ART rates were tied to IEEPA, so they ended with the Feb 2026 SCOTUS ruling — only the frameworks survive. The Section 122 10% surcharge is what applies now.

## 20. GSP (Generalized System of Preferences)

A program letting goods from developing countries enter at 0%. The U.S. GSP **expired end of 2020** and has not been renewed. Even when active, it usually excluded apparel and footwear. EU, UK, Canada, Japan, Australia still run their own GSP programs.

## 21. Country of Origin

Where the product was actually made — not where it shipped from. Just repackaging or relabeling does not change origin. All tariffs (Section 301, 122, AD/CVD) are based on country of origin. Lying about origin is customs fraud.

## 22. Transshipment (legal vs. illegal)

Goods passing through one country en route to another. **Legal:** Vietnamese sneakers transit Singapore — origin is still Vietnam. **Illegal:** Chinese sneakers shipped to Vietnam, relabeled "Made in Vietnam," then shipped to the U.S. to dodge Section 301 — that's a federal crime.

## 23. Re-Export Reality

Many guides oversell "import to the U.S., then re-export to avoid U.S. duty." In practice the destination country has its own duty, and you pay two legs of freight + two clearances. Direct shipping from source to destination almost always wins. See [common-strategies.md § 7](./common-strategies.md) for the framework.

|---|---|---|
| HTS | Harmonized Tariff Schedule | Product ID + duty rate book |
| MFN | Most Favored Nation | Normal/baseline duty |
| AD | Antidumping | Punishment for dumping |
| CVD | Countervailing Duty | Punishment for foreign subsidies |
| FTZ | Foreign-Trade Zone | "Outside U.S." warehouse |
| MPF | Merchandise Processing Fee | Customs paperwork fee |
| HMF | Harbor Maintenance Fee | Seaport upkeep fee |
| CBP | Customs and Border Protection | The customs agency |
| USTR | U.S. Trade Representative | Trade negotiator |

| TRQ | Tariff-Rate Quota | Two-tier duty system |
| FOB | Free On Board | Price up to ship loading |
| CIF | Cost, Insurance, Freight | Price including freight to U.S. port |

| GSP | Generalized System of Preferences | (Expired) 0% duty for poor countries |

- **CBP rulings:** [rulings.cbp.gov](https://rulings.cbp.gov/)
- **AD/CVD orders:** [trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties](https://www.trade.gov/us-antidumping-and-countervailing-duties)
- **USTR trade actions:** [ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/)
- **CBP general guidance:** [cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/)
- **Federal Register (latest tariff proclamations):** [federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/)

For real shipments, always work with a licensed customs broker.
