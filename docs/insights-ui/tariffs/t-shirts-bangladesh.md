# T-shirts / Cotton Apparel from Bangladesh — Detailed Tariff & Cost Reduction Guide

> **For beginners:** Read [basic-concepts.md](./basic-concepts.md) first if you don't know words like HTS code, MFN, TRQ, etc. For deep how-to detail on common strategies (First Sale Rule, FTZ, Bonded Warehouse, Drawback, Tariff Engineering, Country Switching), see [common-strategies.md](./common-strategies.md). This guide assumes you understand those terms.

---

## 1. The Product

- **What it is:** Basic men's, women's, and kids' cotton knit T-shirts (and similar simple knit garments).
- **Country of origin:** Bangladesh.
- **Why Bangladesh:** It is the **#2 apparel exporter in the world** after China, and **#1** for basic cotton knits. Strong vertically integrated supply chain (yarn → fabric → garments) at the cheapest labor rates of any major exporter (~$120/month minimum wage).

## 2. The HTS Code

Apparel HTS codes depend on:

1. **Knit vs. woven** (T-shirts are knit; dress shirts are woven)
2. **Fiber composition** (cotton vs. synthetic vs. wool — chapter 61 = knit, chapter 62 = woven)
3. **Gender** (men's, women's, unisex)
4. **Garment type** (T-shirts, polos, undershirts, tank tops)

| HTS Code | Description | MFN Duty |
|---|---|---|
| **6109.10.0012** | Men's/boys' cotton T-shirts, knit | **16.5%** |
| 6109.10.0040 | Women's/girls' cotton T-shirts, knit | 16.5% |
| 6109.90.1010 | Men's MMF (synthetic) T-shirts | 32% |
| 6109.90.1090 | Women's MMF T-shirts | 32% |
| 6105.10.00 | Men's cotton polo shirts (knit, with collar) | 19.7% |
| 6110.20.20 | Cotton sweaters/sweatshirts (knit) | 16.5% |

**Key insight:** **Synthetic-fiber T-shirts pay nearly DOUBLE the duty (32%)** compared to cotton (16.5%). Always confirm fiber composition.

## 3. Current Tariff Stack (April 2026)

| Component | Rate | Why it applies |
|---|---|---|
| MFN cotton T-shirts | **16.5%** | High base rate for apparel |
| Section 301 | 0% | Bangladesh (not China) |
| Reciprocal 35% (was active in 2025) | **0% now** | Struck down by SCOTUS Feb 2026 |
| Section 122 | **10%** | All countries, expires ~July 24, 2026 |
| AD/CVD | None | No active orders on Bangladesh apparel |
| GSP | N/A | GSP expired end of 2020; apparel was excluded anyway |
| **Special: U.S.-Bangladesh ART TRQ** | **0% on Section 122 portion** | If made with U.S.-origin cotton + within quota |
| **Total all-in duty (most cases)** | **26.5%** | |
| Total all-in (with TRQ qualification) | **16.5%** (just MFN) | |

## 4. Landed Cost Calculation on $10,000 of Cotton T-shirts

```
Product value (FOB):              $10,000.00
MFN duty (16.5%):                 $ 1,650.00
Section 122 (10%):                $ 1,000.00
MPF (0.3464%, min $33.58):        $    34.64
HMF (0.125%, ocean only):         $    12.50
                                  -----------
Total LANDED COST:                $12,697.14
Effective tariff rate:            26.97%
```

(Bangladesh → U.S. ocean freight: ~$1,000–$2,000 for a small container, not included.)

## 5. Detailed Ways to Reduce the Tariff Cost

### Option A: Use the U.S.-Bangladesh ART TRQ (Use U.S. Cotton)

**This is the single most powerful tool that's specific to Bangladesh.**

#### What the TRQ does:

The U.S.–Bangladesh Agreement on Reciprocal Trade (signed February 2026) created a **Tariff-Rate Quota** for textile and apparel goods made with **U.S.-origin cotton or man-made fiber inputs**.

- Up to a defined annual volume, qualifying T-shirts get **0% on the Section 122 portion**.
- The MFN 16.5% still applies (the TRQ doesn't waive MFN, only the country-specific extra).
- You must **prove and document** that the cotton/yarn comes from the U.S.

#### Math on a $10,000 import (with TRQ qualification):

- Without TRQ: $1,650 (MFN) + $1,000 (Section 122) = **$2,650 in duty**.
- With TRQ: $1,650 (MFN) + $0 (Section 122) = **$1,650 in duty**.
- **Saving: $1,000.**

#### Practical setup:

1. **Source your cotton from the U.S.** Major U.S. cotton exporters include Cargill, Allenberg, Cargill-Calcot, and Olam Cotton (USA division).
2. **Ship the cotton bales to Bangladesh.** Most U.S. cotton already goes to Bangladesh — they import ~600,000+ tons/year.
3. **Spinning, knitting, and finishing in Bangladesh.**
4. **Documentation:** Keep certificates of origin for the cotton, mill certificates, traceability records.
5. **Apply for TRQ allocation** through CBP / USTR-administered quotas.

#### Hurdles:

- The TRQ is **volume-limited** (the U.S. doesn't want unlimited duty-free imports). Once the quota is full, additional imports pay the full Section 122 surcharge.
- TRQs typically work **first-come, first-served** at the start of the year. Big brands grab the quota fast.
- Documentation requirements are **strict**. One missed certificate = lose the benefit.

#### Cost reality check: Does U.S. cotton actually save money?

This is the most important question — U.S. cotton **does cost more** than what Bangladesh mills would otherwise buy. Let's check whether the tariff saving still wins.

**Cotton price comparison (April 2026, raw fiber FOB):**

| Source | Approx. price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| U.S. cotton (medium-grade) | $0.85–$0.95/lb | High quality, full traceability |
| Brazilian cotton | $0.75–$0.85/lb | Major Bangladesh supplier |
| Indian cotton | $0.65–$0.75/lb | Cheaper, variable quality |
| Uzbek/Central Asian cotton | $0.65–$0.80/lb | Lower-grade |
| West African cotton | $0.70–$0.80/lb | Mid-grade |

A basic T-shirt uses about **0.5–0.6 lb of cotton fiber** (yarn losses + fabric weight).

**Math on a $10,000 order (~2,800 shirts at $3.50 FOB each):**

| Item | Local (cheaper) cotton | U.S. cotton |
|---|---|---|
| Cotton fiber needed (~1,600 lbs) | ~$1,200 (~$0.75/lb avg) | ~$1,440 (~$0.90/lb avg) |
| Extra cotton cost | — | **+$240** |
| Section 122 saved (TRQ) | — | **−$1,000** |
| TRQ paperwork / broker fee | — | **+$150** (first time; less on repeat) |
| **Net effect on $10,000 order** | baseline | **~$610 saving** |

**Verdict:**

- **Big brands (>$500k/year):** Clearly worth it. The cotton premium is small relative to the tariff saving, and admin cost amortizes over many shipments. Net savings of 5–8% of FOB.
- **Mid-size importers ($50k–$500k/year):** Still positive, but watch the admin overhead. Net saving drops to 2–4% of FOB once you factor first-time setup cost.
- **Small importers (<$25k/year):** Marginal. The $150–$300 in paperwork + risk of TRQ rejection or quota fill can wipe out the saving on a single shipment. Often not worth it.
- **Below $10k/year:** Almost never worth it. Stick with the standard 26.5% duty stack.

**Hidden non-cost benefits of U.S. cotton:**
- Better traceability — important for anti-forced-labor compliance (Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act blocks Xinjiang cotton; U.S. cotton is the safest sourcing).
- More uniform fiber quality — fewer defects, easier color matching.
- Marketing premium — "Made with U.S. cotton" labels can support a $1–$3 retail price increase per shirt for premium brands.

#### Best for:

- Brands ordering at scale ($500k+/year) who can negotiate cotton sourcing with mills.
- Brands willing to lock in U.S. cotton purchases 6+ months ahead.
- Brands selling to compliance-sensitive retailers (Walmart, Target, Costco) where U.S. cotton documentation is a side benefit.

### Option B: First Sale Rule

Bangladesh apparel supply chains typically look like:

```
Bangladesh factory  →  Singapore/HK trading agent  →  U.S. brand  →  U.S. retailer
   ($3.50)              ($5.00)                       ($8.00)         ($25)
```

If you declare duty on the **first sale ($3.50)** instead of the **last sale ($5.00)**, you save 30% on duty.

#### Math on $10,000 (first sale price = ~70% of import price):

- Last-sale duty (current): 26.5% × $10,000 = $2,650.
- First-sale duty: 26.5% × $7,000 = **$1,855**.
- **Saving: $795.**

#### Practical setup:

1. Get the **factory's invoice** (showing what they sold to the trading agent).
2. Get the **agent's invoice** (showing what you paid).
3. Show that the goods were destined for the U.S. from the start (POs reference U.S. addresses).
4. Use a customs broker experienced in first-sale claims.

#### Hurdles:

- Agents protect their margins. They may not want to show you the factory's invoice.
- For very low-priced goods (under $3 FOB), the first-sale price gap may be narrow.
- CBP audits these claims more carefully than other claims, so paperwork must be perfect.

### Option C: Tariff Engineering for Apparel

Apparel classification has many specific rules that can shift duty rates significantly. Here are the common moves:

#### Tactic 1: Tank tops vs. T-shirts

- **HTS 6109.10.0012** (men's cotton T-shirts): 16.5%
- **HTS 6109.10.00** subheadings include "tank tops" — but **tank tops with armhole bands sewn in a specific way** can fall under different HTS codes with different rates.

CBP's classification rules for "T-shirts" require certain construction features (no buttons, simple neckline, hem at the bottom). A garment that doesn't fit those features is "other knitwear."

#### Tactic 2: Add a chest pocket — change classification

- A standard cotton T-shirt is HTS 6109.10.
- Add a small pocket and binding details → may shift to HTS 6110 (sweaters/pullovers/sweatshirts) at the same 16.5%, but with different rule applicability.
- More importantly: a polo with a knit collar shifts to HTS 6105 at 19.7% (worse). So this can go either way — verify before changing.

#### Tactic 3: Garment "weight" thresholds

- Some apparel categories have **weight per square meter** thresholds. Heavier knits ("from light fabric") might fall in different sub-classification.
- A heavy-cotton "thermal shirt" can sometimes classify as **underwear** (HTS 6109.10.0027 men's cotton underwear T-shirts), with a duty rate that may differ.

#### Tactic 4: Fiber composition just under the 50% threshold

- Above 50% cotton: HTS 6109.10 (16.5%).
- Above 50% synthetic: HTS 6109.90 (32%).
- **Stay above 50% cotton to keep the lower rate.** Many fast-fashion brands accidentally cross the threshold by switching to cheaper polyester/cotton blends, doubling their duty.

#### Tactic 5: Embellishment thresholds

- Heavily embroidered garments (more than the 5 "decorative motif" rule allowed in the basic T-shirt code) move to the **"with embroidery"** subheadings, which have different duty rates and may require separate quota allocation.

#### Best practice:

- Always file a **CBP Binding Ruling** before locking in a new garment design.
- Test fiber composition with a third-party lab — claimed "100% cotton" goods are sometimes 95% cotton + 5% spandex, which can change classification.

### Option D: Move Sourcing to Another Country

| Country | MFN | Reciprocal/Country | Section 122 | Total | Comments |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bangladesh | 16.5% | 0% (post-SCOTUS) | 10% | 26.5% | TRQ available |
| India | 16.5% | 0% (post-SCOTUS) | 10% | 26.5% | Excellent capacity |
| Vietnam | 16.5% | 0% (post-SCOTUS) | 10% | 26.5% | Higher labor cost |
| Mexico (USMCA) | 0% | 0% | 0% | **0%** | Best by far |
| Honduras (CAFTA-DR) | 0% | 0% | 0% | **0%** | Pricey but FTA |
| Pakistan | 16.5% | 0% (post-SCOTUS) | 10% | 26.5% | Cotton powerhouse |

#### The standout: Mexico under USMCA

For T-shirts that meet USMCA "yarn forward" rules (yarn made in Mexico/USA/Canada, fabric knit there, garment cut and sewn there), duty is **0%**.

#### Math:

- Bangladesh T-shirt FOB: $3.50, total landed cost (with duty + freight): $4.95
- Mexico T-shirt FOB: $5.50 (higher labor), total landed cost: $5.95

Mexico is **~20% more expensive** per unit, but you get:

- 1-week shipping vs. 4–6 weeks
- 0% duty
- Easier supplier visits
- Lower carrying cost on inventory

For brands selling fast-fashion or replenishment items where speed matters, Mexico beats Bangladesh on total economics. For commodity basics where price-per-shirt is everything, Bangladesh wins.

#### Honduras / Central America (CAFTA-DR)

Similar story — yarn-forward rules apply. Prices typically between Mexico and Bangladesh.

#### When does moving sourcing actually make sense?

Switching countries is the biggest single tariff lever, but it's also the most disruptive. Use this checklist:

**Move to Mexico (USMCA) when:**
- You need **fast replenishment** (1-week shipping vs. 4–6 weeks from Bangladesh). Critical for fast-fashion and seasonal lines.
- Your shirts are **higher-value** ($8+ FOB) where the labor-cost gap shrinks in percentage terms.
- You sell mainly in **North America** (no need for Asian distribution hub).
- Your annual volume can support a Mexican factory's MOQ (typically **50,000–100,000 shirts/year**).
- You want to qualify for **0% USMCA duty** under yarn-forward rules (yarn + fabric + cut/sew all in NA).

**Move to Honduras / CAFTA-DR when:**
- Volume is **between Mexico and Bangladesh scale** ($200k–$2M/year).
- You want a hedge against Mexico capacity issues but still need NA proximity.
- Yarn-forward sourcing is feasible.

**Move to India / Pakistan when:**
- Bangladesh capacity is constrained during peak season (Q3 holiday rush).
- You want **vertical cotton integration** (the same supplier grows cotton and makes shirts).
- You want to **diversify country risk** — Bangladesh has had political/labor disruptions.
- Tariff stack is identical to Bangladesh (16.5% MFN + 10% Section 122), so this is a capacity/risk play, not a tariff play.

**Move to Vietnam when:**
- You need **premium quality** (better fabrics, finishing, technical garments).
- You're already buying other apparel from Vietnam (consolidation savings).
- You can absorb 15–25% higher per-unit cost.

**Stay with Bangladesh when:**
- **Cost-per-shirt is the deciding factor** (commodity basics, private label, dollar stores).
- **Annual volume is small** (<25,000 shirts) — you can't get good prices from Mexico/Vietnam at low volume.
- Lead time is not a competitive issue.
- You can use the **ART TRQ** (see Option A) to neutralize most of the Section 122 surcharge.

**General rule of thumb:** Switching countries is rarely worth it for a single $10,000 shipment. Transition costs (factory development, sample rounds, QA setup, tooling) typically run **$5,000–$20,000 per new factory** before you save a single dollar. The math works only at sustained volume — usually **$200,000+/year** through the new factory.

### Option E: Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ)

> See [common-strategies.md § 2](./common-strategies.md) for the general FTZ explanation. Below is when FTZ is a winner specifically for T-shirts.

#### When FTZ wins for T-shirts:

- **Annual import volume >$1M/year.** FTZ has $30k–$60k setup + $20k–$50k/year in operating costs (compliance staff, software, security). Below ~$1M/year, fees eat the savings.
- **You re-export 10%+ of inventory** (Canada, Mexico, Latin America off-price channels). No U.S. duty on the re-exported portion = direct duty saving.
- **Slow inventory turn (>60 days from import to sale).** Apparel often sits 2–4 months — FTZ defers the 26.5% duty for the whole holding period, freeing working capital.
- **Section 122 expiry timing.** Goods sitting in an FTZ when the Section 122 surcharge ends (~July 24, 2026) can clear at the lower post-expiry rate. Saves 10% of FOB.
- **Returns processing.** International returns can leave the FTZ without U.S. duty being assessed.

#### When FTZ does NOT make sense for T-shirts:

- **Volume <$500k/year** — overhead exceeds savings.
- **Pure domestic, fast-turnover business** (sells within 30 days). The cash-flow benefit is too small.
- **Single SKU drop-ships** (one shipment a year) — bonded warehouse is simpler and cheaper.

#### Math on $1M/year T-shirt importer:

- Section 122 (10%) deferred on $1M = ~$100k temporarily held back. Working-capital benefit at 6% cost of capital = ~$3k/year.
- Re-export benefit if 15% goes to Canada: avoids ~$40k in U.S. duty.
- Tariff inversion: minimal for T-shirts (no big finished-vs-parts gap).
- Possible Section 122 timing saving: $50k–$100k one-shot.
- **Net annual benefit: ~$50k–$100k.** Worth it if it exceeds the ~$50k overhead.

#### Easier on-ramp:

You don't need to set up your own FTZ. Many 3PL providers operate **shared FTZs** — pay $1,000–$5,000/month with no upfront investment. Best for first-time FTZ users.

### Option F: Bonded Warehouse

> See [common-strategies.md § 3](./common-strategies.md) for the general bonded warehouse explanation. Below is when it's a winner for T-shirts.

#### When Bonded Warehouse wins for T-shirts:

- **Section 122 expiry timing — the biggest near-term opportunity in 2026.** If your goods arrive in May–July 2026 and you can hold customs entry until after July 24, 2026, you skip the entire 10% Section 122 surcharge. **Saves $1,000 on a $10,000 shipment.**
- **Mid-size importers ($100k–$1M/year)** — too small for an FTZ, but big enough to benefit from 5-year duty deferral.
- **Seasonal inventory** — order holiday shirts in June, hold in bonded warehouse, clear in November when ready to sell.
- **Occasional re-export** (10–20% of inventory) — bonded re-exports pay no U.S. duty.
- **You don't need to manufacture or rework** the goods (FTZ is better for that).

#### Cost / benefit on a $10,000 shipment held 3 months:

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Storage (~30 sqft × 3 months × $1/sqft) | $90 |
| Customs bond (annual, prorated) | $50–$200 |
| Broker entry fee | $100–$200 |
| **Total cost** | **~$240–$490** |
| Section 122 saved (if cleared after July 24, 2026) | **−$1,000** |
| **Net saving on $10,000** | **~$510–$760** |

#### When NOT to use bonded warehouse:

- Goods are seasonal and selling **now** — you'd miss the season.
- Inventory turns fast (<30 days) — storage cost beats the deferral benefit.
- **Section 122 gets extended past July 2026** by Congress — the timing benefit disappears. Verify the legal status before committing inventory.
- You only have a single shipment a year and the saving doesn't cover broker fees.

#### Setup:

Most importers don't operate their own bonded warehouse — they rent space at a third-party CBP-bonded facility. Most major customs brokers offer this as a service. Setup is **days, not months**.

### Option G: Duty Drawback (and the Re-Export Reality Check)

#### **Re-Export Reality Check for Bangladesh T-shirts:**

If you import to the U.S. and ship to (say) the EU:

**Option 1: Bangladesh → U.S. → EU (drawback)**
- Bangladesh → U.S. ocean: $0.30/shirt
- U.S. duty: 26.5% × $5 = $1.33
- U.S. → EU air/ocean: $0.50/shirt
- EU import duty on Bangladesh-origin cotton T-shirts: **0%** (Bangladesh has GSP "Everything But Arms" status with EU).
- Drawback: -$1.32 (99% of $1.33)
- Net per shirt: ~$0.81 (mostly extra freight)

**Option 2: Bangladesh → EU directly**
- Bangladesh → EU ocean: $0.40/shirt
- EU duty: 0% (Bangladesh-EU EBA preferences)
- Net per shirt: $0.40

**Direct shipping to EU is way cheaper** — about half. EU's duty-free treatment of Bangladesh apparel makes re-exporting through the U.S. wasteful.

#### Same logic for Canada and UK:

Both Canada and UK extend GSP-like preferences to Bangladesh apparel (0% duty). Direct shipping wins.

#### When drawback works for Bangladesh apparel:

- You're a U.S. brand selling primarily in the U.S.
- A small portion (10–15%) ends up exported through normal business (returns, redistribution).
- You can recover 99% of the U.S. duty paid on those exported units.

### Option H: Cut-and-Sew in U.S. with Imported Bangladesh Fabric

Some brands import **Bangladeshi fabric** (much lower duty than finished apparel) and cut/sew in the U.S.

- Cotton knit fabric (HTS 6006.21): MFN ~10% (lower than 16.5% on the finished shirt).
- "Made in USA" branding premium.

This works for high-margin "premium basics" brands (e.g., Buck Mason, American Giant). Not viable for fast-fashion price points.

## 7. The Best Practical Option for Bangladesh T-shirts

### **Recommendation: Combine ART TRQ + First Sale Rule**

**Why:**

- The **ART TRQ** is uniquely available for Bangladesh and saves a clean $1,000 on a $10,000 order. No other country gets this benefit.
- **First Sale Rule** stacks on top — it reduces the duty base, so your savings are multiplicative.
- Combined savings can hit **15–20% of import value** ($1,500–$2,000 on $10,000) without changing factories.
- Both are well-tested, low-risk strategies used by brands like H&M, Gap, Walmart, Levi's, and Target.

**Implementation order:**

1. **Confirm fiber composition** — make sure your shirts are >50% cotton (not accidentally synthetic-blend).
2. **File a CBP binding ruling** to lock in the 16.5% classification.
3. **Negotiate U.S. cotton sourcing** with your Bangladesh mill. Many already buy U.S. cotton.
4. **Apply for TRQ allocation** through your customs broker.
5. **Set up first-sale documentation** with your buying agent — get factory invoices.
6. **Audit annually** to make sure savings are real.

**When to skip these and just move sourcing:**

- If your brand is small (<$100k/year) and the paperwork burden is too much.
- If Mexico's 1-week shipping is critical (replenishment or fast-fashion).
