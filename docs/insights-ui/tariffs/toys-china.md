# Toys from China — Detailed Tariff & Cost Reduction Guide

> **For beginners:** Read [basic-concepts.md](./basic-concepts.md) first if you don't know words like HTS code, MFN, Section 301, FTZ, drawback, etc. This guide assumes you understand those terms.

---

## 1. The Product

- **What it is:** Plastic and mixed-material children's toys (action figures, dolls, building sets, plush toys, etc.).
- **Country of origin:** China.
- **Why China dominates:** China makes **80%+** of the world's toys. The supply chain (plastics, electronics, packaging, mold-making) is concentrated in Guangdong province.

## 2. The HTS Code

| HTS Code | Description | MFN Duty |
|---|---|---|
| **9503.00.0073** | "Other" toys, put up in sets or outfits (most action figure / role-play sets) | **0%** |
| 9503.00.0011 | Dolls representing only human beings | 0% |
| 9503.00.0090 | Other toys, n.e.s. | 0% |
| 9504.50.0000 | Video game consoles | 0% |
| 9505.10.4020 | Christmas ornaments, plastic | 0% |

**Toys are normally duty-free** under MFN. The duty problem comes from extra tariffs.

## 3. Current Tariff Stack (April 2026)

| Component | Rate | Why it applies |
|---|---|---|
| MFN (base) | 0% | Toys are duty-free |
| Section 301 List 4A | **7.5%** | China-only; still active after SCOTUS ruling |
| Section 122 surcharge | **10%** | Temporary, all countries, expires ~July 24, 2026 |
| **Total extra duty** | **17.5%** | |

## 4. Landed Cost Calculation on $10,000 of Toys

```
Product value (FOB):              $10,000.00
MFN duty (0%):                    $     0.00
Section 301 (7.5%):               $   750.00
Section 122 (10%):                $ 1,000.00
MPF (0.3464%, min $33.58):        $    34.64
HMF (0.125%, ocean only):         $    12.50
                                  -----------
Total LANDED COST:                $11,797.14
Effective tariff rate:            17.97%
```

(Shipping cost from China to U.S. typically adds another **$300–$1,500** for a small ocean shipment, not included above.)

## 5. Detailed Ways to Reduce the Tariff Cost

### Option A: Move Sourcing to a Different Country

**The biggest single saving for toys.** The 7.5% Section 301 only applies to China-made toys. If you make the toys somewhere else, that 7.5% disappears entirely.

#### Real options for toy manufacturing:

| Country | Section 301? | Section 122? | Total extra | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| China | 7.5% | 10% | **17.5%** | Current baseline |
| Vietnam | None | 10% | **10%** | Strong toy capacity, especially plush + plastic |
| India | None | 10% | **10%** | Growing toy industry, government incentives |
| Mexico | None | None (USMCA) | **0%** | Best by far if you can find a maker |
| Indonesia | None | 10% | **10%** | Limited toy capacity |
| Cambodia | None | 10% | **10%** | Some plush/wooden toy makers |

#### What to actually do:

1. **Visit Canton Fair (China) and Chinaplas to source initial designs**, but ask suppliers if they have **Vietnam or Mexico facilities** (many big Chinese toy makers do — including Mattel, Hasbro contract manufacturers).
2. **For plush / soft toys:** Vietnam and Cambodia are excellent.
3. **For complex plastic toys:** Vietnam and India have grown a lot. Mexico is the gold-standard for North-American sales.
4. **For electronic toys:** Vietnam has good electronics assembly; China still leads on components.

#### The math on a $10,000 order:

- Stay in China: pay $1,750 in extra tariff (Section 301 + 122).
- Move to Vietnam: pay $1,000 in extra tariff. **Saving: $750.**
- Move to Mexico: pay $0 in extra tariff (USMCA). **Saving: $1,750.**

#### Practical hurdles:

- Vietnam and Mexico typically charge **8–15% more** per unit than China for the same toy due to smaller scale.
- Tooling (the molds for plastic toys) is expensive — moving production means re-tooling unless your supplier already has a sister factory.
- Mexico is best for higher-volume (10,000+ units), automated production.

### Option B: Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ)

You import toys into an FTZ near a major port (Los Angeles, Long Beach, Houston, Savannah). You only pay the duty when toys leave the FTZ for U.S. sale.

#### When this saves money:

1. **Re-exports to Canada/Mexico/Latin America:** Toys that leave the FTZ for another country = **$0 U.S. duty**.
2. **Cash-flow:** If toys sit in inventory for 3 months before retail sale, you delay paying the duty for those 3 months.
3. **Section 301 protection:** If the Section 301 rate later drops or gets eliminated, goods in the FTZ might benefit (but recent rules limit this — check with your broker).

#### Practical reality:

- You don't need to own an FTZ. Rent space in one (most major 3PL warehouses near ports have FTZ status).
- Setup cost: $5,000–$20,000 first-year setup + ~$1–$2 per cubic foot per month storage.
- **Worth it if you import $500,000+/year in toys.** Below that, the setup cost eats the savings.

---

### Option C: Bonded Warehouse

Simpler version of an FTZ. Store the toys for up to 5 years; pay duty only when released for sale.

#### When to use:

- You buy toys for a specific season (e.g., Christmas) but want to import early to spread shipping cost.
- You have variable demand and don't want to commit duty cash too early.

#### Practical reality:

- Setup is faster and cheaper than FTZ.
- Most warehouses near ports have bonded status — just ask your 3PL.
- Costs: ~$0.50–$1.00 per cubic foot per month, similar to regular storage.

---

### Option D: First Sale Rule

Toy supply chains usually have layers:

```
Chinese factory  →  Hong Kong trading company  →  U.S. importer  →  U.S. retail
   ($4)               ($6)                          ($8)              ($14.99)
```

If you declare duty on the **first sale ($4)** instead of the **last sale ($6)**, you save 33% on duty.

#### How much you save on $10,000:

- Last-sale duty (current): 17.5% × $10,000 = $1,750.
- First-sale duty (factory price 2/3 of sale price): 17.5% × $6,667 = **$1,167**.
- **Saving: $583.**

#### Practical setup:

1. Get the factory's invoice (showing the price they sold to the trading company).
2. Get the trading company's invoice (showing what you paid).
3. Show CBP that the goods were destined for the U.S. from the start (purchase orders, design specs, shipping labels for U.S. addresses).
4. Use a customs broker experienced in first-sale claims.

#### When it doesn't work:

- If you buy directly from the factory (no middleman, no first sale advantage).
- If the trading company refuses to share the factory's invoice (common — they don't want you to know their margin).
- If the markup is small (under 10%).

### Option E: Tariff Engineering for Toys

The **HTS classification of a toy can change based on its features**. Here are the most common toy tariff engineering tactics:

#### Tactic 1: "Educational" or "Instructional" Toys

- **HTS 9023.00.00** ("Instruments, apparatus, and models, designed for demonstrational purposes") has duty 0% and may not be on the Section 301 List 4A.
- For example: a science kit with experiments and a printed lesson booklet may classify here instead of toys (9503).
- LeapFrog and similar brands have used this approach for talking books and educational electronics.

#### Tactic 2: Toy Sets vs. Individual Toys

- Some toy sets fall into specific subheadings with different Section 301 list assignments.
- A single doll is HTS 9503.00.0011; a doll **with accessories sold together** can be HTS 9503.00.0073.
- The Section 301 list assignment for these subheadings can differ.

#### Tactic 3: Stuffed vs. Unstuffed Animals

- HTS 9503.00.0090 covers most "other" toys, including unstuffed animals.
- Toys "stuffed" with material can sometimes fall under different subheadings.

#### Tactic 4: Add Real Functional Use

- A toy phone that can also receive emergency calls might classify under telecommunications (different HTS chapter).
- A toy kitchen set with real working appliances may classify under appliances.
- *Caution:* The "real function" must be genuine and meaningful — not just a token feature.

#### Tactic 5: Material Composition

- A wooden toy (HTS 9503.00.0011) might face different Section 301 treatment than a plastic version.
- However, **most toys are on List 4A** so material change rarely shifts the Section 301 rate within toys.

#### Best practice:

- File a **CBP Binding Ruling** for any new design before mass production.
- Track Section 301 list assignments carefully (USTR has changed the lists multiple times; check the current annex).

### Option F: Duty Drawback (and the Re-Export Reality Check)

If you import toys to the U.S., pay the 17.5% extra duty, then re-export them to Canada/Mexico/Australia, you can recover **99% of the duty paid**.

#### Why it sounds appealing:

- Recovers $1,732 out of $1,750 paid on a $10,000 China toy import.

#### **The Re-Export Reality Check:**

Let's say your customer is in Canada. Two options:

**Option 1: Import to U.S., then re-export to Canada (drawback)**
- Pay China → U.S. shipping: ~$800
- Pay U.S. duty: $1,750
- Pay U.S. → Canada shipping: ~$400
- Pay Canada import duty (Canada also has duties on toys, ~0% on most toys MFN): ~$0
- Get U.S. drawback refund: -$1,732
- **Net cost: ~$1,218 in extra logistics, just to get a drawback refund.**

**Option 2: Direct from China to Canada**
- Pay China → Canada shipping: ~$1,000
- Pay Canada import duty on toys: ~$0 (Canada doesn't have a Section 301 equivalent)
- **Net cost: ~$1,000 in shipping.**

**Conclusion:** For toys, **direct shipping to Canada is cheaper** than re-export with drawback. Drawback for toys only makes sense if:

1. You're a **U.S.-based brand** with U.S. as your main market.
2. A small portion of inventory **naturally** gets shipped to Canada/Mexico (returns, redistribution, B2B).
3. You're already paying the U.S. duty for the U.S. portion.

In that scenario, drawback is "found money" — but it's not a strategy to actively pursue.

### Option G: Importer Self-Assessment / Reconciliation Programs

CBP has programs (Importer Self-Assessment, Reconciliation) that let you fix valuation/classification issues over a year and get refunds. Useful for large importers ($1M+/year).

## 7. The Best Practical Option for Toys

### **Recommendation: Move to Vietnam or India.**

**Why this beats other options:**

- **Saves $750 on $10,000 (7.5% Section 301).** Real money.
- **Easy to implement** — many Chinese toy makers already have sister facilities in Vietnam.
- **No legal risk** — you're just changing where the goods are made.
- **No upfront capital** like FTZ setup or first-sale documentation.

**When to combine strategies:**

- If you're a brand doing $500,000+/year in toy imports: Vietnam sourcing **+ FTZ** **+ First Sale Rule** can stack savings to ~15% of value.
- If you ship to Mexico/Canada too: add **drawback** on the export portion.

**When NOT to move sourcing:**

- If your toys require complex tooling that you've already amortized in China.
- If your run quantities are small (under 5,000 units) — minimum order quantities in Vietnam/Mexico can be high.
- If you sell to retail buyers who specifically want China-made (rare but exists in collectibles).
