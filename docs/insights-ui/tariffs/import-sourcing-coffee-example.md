## What you should be able to learn from this report

By the time you finish reading, you should be able to answer:

1. Where does U.S. coffee come from, and how much?
2. What does it actually cost to import 1 lb (or 1 container) from each country, all-in?
3. What are the **good things** and **bad things** about buying from each country — quality, price, payment terms, lead times, port reliability, climate risk, certifications, fraud risk?
4. Which country fits **my** specific business — am I a 3-person specialty roaster, a 50-store grocery brand, a private-label decaf seller, or an instant-coffee blender?

6. What can go wrong, and how do I protect myself?

If after reading you still need to "go look up a few more things" before deciding, this report has failed its job.

## 1. Why Coffee Is a Good Example

- **One of the largest U.S. food imports.** The U.S. is the world's #1 coffee importer; coffee sits on almost every grocery shelf, café, and office.
- **Almost 100% imported.** The U.S. only grows a tiny amount in Hawaii, Puerto Rico, and California; everything else comes from other countries.
- **Many real source countries.** Unlike toys (mostly China) or smartphones (mostly China + Vietnam), coffee is sourced from 15+ countries. That makes it a real comparison, not a fake one.
- **Clear unit price.** A pound of green coffee is a unit anyone understands.
- **Uses every type of trade rule.** MFN duties, FTAs (USMCA, CAFTA-DR, CTPA, PTPA), preference programs (AGOA), and temporary surcharges all apply to coffee in some way — so the comparison shows the full picture.

## 2. The Product and HTS Codes

| HTS Code | Description | MFN Duty |
|---|---|---|
| **0901.11** | Green (unroasted) coffee, not decaffeinated | **0%** (Free) |
| 0901.12 | Green coffee, decaffeinated | 0% |
| 0901.21 | Roasted coffee, not decaffeinated | 0% |
| 0901.22 | Roasted coffee, decaffeinated | 0% |
| 0901.90 | Coffee husks, skins, substitutes containing coffee | 0% |
| 2101.11 | Extracts, essences, concentrates of coffee (instant) | 0% |
| 2101.12 | Preparations with a basis of coffee extracts | 8.5%* |

*HTS 2101.12 (ready-to-drink coffee, sweetened blends) carries a real MFN duty. Most importers buying green or roasted coffee fall under HTS 0901.x — duty-free.

**Key point:** Coffee enters the U.S. duty-free under MFN. Cost differences across countries come from **temporary surcharges, freight, quality, and average price per pound** — not the base tariff.

## 6. Hidden Costs and Fees Most Buyers Forget

Tariff alone is misleading. The full landed cost per pound includes:

| Cost | What it is | Typical value |
|---|---|---|
| FOB price | Price at the port of origin (Free On Board the ship) | $1.20–$5.00/lb |
| Ocean freight | Shipping by container | $1,400–$2,500 / 20-ft container |
| Marine insurance | ~0.3–0.5% of cargo value | $100–$300 / container |
| MPF (Merchandise Processing Fee) | 0.3464% of value, min $32, max $634.62 | up to $635 / entry |
| HMF (Harbor Maintenance Fee) | 0.125% of cargo value (sea only) | varies |
| Customs broker | Filing entry summary, ISF, etc. | $150–$400 / entry |
| ISF (Importer Security Filing) | 10+2 filing pre-arrival | $35–$50 / shipment |
| Drayage / trucking from port | Move container to warehouse | $300–$900 |
| Warehousing / palletizing | Receiving at U.S. warehouse | $50–$200 |
| FDA Prior Notice | Filing food import notice | $0 if self, $50–$100 via agent |
| USDA/APHIS exam (random) | Pest inspection | $200–$500 if pulled |
| Demurrage / detention | If you don't pick up the container fast enough | $150–$300/day |
| Quality reject / cup score risk | Coffee that fails contract spec | varies |
| FX cost | Wire fees + spread, especially for non-USD payments | 0.5–2% |

**Rule of thumb:** add roughly **$0.10–$0.20/lb on top of FOB** for everything that isn't tariff and freight.

## 7. Lead Time, Harvest Season, and Payment Timing

Coffee is seasonal — buying at the right point in the year gives you fresher beans and better prices.

| Country | Main harvest months | "Fresh crop" lands in U.S. | Typical payment terms | Vessel transit to U.S. |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brazil | May – Sep | Aug – Nov | Cash against documents (CAD), 30-day terms common | 16–22 days to NY/NJ |
| Colombia | Main: Oct – Dec; Mitaca: Apr – Jun | year-round (two harvests) | CAD or 60-day | 6–10 days to Houston / NJ |
| Vietnam | Oct – Jan | Feb – May | T/T 30% deposit + balance on B/L | 22–30 days to LA/Long Beach |
| Honduras | Nov – Mar | Mar – Jun | CAD, occasional 30-day | 5–8 days to Gulf Coast |
| Guatemala | Nov – Mar | Mar – Jun | CAD, sometimes pre-finance with importer | 6–9 days to Gulf Coast |
| Nicaragua | Nov – Mar | Mar – Jun | CAD | 6–9 days to Gulf Coast |
| Mexico | Nov – Mar | Mar – May | T/T or CAD | 4–7 days to Gulf Coast |
| Indonesia (Sumatra) | Oct – Mar | Apr – Jul | T/T deposit + LC | 25–32 days to LA |
| Ethiopia | Oct – Dec | Mar – Jun | LC at sight (usually mandatory) | 28–35 days via Suez to NJ |
| Peru | May – Sep | Sep – Dec | CAD | 12–16 days via Panama to Gulf Coast |

**Why this matters:** if you are roasting for a holiday (Q4), you need to lock in fresh-crop Honduras/Guatemala/Mexico **months earlier** because the boats only leave during a small window. Indonesia and Ethiopia have the longest lead times, so plan 3–4 months ahead.

## 8. Quality Grading and Certifications by Country

Different countries use different grading systems. Knowing them prevents getting tricked.

| Country | Grading system | What "good" looks like | Common certifications |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brazil | Screen size (14–19), defect count, NY 2/3 | NY 2/3, screen 17/18, fine cup | Rainforest Alliance, UTZ, some organic |
| Colombia | Excelso / Supremo (screen size) | Supremo screen 17+, MAM (Medellín–Armenia–Manizales) origin | Fair Trade, organic, Rainforest |
| Vietnam | Grade 1, Grade 2, defects-per-300g | G1 13% moisture, ≤30 defects | UTZ; less common organic / Fair Trade |
| Honduras | SHG (Strictly High Grown), HG, CS | SHG 1300m+, cup ≥84 | Organic, Fair Trade, Rainforest, Bird Friendly |
| Guatemala | SHB (Strictly Hard Bean), HB, EPW | SHB 1500m+, Antigua / Huehue origin | Anacafé "GENUINE", organic |
| Nicaragua | SHG, HG, EP | SHG 1400m+, Jinotega/Matagalpa | Organic, Fair Trade |
| Mexico | Altura, Prima Lavado, Buen Lavado | Altura 1100m+, Chiapas / Veracruz | Organic, Fair Trade |
| Indonesia | Grade 1–6, defect count | Sumatra Mandheling Grade 1, ≤11 defects | Organic, Rainforest, Bird Friendly |
| Ethiopia | Grade 1–9, ECX cup score | G1/G2 Yirgacheffe, cup 85+ | Organic, Fair Trade, single-origin traceable |
| Peru | Grade 1, MCM, defects | G1, organic-certified, high-altitude | Organic (very strong), Fair Trade |

If your brand requires **Rainforest Alliance, Fair Trade USA, Organic (USDA NOP), Bird Friendly (Smithsonian), or Certified B Corp** sourcing, only some countries reliably supply each. The certifications column matters.

## 9. Country-by-Country Profile (Pros and Cons)

This is the core of the report. Read whichever countries are on your shortlist.

### Brazil

- **What it's good for:** Volume and price. Brazil supplies the largest share of U.S. coffee. If you need a steady, mid-quality Arabica or Robusta blend base, Brazil is the default.
- **Advantages:**
  - Largest exporter in the world — supply is rarely a problem.
  - Best price-per-pound for "good enough" Arabica.
  - Many large exporters and cooperatives — easy to find suppliers.
  - Good infrastructure (Santos port handles ~80% of exports).
  - Mechanically harvested, so consistent grading.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Currently pays Section 122 surcharge (10%) — narrows the gap with FTA countries.
  - Frost or drought (2021, 2024) can spike prices 30–60% in months.
  - Mostly **mid-quality**; not where you go for prize-winning specialty.
  - Currency (BRL) volatility — when BRL weakens, Brazil gets cheaper, but prices in USD can swing 10% in weeks.
  - Mass-harvested = less traceability if you want single-farm transparency.
- **Best fit:** Big roasters making blends; instant-coffee blenders; private-label grocery brands.
- **Risks to watch:** Frost, currency shock, ASTRA strikes at Santos.

### Colombia

- **What it's good for:** Premium washed Arabica, FTA-exempt landed cost, two harvests per year so supply is steady.
- **Advantages:**
  - **CTPA (FTA)** — exempt from Section 122 (verify per shipment).
  - Two harvests (main Oct–Dec and Mitaca Apr–Jun) = year-round fresh crop.
  - Strong national brand (FNC — Federación Nacional de Cafeteros) — trusted quality.
  - Reliable washed processing and clean cup profile.
  - Excellent decaf production (Mountain Water decaf).
  - Short transit times to U.S. East and Gulf coasts.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - More expensive per pound than Brazil and Honduras for similar grade.
  - Heavy rains (La Niña years) can damage harvests.
  - Specialty supply is concentrated in Huila / Nariño — tight competition for top lots.
  - Less Robusta — if you need cheap blender Robusta, Colombia is not the place.
- **Best fit:** Specialty roasters wanting premium washed Arabica, decaf-focused brands, year-round flagship blends.
- **Risks to watch:** Climate (La Niña), strikes, occasional FNC pricing intervention.

### Vietnam

- **What it's good for:** Cheapest coffee in the world. Robusta volume.
- **Advantages:**
  - Lowest FOB price per pound globally.
  - Huge supply (#2 global producer after Brazil).
  - Excellent for instant-coffee bases, espresso blends needing crema, and cost-driven private-label.
  - Big consolidated exporters (Trung Nguyen, Vinacafe, Intimex) — large lot sizes available.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Currently pays Section 122 surcharge (10%).
  - Robusta is **lower-cup-quality** than Arabica — almost no specialty.
  - Long ocean transit (22–30 days) and pricier freight to East Coast.
  - Quality consistency varies between harvests; defect counts can spike.
  - Less third-party certification (organic, Fair Trade are rare).
  - Some buyers report aggressive renegotiation when global prices drop.
- **Best fit:** Instant-coffee makers, cost-driven blends, espresso/crema blenders, food-service.
- **Risks to watch:** Drought (2024 cut output ~20%), shipping delays, USD/VND fluctuation.

### Honduras

- **What it's good for:** The "value sweet spot" for Arabica right now — FTA-exempt + decent-to-specialty quality + close to U.S. ports.
- **Advantages:**
  - **CAFTA-DR (FTA)** — exempt from Section 122.
  - Cheapest Arabica with FTA exemption — beats Colombia on price.
  - Strong washed processing.
  - Short transit (5–8 days to Gulf Coast) — faster restock cycles.
  - Many small farms = good organic / Fair Trade availability.
  - Honduran coffee institute (IHCAFE) increasingly trusted on grading.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - **Coffee leaf rust (roya)** has hit Central America hard since 2012; quality varies year to year.
  - Smaller industry than Brazil/Colombia — cup profile less consistent across exporters.
  - Some political instability around elections.
  - Highest-grade specialty is not as celebrated as Guatemala/Ethiopia.
- **Best fit:** Private-label grocery brands, mid-tier specialty roasters, organic / Fair Trade brands wanting an alternative to Peru.
- **Risks to watch:** Roya outbreaks, political volatility, hurricane season (Sep–Nov).

### Guatemala

- **What it's good for:** **Specialty Arabica.** Antigua and Huehuetenango are world-famous origins.
- **Advantages:**
  - **CAFTA-DR (FTA)** — exempt from Section 122.
  - Among the most respected specialty origins in the world.
  - Anacafé runs strict grading and provides traceability.
  - Diverse microclimates = wide range of cup profiles for blenders.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Higher FOB than Honduras/Nicaragua for similar grade.
  - Roya and climate stress hit Guatemala too.
  - Top-tier Antigua lots are expensive and limited.
  - Smaller export volumes mean booking ahead is essential.
  - Some farms are at higher altitudes — labor shortages occasionally affect harvest timing.
- **Best fit:** Specialty roasters, single-origin café brands, premium retail bags.
- **Risks to watch:** Climate, labor shortages during peak harvest, hurricane risk.

### Nicaragua

- **What it's good for:** A cheaper substitute for Guatemala/Honduras with similar profile.
- **Advantages:**
  - **CAFTA-DR (FTA)** — exempt from Section 122.
  - Good mid-specialty Arabica from Jinotega and Matagalpa.
  - Often $0.20–$0.30/lb cheaper than Guatemala for similar grade.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - **Political and U.S. relations risk** — sanctions risk has flared up periodically; check OFAC before signing.
  - Smaller industry, fewer exporters to choose from.
  - Roya impact ongoing.
  - Transit and infrastructure slightly weaker than Honduras.
- **Best fit:** Buyers wanting Honduras-style quality at a discount, when politics are stable.
- **Risks to watch:** OFAC / sanctions actions, political volatility.

### Mexico

- **What it's good for:** Easiest customs of any source country — overland trucking option, USMCA simplicity.
- **Advantages:**
  - **USMCA (FTA)** — exempt from Section 122.
  - Closest of all sources — can ship by truck overland (no ocean freight risk).
  - Mature **Mountain Water decaf** processing — top-tier decaffeination origin.
  - Easy paperwork; small-volume orders feasible.
  - Strong organic certification ecosystem in Chiapas and Oaxaca.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Volume is limited — Mexico is not a huge coffee producer.
  - Quality is mostly mid-tier; specialty is small.
  - Cartel-related risk in some growing regions affects logistics.
  - FOB price is higher than Brazil.
  - Dollar peso swings can move prices ±10%.
- **Best fit:** Organic / Fair Trade brands, decaf-heavy buyers, brands wanting low-friction reorder cycles.
- **Risks to watch:** Cartel-disrupted regions (parts of Guerrero), border bottlenecks.

### Indonesia

- **What it's good for:** Distinctive Sumatran (wet-hulled "Mandheling") and Java profiles. Robusta also abundant.
- **Advantages:**
  - Unique cup profile (heavy body, low acidity) — irreplaceable for some blends.
  - Wide quality range: cheap Robusta to top specialty.
  - Strong organic and Bird Friendly certifications available.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Currently pays Section 122 surcharge (10%).
  - Long transit (25–32 days) and higher freight.
  - **Highest defect rates** of major origins — wet-hulling produces inconsistency.
  - Lots of small intermediaries — traceability is harder.
  - Earthquake / volcano disruption in Sumatra and Java.
- **Best fit:** Specialty roasters wanting Sumatra/Java profile, blenders needing heavy-body component.
- **Risks to watch:** Earthquakes, volcanic ash damage, monsoon-driven shipping delays.

### Ethiopia

- **What it's good for:** **The most prestigious specialty origin in the world.** Yirgacheffe, Sidamo, Guji, Limu — all are top-tier single-origin names.
- **Advantages:**
  - World-class cup quality — wins almost every "best of" competition category.
  - Genetic diversity unique to Ethiopia.
  - Strong traceability via ECX (Ethiopia Commodity Exchange) and direct-trade channels.
  - High retail price-per-bag potential.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Currently pays Section 122 surcharge (10%) — AGOA preference is in renewal limbo.
  - **Highest FOB price** of any major origin.
  - Long transit (28–35 days via Suez).
  - LC at sight is usually required — banking friction.
  - Tigray / regional conflict has previously disrupted exports.
  - ECX rules occasionally restrict direct-farm purchases.
- **Best fit:** Specialty / single-origin café brands, $20+ retail bag premium roasters.
- **Risks to watch:** Conflict, AGOA expiration, Suez disruption, currency controls.

### Peru

- **What it's good for:** **Best origin for organic and Fair Trade certification at scale.** Strong cooperative system.
- **Advantages:**
  - **PTPA (FTA)** — exempt from Section 122.
  - Largest organic-certified coffee producer in Latin America.
  - Strong cooperative system = small-farm traceability and Fair Trade compliance.
  - Mid-to-high cup quality at fair prices.
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Smaller industry than Colombia / Brazil — limited specialty volume.
  - Internal logistics (mountain regions) can delay shipments.
  - Quality variance between coops can be wide.
  - Political instability has affected logistics in the past.
- **Best fit:** Certified-organic brands, Fair Trade brands, retail private-label organics.
- **Risks to watch:** Political/civil unrest, mountain logistics.

## 11. What This Tells the Buyer

- **Cheapest per pound: Vietnam.** Best for instant coffee blends and big-volume Robusta. Quality is not specialty.
- **Best Arabica value: Honduras and Brazil.** Honduras wins when Section 122 is active because it gets FTA exemption. Once Section 122 expires (~July 24, 2026), Brazil becomes more competitive again.
- **Best premium Arabica: Colombia and Guatemala.** Higher price per pound, but FTA exemption helps offset.
- **Best specialty (highest price per pound): Ethiopia.** Pays Section 122; only worth it for specialty roasters who can charge $18+/bag retail.
- **Smallest customs paperwork risk: Mexico (USMCA).** Volume is limited but quality is decent and customs is simple.
- **Best certified-organic origin: Peru.** Followed by Mexico and Honduras.

## 12. Documentation and Regulatory Checklist (per shipment)

Every U.S. coffee import — **regardless of origin** — needs:

- **Commercial invoice** (with HTS code, country of origin, FOB value).
- **Packing list** (bags, weight per bag, lot numbers).
- **Bill of Lading (B/L)** or Air Waybill.
- **ISF 10+2 filing** at least 24 hours before vessel loads.
- **FDA Prior Notice** (food import, mandatory).
- **Phytosanitary certificate** from origin country (USDA / APHIS requirement).
- **Certificate of Origin** if claiming FTA preference (USMCA, CAFTA-DR, CTPA, PTPA — each has its own format).
- **ICO (International Coffee Organization) certificate** — historically required, less strict today but some buyers still ask.
- **Organic / Fair Trade certificate** if claiming those.
- **Customs entry summary (CBP Form 7501)** filed by your broker.

Country-specific extras:

| Country | Extra docs / things to watch |
|---|---|
| Brazil | Cecafe export registration |
| Colombia | FNC stamp / mark-of-origin certificate |
| Vietnam | C/O Form B (sometimes Form A residue) |
| Honduras / Guatemala / Nicaragua | CAFTA-DR certificate of origin |
| Mexico | USMCA C/O; if trucked overland, no maritime ISF |
| Indonesia | SUCOFINDO inspection certificate sometimes required |
| Ethiopia | ECX export contract; Letter of Credit usually mandatory |
| Peru | SENASA phytosanitary; coop traceability certificates if Fair Trade |

## 13. Common Problems and Scams (How to Protect Yourself)

These are the issues importers actually run into. None of them are unique to coffee, but they hit coffee imports often.

1. **"Sample-and-switch."** The cup-tested sample is great, the container that arrives isn't. Defense: contractual cup-score floor, third-party SGS / Cargill quality inspection at origin before loading.
2. **Defects above contract spec.** You agreed to ≤8 defects per 300g; you got 22. Defense: contract clause for rejection or price discount tied to defect count.
3. **Moisture above 12.5%.** Causes mold, weight loss, and rejection. Defense: pre-shipment moisture test.
4. **FX wire fraud.** Email impersonating supplier asks for payment to a new bank account. Defense: confirm by phone with the actual contact before any wire.
5. **Sub-grade Robusta passed off as Arabica.** Especially in low-end blends. Defense: cup at receipt, not just pre-purchase.
6. **Misclassification under HTS.** A broker codes your roasted decaf as a sweetened preparation (HTS 2101.12 → 8.5% duty) instead of 0901.22 (0%). Defense: review entry summaries.
7. **Demurrage from slow pickup.** A container sits at port over weekend = $300/day. Defense: schedule drayage before vessel arrives.
8. **AGOA / FTA paperwork errors.** A missing or incorrect certificate of origin = full duty assessment retroactively. Defense: get the C/O reviewed by a broker before filing.
9. **AD/CVD scope creep.** Coffee is not currently subject to AD/CVD, but blends or preparations sometimes are. Defense: HTS check on ALL sub-components.
10. **Counterfeit Fair Trade / Organic certificates.** Defense: verify cert numbers directly with the certifying body's online database.

## 16. Quick "If-Then" Recommendations

- **If** Section 122 is still active and you sell Arabica blends in U.S. groceries → **buy Honduras** (FTA + low FOB).
- **If** Section 122 expires (~Jul 24, 2026) → **shift to Brazil** for cost; Honduras still wins for organic/Fair Trade lines.
- **If** you need 1 container and don't want to deal with LCs → **buy Mexico or Honduras**.
- **If** your brand sells $20+ specialty single-origin bags → **Ethiopia first, Guatemala second**.
- **If** your brand is sustainability-led → **Peru (organic), Mexico (organic), Honduras (Fair Trade)**.
- **If** you make instant or RTD coffee → **Vietnam Robusta + small Brazil top-up**.
- **If** Brazil has a frost year → **lean Colombia + Honduras**; expect spot prices to spike for 12–18 months.
- **If** Red Sea / Suez is closed → **Ethiopia FOBs become unworkable**; expect 10–15 day longer transit and reroute fees.
- **If** you need year-round fresh-crop supply → **Colombia (two harvests)** is the only single-country answer.

## 17. Caveats and Risks (Be Honest)

- **Coffee prices swing 30–60% in a year.** Frost in Brazil, drought in Vietnam, or harvest disease can flip the rankings within months.
- **Quality is not in this table.** Two coffees at the same price can taste very different. Always cup-test before signing a contract.
- **Section 122 is temporary.** When it expires, the gap between FTA and non-FTA countries will narrow.
- **Non-tariff barriers exist.** FDA Prior Notice, USDA APHIS, organic certification, mycotoxin (ochratoxin A) rules — all add cost and are not in this comparison.
- **Freight cost is volatile.** A Red Sea closure or a Panama Canal drought can change the answer.
- **AD/CVD checks.** Coffee currently has no anti-dumping orders, but always verify before signing.
- **AGOA renewal status** affects Ethiopia preference treatment.
- **Climate change is real and reshaping origins.** Lower-altitude Arabica regions are stressed; specialty growers are moving up the mountain. Long-term, expect Honduras/Nicaragua/low-altitude Mexico to be at risk.
- **One-pager warning:** this report is a starting point. For real purchase decisions, a licensed customs broker, a green-coffee importer/trader, and a Q-grader cupping the actual lot all need to be in the loop.
