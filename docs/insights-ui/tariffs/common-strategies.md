# Common Tariff Reduction Strategies — How They Work and When to Use Them

This file is the **how-to guide** for the legal tariff-reduction methods that show up across all five product reports (toys, sneakers, T-shirts, bedsheets, furniture). Each strategy is explained in detail so a beginner can see:

- **What it does** (in plain English)
- **How big the saving usually is**
- **When it works well** (the conditions that make it worth doing)
- **When it does NOT work** (so you don't waste effort)
- **Setup cost and effort** (real numbers)
- **Common mistakes to avoid**

For shorter definitions of any term, see [basic-concepts.md](./basic-concepts.md). For per-product application of each strategy, see the individual product files.

---

## 1. First Sale Rule

### What it does

When goods change hands multiple times before reaching the U.S. importer, the First Sale Rule lets you pay duty on the **first** (factory-to-middleman) sale price instead of the **last** (middleman-to-you) sale price.

```
Factory  →  Trading agent  →  U.S. importer  →  U.S. customer
($5)         ($7)              ($10)             ($30 retail)
                ↑
        Without First Sale Rule, duty is on $7
        With First Sale Rule, duty is on $5
```

### How big is the saving?

The first-sale price is typically **20–35% lower** than the importer's price. So duty drops by the same percentage on the dutiable base.

| Duty rate | Markup % saved | Saving on $10,000 |
|---|---|---|
| 10% | 25% | $250 |
| 16.5% (T-shirts) | 25% | $412 |
| 20% (sneakers) | 30% | $600 |
| 26.5% (T-shirts incl. Section 122) | 30% | $795 |

### When First Sale Rule works well

- There is a **clear middleman** in the chain (trading agent, sourcing agent, buying office).
- The middleman's markup is **at least 15%** — smaller markups don't move the needle.
- The **duty rate is high (15%+)**. The higher the rate, the bigger the dollar saving.
- The factory is willing to **disclose its invoice price** to the middleman (and to you, indirectly).

### When it does NOT work

- You buy **directly from the factory** — no middleman, no first-sale gap.
- The middleman is **related** to you (related-party transactions need extra documentation under U.S. law).
- The middleman **refuses to share invoices** (some agents fiercely protect their margin).
- Markup is small (<10%) — paperwork cost can exceed the saving.

### Setup cost and effort

- **One-time setup:** $1,500–$5,000 for a customs broker / trade attorney to review the supply chain.
- **Per-shipment effort:** ~1 hour additional documentation per entry.
- **Documentation needed:** Factory invoice, middleman invoice, payment records, evidence the goods were destined for the U.S. from the start (POs referencing U.S. delivery addresses help).

### Common mistakes

- **Trying to use First Sale on a single-tier purchase** (factory → you). There is no first sale to claim.
- **Using a related party** as the "first seller" without proper transfer-pricing documentation. CBP audits these aggressively.
- **Lacking documentation.** If you can't produce the factory's invoice in an audit, CBP rejects the claim and bills you the difference plus penalties.

### Legal basis

*Nissho Iwai American Corp. v. United States* (1988). Reaffirmed many times since. CBP guidance: 19 CFR § 152.103.

## 2. Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ)

### What it does

A Foreign-Trade Zone is a U.S. site (warehouse or industrial park) that legally counts as **outside U.S. customs territory**. Goods can sit there indefinitely without duty being assessed. You pay duty only when goods move from the FTZ into U.S. commerce. If goods leave the FTZ to be **re-exported**, you pay no U.S. duty at all.

### How big is the saving?

Four layers of benefit:

1. **Cash flow:** Duty is deferred until sale. On a $1M/year importer, that frees up $50k–$100k of working capital.
2. **Re-export saving:** No duty on goods that go to Canada, Mexico, or other countries. Up to 100% saving on those units.
3. **Tariff inversion:** Import parts, assemble in the FTZ, and pay the lower **finished-goods** rate on output. (Limited under recent rules.)
4. **Temporary tariff timing:** Sit on goods until a temporary surcharge (like Section 122) expires. Pay the lower post-expiry rate.

### When FTZ wins

- Annual import volume **>$1M/year** (the only level where setup cost is recovered).
- You re-export at least **10%** of your inventory.
- Inventory turns slowly (>60 days from import to sale).
- You manufacture or assemble in the U.S. with imported components.
- A temporary tariff (like Section 122) is set to expire — sit on goods until rate drops.

### When FTZ does NOT make sense

- Annual volume **<$500k**: setup cost alone eats the savings.
- Fast-turnover business (you sell within 30 days of import).
- Pure domestic sales, no re-export.
- Single annual shipment — a bonded warehouse is simpler.

### Setup cost and effort

- **Custom subzone setup:** $30k–$60k (FTZ Board application, security, IT integration).
- **Ongoing operating cost:** $20k–$50k/year (compliance staff, software, weekly entry filings).
- **Time to set up:** 6–12 months for a custom subzone.
- **Easier path:** Many 3PL providers operate **shared FTZs** — rent space for $1k–$5k/month with no up-front investment. **Best option for first-time FTZ users.**

### Common mistakes

- **Setting up an FTZ without enough volume.** Most small importers regret it.
- **Mixing FTZ-status and non-FTZ goods** in the same warehouse without segregation — audit nightmares.
- **Forgetting the weekly/quarterly entry filings** that FTZs require.

### Where to start

[FTZ Board directory](https://www.trade.gov/ftz). 300+ FTZs across the U.S.

## 3. Bonded Warehouse

### What it does

A bonded warehouse is a private facility approved by U.S. Customs where you can **store imported goods for up to 5 years without paying duty**. You pay duty when goods leave for U.S. sale. If you re-export, you pay no U.S. duty.

It is the **simpler, cheaper cousin of the FTZ** — less paperwork, less manufacturing flexibility, but a much lower bar to entry.

### How big is the saving?

Three flavors:

1. **Tariff timing:** Hold goods until a temporary tariff expires. The Section 122 surcharge (10%) is set to expire ~July 24, 2026. Goods cleared after that date skip the 10%. **This is the biggest near-term opportunity in 2026.**
2. **Cash flow:** Duty is deferred for up to 5 years.
3. **Re-export:** No U.S. duty on goods that leave the U.S. again.

### When bonded warehouse wins

- **Section 122 expiry timing** (the biggest 2026 use case). Importing in May–July 2026 and clearing after July 24, 2026 saves the entire 10% surcharge.
- Mid-size importers ($100k–$1M/year) — too small for an FTZ, big enough that 5-year deferral helps cash flow.
- Seasonal inventory you don't want to clear all at once.
- Importers who occasionally re-export (10–20% of inventory).

### When bonded warehouse does NOT make sense

- Fast-moving inventory (sells in <30 days from import) — storage cost beats the deferral benefit.
- You need to rework or repackage extensively (FTZ is better for that).
- Section 122 gets extended past July 2026 — the main timing benefit goes away (verify the legal status before betting).

### Setup cost and effort

- **Setup:** $0–$1,500 if you use a third-party bonded warehouse — most importers do.
- **Ongoing storage:** $0.40–$1.50/sqft/month.
- **Customs bond:** Required (~$500–$3,000/year for a typical importer; you may already have one).
- **Time to set up:** Days to weeks (much faster than FTZ).

### Common mistakes

- **Underestimating storage cost.** A 5,000-sqft bonded warehouse for a year can cost $30,000+.
- **Missing the 5-year deadline.** Goods past 5 years are auctioned by CBP.
- **Treating bonded warehouse like an FTZ** — you can't manufacture or significantly transform goods there.

## 4. Duty Drawback

### What it does

If you import goods, pay duty, and then re-export them (or use them to make a product that gets exported), CBP refunds **up to 99% of the duty paid**.

### Three flavors

1. **Unused merchandise drawback:** Imported, paid duty, never used the goods, exported. 99% refund.
2. **Manufacturing drawback:** Imported parts, paid duty, made a product in the U.S., exported the finished product. 99% of the parts duty back.
3. **Substitution drawback:** Even if you exported a different (but commercially similar) product than you imported, you can claim 99% under specific rules.

### How big is the saving?

99% of the duty paid on the exported portion. So if 15% of your $1M/year imports get re-exported and paid $50k in duty, you can recover ~$49,500.

### The Re-Export Reality Check

**Drawback sounds great, but it does not magically erase costs.** Here is the reality if you try to use it as a primary tariff strategy:

If you import to the U.S. just to re-export elsewhere (say, Canada or the EU), you face:

- The destination country's **own import duty** on the same goods.
- **Two legs of shipping** instead of one.
- **Two customs clearances** instead of one.
- Drawback recovers your U.S. duty, but **not** the foreign duty or the extra freight.

**Direct shipping from source country to destination country is almost always cheaper** when re-export is the goal.

#### Example: Bangladesh T-shirts to EU

| Route | U.S. duty | EU duty | Freight | Net cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bangladesh → U.S. → EU (with drawback) | $1.33/shirt → refunded $1.32 | 0% (Bangladesh has EU GSP) | $0.30 + $0.50 = $0.80 | ~$0.81/shirt extra |
| Bangladesh → EU direct | — | 0% | $0.40 | $0.40/shirt |

Direct shipping wins by ~$0.40/shirt.

### When drawback DOES work (the real use cases)

- **Returns from international customers.** Some inventory naturally comes back and gets re-exported — recover the duty.
- **Regional distribution.** A U.S.-based brand that distributes to Canada and Mexico from a U.S. warehouse — the portion shipped north/south qualifies.
- **Manufacturing for export.** If you import components, build something in the U.S., and export the finished product, drawback recovers the input duty.

### When drawback does NOT make sense

- Pure re-export "tariff dodge" plans (see reality check above).
- Sketchy paperwork. Drawback eligibility requires **records linking specific imports to specific exports** — sloppy records = denied claim.
- You are filing more than **5 years** after import (the deadline is 5 years).

### Setup cost and effort

- **Filing per claim:** $500–$2,000 (typical broker fee).
- **Recordkeeping:** A detailed import-to-export tracing system.
- **Time to refund:** 3–12 months after filing.
- **Bond required:** ~$50,000+ for accelerated payment privilege.

## 5. Tariff Engineering

### What it does

Tariff engineering is the **legal practice of designing a product so it falls under a different (lower-duty) HTS code**. The product really must have the new feature — you are not lying, you are designing.

### Famous historical examples

- **Converse**: added felt to the rubber sole. Felt-soled shoes have ~12% duty vs. ~37% for rubber-soled. Saved millions.
- **Columbia Sportswear**: redesigned women's overcoats with a zip-out lining. Changed classification from "outerwear" to "household article."
- **Marvel toys**: argued action figures were "non-human creatures" (different HTS, lower duty than "dolls"). Won in court.
- **Snuggie blankets**: classified as "blankets" (low duty) instead of "garments" (high duty) by adding sleeves but no closures.

### How big is the saving?

Highly variable — typically **5–25 percentage points** of duty rate. On high-duty products (apparel, footwear, toys), this can mean **15–50% of the duty bill**.

### Common categories of tariff engineering

1. **Material composition:** Apparel duty depends on fiber blends. Cotton vs. synthetic, leather vs. textile, etc.
2. **Construction details:** Felt soles on footwear, embroidery thresholds on garments, sugar/fat ratios in food.
3. **Use case description:** Action figures vs. dolls, decorative item vs. functional, novelty vs. utility.
4. **Subdivision into parts:** Knock-down (KD) furniture often pays a different duty than assembled. Components and finished products often differ.
5. **Edge of HTS bracket:** Where the tariff schedule has thresholds (price, weight, dimension), design near the favorable side.

### When tariff engineering works well

- High-duty products (15%+) — savings worth the design cost.
- Products with clear **material/construction levers** (apparel, footwear, food, toys, furniture).
- You have **long-term volume** — design changes pay off over many shipments.
- You can get a **CBP Binding Ruling** confirming the new classification before changing your supply chain.

### When tariff engineering does NOT work

- Low-duty products (1–3%) — too small to justify design effort.
- Single shipment / one-time purchase — design cost not recoverable.
- Products where the lower-duty code requires a feature your customer would dislike.

### Critical: get a CBP Binding Ruling first

A binding ruling is **free and takes ~30 days**. It locks in the classification — CBP can't change its mind later. **Never make a tariff engineering change without one.** Companies that skipped this step have paid millions in back duties.

How to file: [CBP CROSS Rulings system](https://rulings.cbp.gov/).

### Common mistakes

- **Changing the marketing description but not the actual product.** CBP won't accept this.
- **Skipping the binding ruling** and assuming your interpretation is right.
- **Borderline classifications** (could go either way) — CBP often picks the higher-duty one if challenged.

## 6. Sourcing Alternatives (Country Switching)

### What it does

Move production from a high-tariff country to a lower-tariff one. Often the biggest single tariff saving you can achieve — but also the most disruptive.

### Major lower-tariff country options

| Source country | Trade benefit | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Mexico | USMCA: 0% on most goods (with rules of origin) | NA-focused brands, fast turnaround needs |
| Canada | USMCA: 0% on most goods | High-tech, niche manufacturing |
| Honduras / Guatemala / Nicaragua / El Salvador / DR | CAFTA-DR: 0% on apparel with yarn-forward | Textile/apparel brands |
| Jordan / Israel / Bahrain | FTAs with 0% | Apparel, jewelry |
| Vietnam | No FTA, but no Section 301; quality is high | Premium apparel, footwear, electronics |
| India | No FTA; large capacity, English-speaking | Textiles, leather, machinery |
| Indonesia / Malaysia / Thailand | No FTA; competitive labor cost | Furniture, food, rubber |
| Bangladesh / Pakistan / Cambodia | No FTA; cheap labor; some special programs | Cotton apparel basics |

### How big is the saving?

Compare the total tariff stack (MFN + Section 301/232 + Section 122) across countries. For some products, switching from China to Vietnam saves **25+ percentage points** in tariffs. Switching from Asia to Mexico under USMCA can drop the duty rate to **0%** entirely.

### When country switching makes sense

- The tariff difference is **>10 percentage points** (covers transition costs).
- You have **sustained volume** ($200k+/year through the new factory).
- You can absorb a **6–12 month transition** (sample rounds, QA, supply chain rebuilding).
- The product is **standardized** (basic apparel, commodity furniture) — easier to move than custom-engineered goods.

### When country switching does NOT make sense

- Single $10,000 shipment — transition cost ($5k–$20k for a new factory) eats the savings.
- Highly customized product with a deep supplier relationship.
- Tariff savings <5 percentage points — not worth the disruption.
- The alternative country has higher quality / IP / labor compliance risks.

### Hidden costs of switching

- **Sample rounds:** $2,000–$10,000 per round, 2–4 rounds typical.
- **Factory audits:** $3,000–$8,000 per factory.
- **Tooling / molds:** Plastic injection or shoe molds can be $20,000–$200,000.
- **Quality variance:** 10–20% defect rate during ramp-up is normal.
- **Lead time disruption:** 3–6 months of overlap inventory needed.

### Stack with other strategies

Country switching is often best **combined** with First Sale Rule (a Mexican factory with a U.S. agent in the chain), or with FTZ (a Mexican factory feeding goods staged in a U.S. FTZ for re-export).

## 7. Re-Export Reality Check (General Framework)

Many tariff guides oversell the idea of "import to the U.S., then re-export to avoid U.S. duty." In real practice this rarely saves money because:

1. **The destination country has its own import duty.** Bangladesh-origin T-shirts may pay 0% in the EU, but the same shirts pay normal MFN rates in Australia, Japan, and many other markets.
2. **Two legs of freight.** Source → U.S. → destination is almost always more expensive than source → destination directly.
3. **Two customs clearances.** Each adds $100–$500 in broker fees.
4. **Inventory carrying cost.** Goods sitting in the U.S. mid-route tie up working capital.

### The framework: when does U.S.-routing actually win?

| Condition | U.S. routing makes sense |
|---|---|
| You have a **major U.S. distribution hub** | Yes — re-export from existing operations is cheap |
| You're **breaking bulk** in the U.S. for many small markets | Yes — consolidation savings |
| The destination has **high import duties** AND no preference with the source country | Sometimes — compare math |
| You're **handling returns** | Yes — drawback recovers duty efficiently |
| You're **manufacturing in the U.S.** with imported parts | Yes — manufacturing drawback works |
| You're trying to avoid the destination's duty | No — re-routing doesn't change that |
| You're trying to avoid U.S. duty alone | No — direct shipping wins |

## 8. Stacking Strategies (When to Combine)

The strategies above are not mutually exclusive. The best importers stack them. Common combinations:

| Combination | Best for | How it stacks |
|---|---|---|
| **First Sale + Tariff Engineering** | Apparel, footwear | Lower base × lower rate |
| **First Sale + FTZ** | Multi-tier supply chain importers | Lower base + cash flow |
| **FTZ + Drawback** | Manufacturers exporting | Defer duty + recover on exports |
| **Bonded Warehouse + Section 122 timing** | All importers in 2026 | Defer until surcharge expires |
| **Country switch + USMCA/CAFTA** | Apparel, furniture | 0% MFN on entire shipment |
| **TRQ + First Sale (Bangladesh apparel)** | Big apparel brands | Quota benefit + lower base |

## 9. Quick Decision Guide

If your annual imports are...

| Volume | Best strategies to focus on |
|---|---|
| **<$50k/year** | First Sale Rule (if multi-tier), HTS verification, occasional bonded warehouse |
| **$50k–$500k/year** | First Sale Rule, Tariff Engineering, bonded warehouse for timing, drawback if re-exporting |
| **$500k–$5M/year** | All of the above + FTZ consideration, country switching for new SKUs |
| **>$5M/year** | All strategies stacked + dedicated trade compliance team + binding rulings on everything |

## 10. Always Verify

Tariff law changes constantly. Before committing real money to any strategy:

- **Check current rates** at [hts.usitc.gov](https://hts.usitc.gov/).
- **File a binding ruling** for any non-trivial classification at [rulings.cbp.gov](https://rulings.cbp.gov/).
- **Work with a licensed customs broker** — their fees are tiny compared to costly mistakes.
- **Re-check the Section 122 status** — it's the biggest variable in 2026.
