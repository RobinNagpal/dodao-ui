# Common Tariff Reduction Strategies — How They Work and When to Use Them

A how-to summary of the legal tariff-reduction methods. For shorter definitions of any term, see [basic-concepts.md](./basic-concepts.md).

---

## 1. First Sale Rule

**What it does:** When goods change hands multiple times before reaching the U.S. importer, pay duty on the **first** (factory-to-middleman) sale price instead of the last (middleman-to-you) price.

**Saving:** First-sale price is typically 20–35% lower, so duty drops by the same percentage on the dutiable base. Bigger dollar saving when the duty rate is high (15%+).

**When it works:** Clear middleman with ≥15% markup; high-duty product; factory willing to disclose its invoice. **When it doesn't:** Direct factory purchase; related-party middleman without transfer-pricing docs; agent refuses to share invoices; markup <10%.

**Setup:** $1,500–$5,000 one-time broker/attorney review. Per-shipment ~1 hour extra documentation. Need factory invoice, middleman invoice, payment records, and evidence the goods were destined for the U.S. from the start.

**Common mistakes:** Trying to claim it on a single-tier purchase; using a related party without proper documentation; lacking the factory invoice if audited.

**Legal basis:** *Nissho Iwai American Corp. v. United States* (1988); CBP guidance 19 CFR § 152.103.

---

## 2. Foreign-Trade Zone (FTZ)

**What it does:** A U.S. site that legally counts as outside U.S. customs. Duty is assessed only when goods leave the FTZ for U.S. commerce. Re-exports leave with no U.S. duty.

**Saving:** Four layers — cash flow (deferred duty), re-export saving (0% on goods that leave the U.S.), tariff inversion (pay finished-goods rate on assembled output), and timing (sit on goods until a temporary surcharge expires).

**When it works:** Annual import volume >$1M; ≥10% re-export share; slow inventory turn (>60 days); U.S. assembly with imported components; waiting out a temporary tariff like Section 122. **When it doesn't:** Volume <$500k; fast-turnover business; pure domestic sales; one-shot annual shipment.

**Setup:** Custom subzone $30k–$60k + $20k–$50k/year ongoing; 6–12 months. **Easier path:** rent space in a 3PL-operated shared FTZ for $1k–$5k/month with no up-front cost — best option for first-time users.

**Common mistakes:** Setting up at insufficient volume; mixing FTZ and non-FTZ goods without segregation; missing required entry filings.

**Where to start:** [FTZ Board directory](https://www.trade.gov/ftz).

## 3. Bonded Warehouse

**What it does:** A CBP-approved private facility where you can store imported goods up to 5 years without paying duty. Pay duty when goods leave for U.S. sale; no U.S. duty on re-exports. Simpler and cheaper than an FTZ, with no manufacturing rights.

**Saving:** Three flavors — Section 122 timing (clear after the ~July 24, 2026 expiry to skip the 10% surcharge — biggest 2026 use case); cash flow (up to 5-year deferral); re-export.

**When it works:** Section 122 expiry timing; mid-size importers ($100k–$1M) too small for an FTZ; seasonal inventory; occasional re-export (10–20%). **When it doesn't:** Fast inventory (<30-day sale cycles); needs heavy rework (use FTZ); Section 122 gets extended past July 2026.

**Setup:** $0–$1,500 if using a third-party warehouse; storage $0.40–$1.50/sqft/month; customs bond $500–$3,000/year (you may already have one). Days-to-weeks setup.

**Common mistakes:** Underestimating storage cost; missing the 5-year deadline (CBP auctions it); treating a bonded warehouse like an FTZ.

## 4. Duty Drawback

**What it does:** CBP refunds up to 99% of duty when imported goods are later re-exported (or used to make a product that gets exported).

**Three flavors:** Unused merchandise (imported, never used, exported); manufacturing (imported parts, U.S.-built finished good, exported); substitution (commercially similar product exported instead).

**Saving:** 99% of duty paid on the exported portion.

**Re-Export Reality Check:** Drawback recovers your U.S. duty but does not erase the destination country's import duty, the second leg of freight, the second customs clearance, or the U.S. inventory carrying cost. **Direct source-to-destination shipping almost always beats U.S.-routed re-export.**

**When it really works:** Returns from international customers; regional distribution from a U.S. hub to Canada/Mexico; U.S. manufacturing for export. **When it doesn't:** Pure re-export "tariff dodge" plans; sloppy import-to-export records; filings more than 5 years after import.

**Setup:** $500–$2,000 per claim (broker fee); detailed import-to-export tracing; 3–12 months from filing to refund; ~$50,000+ bond for accelerated payment.

## 5. Tariff Engineering

**What it does:** Legally design a product so it falls under a different (lower-duty) HTS code. The product really must have the new feature — design, not deception.

**Famous examples:** Converse added felt to rubber soles (~12% vs ~37%); Columbia zip-out lining changed outerwear → household article; Marvel argued action figures were "non-human creatures"; Snuggies classified as blankets, not garments.

**Saving:** Highly variable, typically 5–25 percentage points of duty rate. On apparel, footwear, toys, this can be 15–50% of the duty bill.

**Common levers:** Material composition (cotton vs synthetic, leather vs textile); construction details (felt soles, embroidery thresholds); use case (action figure vs doll); KD/assembled split; HTS bracket thresholds.

**When it works:** High-duty products (15%+); clear material/construction levers; long-term volume; you can get a CBP Binding Ruling first. **When it doesn't:** Low-duty products; one-time purchase; new features the customer dislikes.

**Critical:** Get a CBP Binding Ruling **before** changing your supply chain — free, ~30 days, locks in the classification. Skipping this has cost companies millions in back duties. File at [rulings.cbp.gov](https://rulings.cbp.gov/).

**Common mistakes:** Changing only the marketing description, not the product; skipping the binding ruling; relying on borderline classifications.

## 6. Sourcing Alternatives (Country Switching)

**What it does:** Move production from a high-tariff country to a lower-tariff one. Often the largest single saving — also the most disruptive.

**Major options:**

| Source country | Trade benefit | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Mexico | USMCA: 0% on most goods (with rules of origin) | NA-focused brands, fast turnaround |
| Canada | USMCA: 0% on most goods | High-tech, niche manufacturing |
| Honduras / Guatemala / Nicaragua / El Salvador / DR | CAFTA-DR: 0% on apparel with yarn-forward | Textile/apparel brands |
| Jordan / Israel / Bahrain | FTAs with 0% | Apparel, jewelry |
| Vietnam | No FTA but no Section 301; high quality | Premium apparel, footwear, electronics |
| India | No FTA; large capacity | Textiles, leather, machinery |
| Indonesia / Malaysia / Thailand | No FTA; competitive labor | Furniture, food, rubber |
| Bangladesh / Pakistan / Cambodia | No FTA; cheap labor | Cotton apparel basics |

**Saving:** Compare the full tariff stack (MFN + Section 301/232 + Section 122). China → Vietnam can save 25+ percentage points; Asia → Mexico under USMCA can drop the duty rate to 0%.

**When it works:** Tariff difference >10 pp; sustained volume ($200k+/year through the new factory); 6–12 month transition tolerable; standardized product. **When it doesn't:** Single small shipment; deeply customized product; tariff savings <5 pp; quality/IP/labor risk in the alternative.

**Hidden costs:** Sample rounds $2k–$10k each (2–4 typical); factory audits $3k–$8k; tooling/molds $20k–$200k; 10–20% defect rate during ramp-up; 3–6 months overlap inventory.

**Stack with other strategies:** Country switching often pairs well with First Sale Rule (Mexican factory + U.S. agent) or FTZ (Mexican factory feeding a U.S. FTZ for re-export).

## 7. Re-Export Reality Check (General Framework)

Many tariff guides oversell "import to the U.S., then re-export." In practice it rarely saves money because:

1. The destination country has its own import duty.
2. Two legs of freight beat one.
3. Two customs clearances cost $100–$500 each in broker fees.
4. Inventory mid-route ties up working capital.

**When U.S.-routing actually wins:**

| Condition | Wins? |
|---|---|
| Major U.S. distribution hub already in place | Yes |
| Breaking bulk in the U.S. for many small markets | Yes |
| Destination has high duties AND no preference with the source country | Sometimes |
| Handling international returns (drawback) | Yes |
| U.S. manufacturing with imported parts (manufacturing drawback) | Yes |
| Trying to dodge the destination's duty | No |
| Trying to dodge U.S. duty alone | No — direct shipping wins |

## 8. Stacking Strategies

The strategies above are not mutually exclusive. Common combinations:

| Combination | Best for |
|---|---|
| First Sale + Tariff Engineering | Apparel, footwear |
| First Sale + FTZ | Multi-tier supply chain importers |
| FTZ + Drawback | Manufacturers exporting |
| Bonded Warehouse + Section 122 timing | All importers in 2026 |
| Country switch + USMCA/CAFTA | Apparel, furniture |
| TRQ + First Sale (Bangladesh apparel) | Big apparel brands |

## 9. Quick Decision Guide

| Annual import volume | Best strategies to focus on |
|---|---|
| **<$50k** | First Sale (if multi-tier), HTS verification, occasional bonded warehouse |
| **$50k–$500k** | First Sale, Tariff Engineering, bonded warehouse for timing, drawback if re-exporting |
| **$500k–$5M** | All of the above + FTZ consideration, country switching for new SKUs |
| **>$5M** | All strategies stacked + dedicated trade compliance team + binding rulings on everything |

---

## 10. Always Verify

Tariff law changes constantly. Before committing real money:

- Check current rates at [hts.usitc.gov](https://hts.usitc.gov/).
- File a binding ruling for non-trivial classifications at [rulings.cbp.gov](https://rulings.cbp.gov/).
- Work with a licensed customs broker.
- Re-check the Section 122 status — it's the biggest moving piece in 2026.
