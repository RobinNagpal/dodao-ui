## 1) Check revenue growth against spending growth

**What to check**

* Revenue growth
* Cost of revenue growth
* Sales and marketing growth
* Total operating expense growth

**Why this matters**

A company can show strong revenue growth, but if costs are rising almost as fast, or even faster, then the quality of that growth is weaker. The stock market usually punishes that.

For Sea in Q4 2025, revenue was up **38.4%**, but cost of revenue was up **40.4%** and sales and marketing was up **33.6%**. In Q3 2025, sales and marketing was also up **30.9%** and total operating expenses were up **28.0%**.

**Simple red flag**

* If revenue grows, but costs grow just as fast for **2 quarters in a row**
* Or revenue grows, but margins do not improve

## 2) Check the lending business separately

This is very important for Sea because its financial-services business, **Monee**, is now big enough to affect the whole company.

**What to check**

* Loan book growth
* Provision for credit losses
* 90+ day non-performing loan ratio
* Whether management is growing lending too fast

**Why this matters**

Fast loan growth can look great at first, but it can become risky if bad loans rise later.

For Sea, Monee's loans outstanding reached **$9.2 billion**, up **80.4% year over year**, while the **90+ day NPL ratio was 1.1%**, stable quarter over quarter. That sounds healthy. But at the same time, Sea's **provision for credit losses** rose **66.7% in Q4** and **76.3% in Q3**.

So the warning sign was not "bad loans already exploded." The warning sign was: **lending was growing very fast, and the company was already setting aside a lot more money for possible losses**.

**Simple lending checklist for Sea**

* Is loan growth slowing to a more normal level, or still running too hot?
* Is provision growth lower than loan growth, or is it staying very high?
* Is the NPL ratio stable, or starting to rise?
* Is lending profit growing without a big jump in credit cost?

**Simple red flag**

* Loans grow very fast, but provisions also keep rising sharply
* NPL ratio starts moving up
* Management talks more about growth than about risk control

## 3) Check whether Shopee's growth is efficient

Let's make this more generic. For an e-commerce business like Shopee, do not just ask, **"Is sales growth good?"** Ask, **"Is the company getting that growth in a healthy way?"**

**What to check**

* Gross orders growth
* GMV growth
* Marketplace revenue growth
* Logistics-related pressure
* Segment profit / EBITDA

**Why this matters**

Good e-commerce growth should come with healthy improvement in revenue and profit, not only from heavy discounts and shipping support.

In Sea's Q4 2025 results, Shopee's gross orders rose **30.5%**, GMV rose **28.6%**, and revenue rose **35.8%**, which is good. But Sea also said Shopee's value-added services revenue fell **7.5%** because of **higher revenue net-off against shipping subsidies**. Reuters also reported Sea was spending on discounts, offers, loyalty benefits, logistics, delivery speed, and fulfillment to stay competitive.

That means the growth was strong, but it was not fully "cheap" growth.

**Simple e-commerce efficiency checklist**

* Are orders and GMV growing?
* Is revenue growing faster than subsidies are eating into it?
* Is logistics cost under control?
* Is segment EBITDA improving, or is growth being bought at a high cost?

**Simple red flag**

* GMV is growing, but segment profit stays flat
* Shipping subsidies rise too much
* The company needs more discounts every quarter to keep gring

## 4) Check profit quality, not just revenue

**What to check**

* Did revenue beat expectations but profit miss?
* Is operating income improving?
* Is net income quality improving, or is growth getting harder to convert into profit?

**Why this matters**

A lot of investors make this mistake: they see strong revenue and assume the quarter was strong. But markets often care more about **profit quality**.

In Q3 2025, Sea reported revenue above estimates, but profit missed analyst expectations. In Q4 2025, Reuters again focused on rising expenses even though revenue beat estimates. This tells you that the market had started to worry more about **how profitable the growth really was**. ([Reuters][1])

**Simple red flag**

* Revenue looks good, but profit disappoints
* This happens more than once
* The stock stops reacting positively to revenue beats

---

## 5) Check forward guidance, not just the latest quarter

**What to check**

* Next year GMV growth target
* Profit target
* Whether management says it will keep spending aggressively

**Why this matters**

Stocks fall hard when investors realize the next year may be weaker than they expected.

In Sea's Q4 release, management guided for Shopee annual GMV growth of around **25%** in 2026, versus **26.8%** growth in 2025, while saying adjusted EBITDA should be **no lower than 2025 in absolute dollar terms**.

That is not terrible guidance, but it tells you something important: management still expects growth, yet not a big jump in profitability. That can disappoint investors if the stock price was expecting cleaner margin expansion.

**Simple red flag**

* Growth guidance slows
* Profit guidance does not improve much
* Management keeps saying competition is intense and spending remains needed

## Final simple takeaway

For Sea, the best checklist is:

1. **Is revenue growing faster than spending?**
2. **Is the lending business growing safely, or too aggressively?**
3. **Is Shopee growth efficient, or is it being bought through subsidies and heavy spending?**
4. **Is profit quality improving, or are revenue beats hiding weaker earnings quality?**
5. **Is next year's guidance strong enough to support the stock price?**
