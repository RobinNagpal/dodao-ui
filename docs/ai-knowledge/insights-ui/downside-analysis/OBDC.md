## OBDC (Blue Owl Capital Corporation) — Downside Analysis

OBDC hit ~$16.70 in late 2024 and fell to ~$11.70–$12.65 by early 2026 — roughly **28% down**. The business did not collapse. What happened was a combination of **NII compression from rate cuts, a failed controversial merger attempt, rising non-accruals, and shrinking dividend coverage** — all hitting a stock that yield-focused investors had priced for stability.

---

## 1) Net investment income (NII) trend vs base dividend

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

OBDC is a BDC — its entire value proposition is paying income from a floating-rate loan portfolio. When base rates fall, portfolio yields compress and NII drops. OBDC's weighted average yield fell from **11.1% (Q4 2024) to ~9.5–10% (Q4 2025)** — about 100–150 bps compression. Adjusted NII went from **$0.40/share (Q2 2025) to $0.36/share (Q3–Q4 2025)**, which no longer covered the **$0.37 base dividend**. BDC investors watch this ratio obsessively.

**Red flag**

- NII/share falls below the base dividend for 1 quarter = warning
- NII/share below base dividend for 2 consecutive quarters = expect a dividend cut or further selling

**Effect**

Yield investors — who are the core holder base for BDCs — sell immediately when dividend coverage breaks. The stock re-prices to a higher yield to compensate for perceived dividend risk, which means a lower share price. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: price drops → yield looks attractive → but new buyers won't step in until coverage is restored.

**Expected pricing impact**

- NII barely covers dividend (100–102% coverage): **3–5% downside**
- NII undershoot for 1 quarter (95–99% coverage): **5–10% downside**
- NII undershoot for 2+ quarters + supplemental eliminated: **10–18% downside**

---

## 2) Supplemental dividend trajectory

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

OBDC paid a **$0.05 supplemental** in early 2025, which shrank to **$0.01–$0.02** and then **$0.00 by Q4 2025**. Total annual dividends dropped from **$1.72 (2024) to $1.56 (2025)**, a 9.3% decline. The supplemental is the early warning — it gets cut before the base, so when it goes to zero, the market starts pricing in base-cut risk.

**Red flag**

- Supplemental cut by >50%: watch
- Supplemental goes to zero: serious warning
- Supplemental zero + NII below base dividend: expect base cut

**Effect**

The elimination of the supplemental is not just a small income reduction — it signals that the earnings buffer is gone. Income-fund managers who benchmark total yield start trimming positions, and the stock's premium-to-NAV compresses or flips to a discount.

**Expected pricing impact**

- Supplemental reduced but still positive: **2–5% downside**
- Supplemental eliminated entirely: **5–10% downside**
- Supplemental zero + base dividend at risk: **10–20% downside**

---

## 3) Non-accrual rate at fair value

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

OBDC's non-accruals tripled from **0.4% (Q4 2024) to 1.3% (Q3 2025)** before improving slightly to **1.1% (Q4 2025)**. For a BDC with an $18.6B portfolio across 232 companies, rising non-accruals directly reduce NII (loans stop paying interest) and erode NAV (mark-to-market losses). OBDC's first-lien focus should limit losses, but the direction of the trend matters more than the absolute level.

**Red flag**

- Non-accruals at FV rising >50% quarter-over-quarter: warning
- Non-accruals above 2% at fair value: serious concern
- Non-accruals rising while NII is already under pressure: compounding problem

**Effect**

Each non-accrual simultaneously hits NII (lost income) and NAV (unrealized depreciation). Investors model potential realized losses and discount the stock further. At cost basis, OBDC's non-accruals were **2.3%** in Q4 2025 — that gap between cost and fair value represents losses already taken but signals more could come.

**Expected pricing impact**

- Non-accruals rise to 1.0–1.5% FV (from a low base): **3–7% downside**
- Non-accruals rise to 1.5–2.5% FV: **7–15% downside**
- Non-accruals above 3% FV + realized losses accelerating: **15–25% downside**

---

## 4) NAV per share erosion

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

NAV fell from **$15.26 (Q4 2024) to $14.81 (Q4 2025)** — a $0.45 or **2.9% decline**. For a BDC trading near or below NAV, persistent NAV erosion tells investors the portfolio is deteriorating in value. The stock moved from trading near NAV to a **17–23% discount to NAV** — the widest among BDC peers. NAV is the floor valuation for a BDC; when it keeps dropping, the floor keeps moving down.

**Red flag**

- NAV declines 2+ consecutive quarters: warning
- NAV declines while stock trades at >15% discount to NAV: serious — market expects more writedowns
- NAV decline accelerates quarter-over-quarter: the portfolio has unresolved credit issues

**Effect**

Persistent NAV erosion makes it impossible for the stock to re-rate higher. Even if the dividend looks attractive, investors won't pay up if they expect NAV to keep falling. It also constrains the company's ability to raise equity (issuing shares below NAV is dilutive).

**Expected pricing impact**

- NAV down 1–2% over 2 quarters: **3–5% downside**
- NAV down 2–4% over a year with widening discount: **8–15% downside**
- NAV decline accelerating + discount >20%: **15–25% downside**

---

## 5) Controversial merger / corporate action risk

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

This was the biggest single catalyst for OBDC's sharp drop. In November 2025, OBDC announced a merger with OBDC II (a non-traded BDC) at **0.80x NAV** — meaning OBDC II investors would take a ~20% haircut. The Financial Times exposed this, and OBDC terminated the deal just 14 days later on November 19. The stock dropped from **~$15.06 to $11.70–$12.65** (17–22% decline). Six securities fraud class-action lawsuits were filed. OBDC II itself was facing a redemption crisis — $150M redeemed in 9 months of 2025, with Q3 alone hitting $60M (6% of NAV).

**Red flag**

- Any merger with a non-traded / illiquid sister fund at a discount to NAV: immediate red flag
- Management prioritizing fee growth (larger AUM) over shareholder value: structural concern
- Lawsuits filed after a corporate action: signals governance trust broken

**Effect**

This destroyed investor trust in Blue Owl's management alignment with public shareholders. Even though the deal was terminated, the damage was done — the market applied a permanent governance discount. Insider buying ($7.1M by co-CEOs and OBDC CEO at $15.06) went underwater, which did not help sentiment.

**Expected pricing impact**

- Merger announced with a related party at a discount: **8–15% downside**
- Merger exposed as unfavorable + terminated + lawsuits: **15–25% downside**
- Ongoing litigation + governance concerns linger: additional **3–8% overhang**

## 6) Portfolio yield compression rate

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

OBDC's portfolio is **predominantly floating-rate first-lien loans**. Each 25bp Fed rate cut compresses portfolio yield by roughly 25bp. Weighted average yield at FV dropped from **11.1% to ~9.5–10%** over 2025 — a direct function of the Fed cutting rates. Unlike banks that also have floating-rate deposits, BDCs have mostly fixed-rate liabilities (unsecured notes), so the squeeze is one-sided.

**Red flag**

- Portfolio yield compresses >50bps in a quarter: watch
- Yield compression outpaces any reduction in funding costs: NII squeeze accelerating
- Forward rate curve implies more cuts: the pain is not over

**Effect**

Yield compression is the most predictable but hardest-to-avoid risk for BDCs. Investors model future NII off the forward curve. When the market expects 100–200bps more in cuts, the stock prices in 2–4 quarters of declining NII before it actually happens.

**Expected pricing impact**

- 50–75bps annual yield compression (moderate rate cuts): **5–10% downside**
- 100–150bps compression (aggressive cuts): **10–18% downside**
- 150bps+ compression + fixed-rate liabilities locking in the squeeze: **15–25% downside**

---

## 7) Price-to-NAV discount vs peers

**Why this matters for OBDC specifically**

OBDC's discount widened to **17–23%** — the highest among major public BDCs. Peers like ARCC and MAIN trade at or near NAV. A widening discount relative to peers signals OBDC-specific concerns (governance, credit quality, management alignment) beyond sector-wide issues.

**Red flag**

- Discount widens to >10% while peers trade near NAV: OBDC-specific problem
- Discount widens after a corporate action: governance discount being priced in
- Discount persists despite aggressive buybacks: market does not trust the NAV marks

**Effect**

A wide P/NAV discount becomes self-reinforcing. It prevents equity issuance, limits growth, and attracts activist or short interest. The company tried to address this with $148M in buybacks and a new $300M program, plus a $400M portfolio sale at 99.7% of book — but the discount persisted.

**Expected pricing impact**

- Discount 5–10% (normal BDC range): **neutral**
- Discount 10–15%: **stock already reflects some concern, limited further downside from this alone**
- Discount 15–25% + governance overhang: **can persist for 6–12 months, trapping capital**

**Was the 28% decline justified?**

Mostly yes. The business fundamentals (NII, NAV, non-accruals) all deteriorated, and the OBDC II merger fiasco added a governance discount that would not have existed otherwise. The stock was not cheap at $16.70 for a BDC entering a rate-cut cycle. However, the $7.1M in insider buying, $148M in buybacks, Moody's upgrade to Baa2, and 8-analyst Buy consensus suggest the market may have overshot to the downside — the governance discount may fade if management rebuilds trust.
