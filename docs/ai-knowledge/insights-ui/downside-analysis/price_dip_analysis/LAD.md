## Lithia Motors (LAD) — Price Dip Analysis

**Current price:** ~$257 (March 2026)
**52-week high / ATH:** $360.56 / $400.70
**52-week low:** $239.78
**Already down:** ~36% from ATH, ~29% from 52-week high

LAD is the world's largest automotive retailer with $37.6B in FY2025 revenue (+4% YoY), operating 300+ dealership locations. Adjusted EPS $33.46 (+16% YoY). The company runs an aggressive acquisition strategy ($2–4B annually) with a $100B revenue target, and has built Driveway Finance Corporation (DFC) as a captive finance arm ($4.8B managed receivables, first profitable year in 2025 at $75M income).

**Business context:** LAD is a "buy and build" auto retail consolidator. Revenue split roughly: new vehicles (~50%), used vehicles (~30%), parts/service (~15%), finance/insurance (~5%). The company moved from $8.7B revenue in 2016 to $37.6B in 2025 (17.7% CAGR). $14.7B total debt (includes floorplan financing standard for auto retail) vs. only $205M cash. Tariffs on imported vehicles are the immediate threat — a 10% tariff could reduce US vehicle sales by 10%. Used car margins normalizing from pandemic peaks. Vehicle GPU (gross profit per unit) already down $258 YoY.

DFC penetration rate rises from 17.5% to 22%+ as captive finance becomes a meaningful profit center.

### Scenario 3: Tariff Shock + Auto Downturn — Earnings Compress (35% probability)

**What happens:** Tariffs escalate to 25% on imported vehicles (including Mexico/Canada). New car prices spike 10–15%, crushing demand. SAAR drops to 14.5–15M (recessionary levels). Consumers defer purchases. New vehicle units at LAD decline 15–20%. Used car demand initially rises but then softens as the broader economy weakens.

GPU compresses another $500–700/unit as dealers compete for scarce buyers. F&I revenue per unit also declines as lenders tighten credit standards. DFC loan losses increase as consumer credit deteriorates.

Revenue drops 5–10% (to $34–36B) as organic declines offset acquisitions. Adjusted EPS falls to $24–28. Interest coverage drops below 2.0x on the $14.7B debt load, triggering analyst concern about debt serviceability. Rating agencies put LAD on negative watch.

**Why 35% (most likely):** Tariff escalation is a live risk with multiple scenarios active. The auto industry is cyclical and already showing deceleration (SAAR declining, units down 8.3%). LAD's leverage means even a moderate earnings miss cascades into credit concerns. The 2015–16 and 2023 auto slowdowns showed 15–25% earnings compression for dealership groups.

### Scenario 4: Recession + Credit Tightening — Leverage Crisis (20% probability)

## Framework 3: Stress Test / Sensitivity Matrix

**Key variables:** FY2026 EPS × PE Multiple

**Formula:** Price = EPS × PE

### Price Matrix (EPS × PE)

| FY2026 EPS → | $20 (recession) | $26 (miss) | $33 (flat) | $38 (beat) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PE 5x** | $100 | $130 | $165 | $190 |
| **PE 7x** | $140 | $182 | $231 | $266 |
| **PE 9x** | $180 | $234 | **$297** | $342 |
| **PE 11x** | $220 | $286 | $363 | $418 |

**What the matrix reveals:**
- **Current price ($257) implies:** ~$33 EPS at 7.8x or ~$28 EPS at 9x. The market is pricing in either flat earnings at a discount multiple or a mild miss at current multiple.
- **Floor ($100):** Recession EPS ($20) at 5x PE. Requires a full recession + credit tightening. Matches the 2022 low territory.
- **Most sensitive variable:** EPS (earnings leverage from revenue × thin margins). A 20% revenue decline on 4.4% operating margins wipes out 40%+ of EPS. **This is a margin-leverage stock — thin margins amplify revenue changes.**

### Framework 3 Takeaway

LAD's stress test confirms the stock is **earnings-and-leverage driven.** The combination of thin margins (4.4% operating, 2.2% net) and massive debt ($14.7B) means small revenue changes create large earnings swings, and earnings changes drive both the EPS number and the multiple the market assigns. In a downturn, both compress simultaneously (lower EPS × lower PE = double whammy).
