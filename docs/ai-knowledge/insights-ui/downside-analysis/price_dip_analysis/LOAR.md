# Loar Holdings (LOAR) — Price Dip Analysis

*Time horizon: next 12 months (through Q1 2027). All prices and ratios as of March 2026.*

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

Loar Holdings is a niche aerospace and defense aftermarket parts manufacturer that went public in 2024. The company has completed 18 acquisitions since 2012, building sole-source positions on proprietary replacement parts across long-lifecycle aircraft platforms (20-30 year programs). Roughly 89% of revenue comes from proprietary parts protected by FAA/EASA certification barriers. Top revenue products are: commercial aerospace aftermarket parts (sole-source replacement components, approximately 60% of revenue), defense aftermarket parts (military aircraft/systems components, approximately 25% of revenue), and industrial components including the LMB Fans & Motors acquisition (approximately 15% of revenue and growing via M&A). FY2025 delivered revenue of $496M (+23% YoY), net income of $72M (+225%), and adjusted EBITDA margin of approximately 35%.

The stock has fallen approximately 35% from its 52-week high of $99.67 to around $65. The decline was driven by the market repricing high-multiple names: LOAR traded at 76x PE and 34.5x EV/EBITDA post-IPO — an unsustainable valuation for a company with ROIC of 5.2%, well below its estimated WACC of 8-10%. Fundamentals are fine (23% revenue growth), but the starting valuation was extreme. The broader selloff in expensive stocks accelerated the compression.

## Competitive Position & Moat

LOAR's moat is real but narrower than its closest peer TransDigm. Sole-source positions on 20-30 year aircraft platforms create switching costs and aftermarket pricing power — once a part is designed into a platform, the OEM effectively owns that replacement stream for decades. FAA/EASA certification requirements add a regulatory barrier that deters new entrants. 89% proprietary content is strong.

The top 2 direct competitors are TransDigm (TDG, the dominant aerospace aftermarket acquirer with 15%+ ROIC and decades of track record) and HEICO (HEI, which offers PMA — Parts Manufacturer Approval — alternatives at 30-50% discounts). HEICO's PMA expansion is the primary threat, offering FAA-approved alternatives that chip away at sole-source pricing power over time. TransDigm is less a direct competitor and more the valuation benchmark that LOAR aspires to match but currently does not warrant given its 5.2% ROIC vs. TDG's 15%+.

LOAR's moat (4/5 — sole-source positions + FAA/EASA regulatory barriers) is strong, but the acquisition-dependent model means the moat is partially "bought" rather than organically built, and each deal carries integration risk. Revenue at risk from HEICO PMA expansion is modest near-term (PMA penetration is still limited on many platforms) but growing.

## Stress Test / Sensitivity Matrix

**Base multiple justification:** 25x EV/EBITDA. Aerospace aftermarket peers trade at 20-30x EV/EBITDA: TransDigm 22-28x, HEICO 20-25x. LOAR's 34.5x current multiple reflects post-IPO premium that is compressing. 25x represents a reasonable mid-cycle premium for a sole-source aftermarket business. Current price of $65 implies approximately 28-30x forward EV/EBITDA on guidance.

**Valuation regime check:** LOAR only went public in 2024, so historical data is limited. Peer comparison: TDG averaged ~18-22x EV/EBITDA in 2016-2020 and ~22-28x in 2021-2025 (reflecting aerospace recovery premium). HEICO averaged ~20-25x. LOAR's post-IPO premium of 34.5x is well above peer range and likely to continue compressing. For the severe case, 15x EV/EBITDA (TDG's COVID-era trough) is appropriate.

**Key variables:** FY2026 Revenue x EBITDA Margin. Assumes 25x EV/EBITDA, net debt $600M, ~76M shares diluted.
