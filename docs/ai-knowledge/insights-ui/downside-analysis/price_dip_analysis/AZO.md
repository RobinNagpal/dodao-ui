# AutoZone (AZO) — Price Dip Analysis

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

AutoZone is the largest US auto parts retailer, operating 6,720+ stores and generating $19.6B in trailing revenue. Roughly 75% of domestic revenue comes from do-it-yourself (DIY) consumers buying parts for self-repair, while the fast-growing commercial/do-it-for-me (DIFM) segment — professional mechanics ordering parts — accounts for about 30% and has been growing near 10% annually. International operations in Mexico and Brazil contribute roughly 15% and are growing faster than the US business.

The stock has fallen approximately 24% from its all-time high of $4,388 to around $3,316. The primary driver is temporary margin compression from LIFO accounting charges tied to tariff-induced cost inflation on imported auto parts. In Q2 FY2026, gross margin fell 137 basis points year-over-year (138 bps from LIFO alone), and management guided for roughly $60M in LIFO charges in each of Q3 and Q4. While revenue growth remains healthy at 8.1% and EPS of $27.63 beat consensus, earnings declined 2.3% year-over-year, and domestic same-store sales decelerated from 4.8% to 3.4%. The market is pricing in continued margin headwinds until tariff costs either normalize or are fully passed through to consumers.

## How Much Further Can It Fall?

The probability-weighted expected price is approximately $3,050, implying roughly 8% further downside from current levels. This makes AZO one of the safer names in a sell-off — a defensive compounder with a temporary, accounting-driven headwind rather than a structural business problem.

The bull case depends on tariff costs being absorbed and LIFO charges reversing in FY2027, with commercial DIFM sustaining double-digit growth and DIY same-store sales reaccelerating to 4-5%. The severe bear case requires a full recession where consumers defer vehicle repairs entirely, pushing same-store sales negative and compressing margins by 300+ basis points — a scenario where even AZO's counter-cyclical characteristics (consumers repair rather than replace aging cars) would be tested.

## Competitive Position & Moat

AutoZone holds the #1 position in US auto parts retail with approximately 25% market share by revenue. The sub-industry is consolidated among three major players: AZO ($19.6B), O'Reilly Auto Parts (#2, ~22% share), and Advance Auto Parts (#3, declining). The moat rests on AZO's hub-and-spoke distribution network that enables 30-minute delivery to commercial customers — a speed advantage Amazon cannot replicate for the DIFM segment, which requires same-day parts availability for mechanics with cars on lifts.

The structural tailwind is the aging US vehicle fleet (average age 12.6 years), which drives increasing demand for replacement parts. AZO has returned $55B+ in cumulative buybacks, demonstrating extraordinary capital allocation with a 42.5% return on invested capital. The EV threat is real but 15-20 years away from materially impacting parts demand. Amazon poses the most credible long-term competitive threat to the DIY segment (estimated 3-5% revenue at risk over time), but cannot match the speed or expertise needed for commercial customers. Overall, revenue at risk from competition is minimal in the near term.

## What to Watch

The key variable is whether LIFO charges normalize as tariff uncertainty resolves. If charges persist beyond FY2026, the base case shifts toward bear. Separately, watch the DIY same-store sales trend — deceleration from 4.8% to 3.4% is manageable, but a move below 2% would signal consumer weakness beyond just price elasticity. Commercial DIFM growth sustaining near double digits would confirm the structural thesis remains intact.

## Precedent Drawdowns

AZO has historically been one of the most defensive retailers. The current -24% drawdown matches the 2022 correction (~-20%). In the 2008-2009 recession, AZO actually outperformed as consumers chose to repair rather than replace vehicles. The LIFO-driven margin issue is temporary and will reverse when tariff-induced inflation abates. Precedent-implied further downside: -5 to -15%, limited because the franchise is exceptional and the headwind is accounting-driven.
