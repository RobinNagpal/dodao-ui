# AutoZone (AZO) — Price Dip Analysis

*Time horizon: next 12 months (through Q1 2027). All prices and ratios as of March 2026.*

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

AutoZone is the largest US auto parts retailer, operating 6,720+ stores across the US, Mexico, and Brazil, generating $19.6B in trailing revenue. DIY retail (do-it-yourself consumers buying parts for self-repair) accounts for roughly 75% of domestic revenue. The commercial/DIFM segment (do-it-for-me — professional mechanics ordering parts for customer repairs) contributes approximately 30% of revenue and has been growing near 10% annually. International operations in Mexico and Brazil contribute roughly 15% of revenue and are growing faster than the US business.

The stock has fallen approximately 24% from its all-time high of $4,388 to around $3,316. The primary driver is temporary margin compression from LIFO accounting charges tied to tariff-induced cost inflation on imported auto parts. In Q2 FY2026, gross margin fell 137 basis points year-over-year (138 bps from LIFO alone), and management guided for roughly $60M in LIFO charges in each of Q3 and Q4. Revenue growth remains healthy at 8.1% and EPS of $27.63 beat consensus, but earnings declined 2.3% year-over-year. Domestic same-store sales decelerated from 4.8% to 3.4%. The market is pricing in continued margin headwinds until tariff costs either normalize or are fully passed through.

## How Much Further Can It Fall?

The probability-weighted expected price is approximately $3,050, implying roughly 8% further downside from $3,316. This makes AZO one of the safer names in the coverage universe — a defensive compounder with a temporary, accounting-driven headwind rather than a structural business problem.

## Competitive Position & Moat

AutoZone holds the #1 position in US auto parts retail with approximately 25% market share by revenue. The sub-industry is consolidated among three major players: AZO ($19.6B), O'Reilly Auto Parts (#2, ~22% share), and Advance Auto Parts (#3, declining). The moat rests on AZO's hub-and-spoke distribution network that enables 30-minute delivery to commercial customers — a speed advantage that Amazon cannot replicate for the DIFM segment, which requires same-day parts availability for mechanics with cars on lifts. AZO has returned $55B+ in cumulative buybacks, demonstrating extraordinary capital allocation with a 42.5% ROIC.

The top 2 direct competitors are O'Reilly Auto Parts (ORLY) and Amazon. O'Reilly is the strongest direct competitor with a similar hub-and-spoke model and comparable store economics, but the two have largely coexisted through geographic discipline. Amazon poses the most credible long-term threat to the DIY segment — price-sensitive consumers can wait for 1-2 day shipping — but cannot match the speed or expertise needed for commercial customers. The structural tailwind is the aging US vehicle fleet (average age 12.6 years), which drives increasing demand for replacement parts. Competitive displacement risk is minimal due to AZO's distribution moat and the physical-proximity requirement of the DIFM business. Revenue at risk from Amazon is estimated at 3-5% of DIY revenue ($500-900M) over a 5-10 year horizon, but near-term impact is negligible.

The EV threat is real but 15-20 years away from materially impacting parts demand. AZO's moat (4/5 — distribution speed + switching costs for commercial customers) is strong: #1 market share is durable, and the physical distribution advantage is nearly impossible to replicate.

## Stress Test / Sensitivity Matrix

**Base multiple justification:** 22x PE. AZO's 5-year median PE is approximately 20-22x. Auto parts retail peers (O'Reilly 24-26x, Advance Auto Parts 12-15x) bracket this range. Current price of $3,316 implies ~$143 EPS at 23x.

**Valuation regime check:** AZO's PE averaged ~16x in 2016-2020 and expanded to ~20-22x+ in 2021-2025. The expansion is partially justified by the DIFM growth engine (commercial business accelerating), aging US fleet tailwind, and proven buyback-driven EPS compounding. However, some of the expansion reflects the pandemic-era "quality compounder" premium. For the severe case, 18x PE (closer to the pre-expansion average) is more honest than 20x. At 18x, the severe floor drops to $2,070 vs. $2,200 at 19x.
