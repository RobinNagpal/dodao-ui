## Downside Analysis — Frameworks, Methodology & Reference Guide

A consolidated reference covering the 7 downside analysis frameworks, their comparison, known shortcomings, financial ratio sensitivity reference, and precedent drawdown methodology used across all 31 stock analyses.

---

# Part 1: Seven Downside Analysis Frameworks

## 1. Scenario-Based Ratio Modeling ⭐ (Primary Framework)

**What it is:** Build 3–5 business-specific scenarios (bull, base, mild bear, severe bear) grounded in **product/service-level narratives**, estimate how key ratios change in each, translate to % price impact, assign probabilities, and compute a probability-weighted expected price.

**Core Principle:** Every scenario must tell a real story about what happens to the company's top 2–3 revenue-generating products or services. "Revenue declines 15%" is an output, not a scenario. "TikTok Shop captures 8% of Indonesian e-commerce GMV, forcing Shopee into a subsidy war" is a scenario.

**Process:** Product/service story → Revenue/margin impact → Valuation change → Price impact

**Steps:**
1. **Identify the top 2–3 revenue-generating products or services.** Be specific — not "e-commerce segment" but "Shopee marketplace ($127B GMV), SeaMoney lending ($9.2B loan book), Garena/Free Fire ($1.9B bookings)"
2. **For each product/service, understand:** Who buys it? Who competes for those customers? What could go right/wrong for THIS specific product?
3. Identify 8–12 key ratios (valuation, profitability, leverage, liquidity, yield)
4. **Build 4 scenarios, each with 2–3 sub-scenarios.** Each sub-scenario describes a different combination of product-level outcomes that leads to a similar overall result. Example: SE's bear case could be "Shopee loses share but lending holds" OR "Shopee holds but lending NPLs spike" — both lead to similar price, but different monitoring triggers.
5. **Headline test each scenario:** Could you write a news headline for it? If not, it's too vague.
6. Estimate ratio changes per scenario
7. Apply price sensitivity per unit of ratio change (see Part 4)
8. Sum overlapping impacts (typically 50–65% of raw sum)
9. Assign probability ranges and compute weighted expected price
10. **Identify monitoring triggers:** Each sub-scenario should point to specific data you can track quarterly (competitor store count, market share report, NPL ratio, FDA decision, same-store sales in specific markets)

**Strengths:** Holistic, business-specific, probability-weighted, easy to update, communicable, **monitorable** (each sub-scenario has tracking triggers).
**Weaknesses:** Probability assignments are subjective, price sensitivity is approximate, overlapping effects hard to isolate. Requires understanding the company's actual products, not just its financials.
**Ease:** Moderate | **Reliability:** Moderate–High | **Best for:** Individual stock deep-dives | **Horizon:** 6–18 months

## 2. Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP) Bear Case

**What it is:** Value each business segment independently using trough multiples, sum them, subtract net debt. Reveals hidden value or overvaluation in specific segments.

**Best for:** Multi-segment companies, conglomerates, companies in transition.
**Ease:** Moderate | **Reliability:** Moderate–High

---

## 3. Stress Test / Sensitivity Matrix ⭐ (Secondary Framework)

**What it is:** Vary 2–3 key value drivers across a range and calculate the stock price at each intersection. Produces a visual matrix showing floors, current implied assumptions, and sensitivity.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the 2–3 most impactful variables (revenue/EBITDA, margin, multiple)
2. Define realistic ranges for each
3. Build a 2D matrix: each cell = stock price at that combination
4. Identify current price position, floor, and break-even points

**Key insight from our analyses:** The matrix reveals *which variable matters more.* For margin-risk stocks (ZBRA, DUOL), margin sensitivity is 1.4–8x greater than revenue sensitivity. For leverage stocks (EQPT, PRKS), the EBITDA-to-equity amplification is the dominant factor.

**Strengths:** Visual, objective, reveals relative sensitivity, easy to communicate.
**Weaknesses:** Limited to 2–3 variables, doesn't assign probabilities, can produce implausible corners.
**Ease:** Easy–Moderate | **Reliability:** Moderate–High | **Best for:** Quick visual analysis, presentations | **Horizon:** 6–18 months

---

## 4. Precedent Drawdown + Catalyst Mapping ⭐ (Tertiary Framework)

**What it is:** Study how the stock (or peers) behaved in past similar downturns. Map current catalysts to historical parallels. Estimate whether the current decline is "normal" or "extreme."

**Steps:**
1. Find 3–5 historical episodes with similar risk catalysts
2. Measure peak-to-trough drawdown in each
3. Assess current vs. historical severity (worse? better? similar?)
4. Adjust for differences (starting valuation, leverage, competitive position)
5. Estimate plausible drawdown range

**Key insight from our analyses:** This framework was the **critical tiebreaker** for ranking stocks. It revealed:
- DUOL had *overshot* Netflix/Meta precedents (−83% vs. −76/−77%) → less downside than F1/F3 suggested
- ZBRA had *not matched* its own 2021–23 precedent (−43% vs. −68%) → more room to fall
- ARE was *matching* its own GFC trough (−75%) → near historical floor

**Strengths:** Grounded in actual market behavior, captures psychology/overreaction patterns, identifies "priced in" vs. "room to fall."
**Weaknesses:** History doesn't repeat exactly, small sample sizes, requires subjective judgment on analogue quality.
**Ease:** Easy–Moderate | **Reliability:** Moderate | **Best for:** Cyclical/macro-sensitive stocks | **Horizon:** 3–18 months

---

## 5. Integrated Fundamental Scorecard

**What it is:** Score a company across 15–25 metrics, compare composite to historical and peer averages. Deteriorating score = further downside; score near historical lows = bottom may be near.

**Best for:** Ranking/comparing multiple stocks in a portfolio.
**Ease:** Moderate | **Reliability:** Moderate–High

---

## 6. Reverse DCF + Implied Expectations Gap

**What it is:** Reverse-engineer a DCF to determine what growth/margin/ROIC assumptions are baked into the current price. Compare to realistic expectations — the gap is the estimated downside.

**Best for:** Stocks priced for perfection, investors with DCF skills.
**Ease:** Moderate–Difficult | **Reliability:** Moderate–High

---

## 7. Multi-Factor Risk Decomposition

**What it is:** Decompose total risk into independent factors (macro, sector, company-specific, market structure). Estimate probability and impact of each. Adjust for correlations.

**Best for:** Portfolio risk management, sophisticated investors.
**Ease:** Difficult | **Reliability:** High (when done well)

# Part 2: Framework Comparison

| Framework | Holistic? | Ease | Reliability | Best For | Horizon |
|-----------|-----------|------|-------------|----------|---------|
| **1. Scenario Ratio Modeling** | Yes | Moderate | Moderate–High | Individual deep-dives | 6–18 mo |
| **2. SOTP Bear Case** | Yes | Moderate | Moderate–High | Multi-segment companies | 1–3 yr |
| **3. Stress Test Matrix** | Partial (2–3 vars) | Easy | Moderate–High | Visual analysis | 6–18 mo |
| **4. Precedent Drawdown** | Yes | Easy | Moderate | Cyclical stocks | 3–18 mo |
| **5. Fundamental Scorecard** | Yes (15–25 metrics) | Moderate | Moderate–High | Portfolio ranking | Ongoing |
| **6. Reverse DCF** | Yes | Hard | Moderate–High | Growth stocks | 1–3 yr |
| **7. Risk Decomposition** | Yes | Hard | High | Portfolio management | 3–12 mo |

### Easiest to Hardest
1. Stress Test Matrix → 2. Precedent Drawdown → 3. Scenario Ratio Modeling → 4. Scorecard → 5. SOTP → 6. Reverse DCF → 7. Risk Decomposition

### Most to Least Reliable
1. Scenario Ratio Modeling → 2. Reverse DCF → 3. Scorecard → 4. SOTP → 5. Stress Test → 6. Risk Decomposition → 7. Precedent Drawdown (standalone)

### Recommendation
- **Individual investors:** Use Framework 1 (primary) + Framework 3 (supplementary) + Framework 4 (tiebreaker). This is the approach used across all 31 analyses.
- **Portfolio comparison:** Use Framework 5 (scorecard) to rank, then deep-dive the worst scores.
- **DCF-skilled investors:** Start with Framework 6 (reverse DCF) → Framework 1 (scenarios).
- **Portfolio managers:** Framework 7 (risk decomposition) + Framework 4 (precedent context).

# Part 3: Known Shortcomings & Improvements

### Shortcoming 1: Probability Assignment Is Subjective
Specific probability numbers (e.g., "35% probability") create false precision. We cannot reliably predict macro conditions or industry dynamics.

**Improvement:** Use probability *ranges* (e.g., "30–40% probability") instead of point estimates. Acknowledge the ranges explicitly.

### Shortcoming 2: No Causal Chain from Business to Price
Scenarios jump from business description to price impact without tracing the chain: business change → financial metric → valuation metric → price.

**Improvement:** Each scenario should follow: **Business trend** (volume, demand, pricing) → **Financial translation** (revenue, margin, EPS impact) → **Valuation change** (multiple expansion/compression) → **Price impact**. This makes the reasoning auditable.

### Shortcoming 3: Starting Valuation Not Accounted For
Some stocks are overvalued when analyzed, meaning even a small negative business change causes outsized price decline. Others are undervalued, providing a cushion.

**Improvement:** Always note whether the starting valuation is above/below historical averages and peer comparables. Adjust scenario price impacts accordingly.

### Shortcoming 4: No Time Dimension
Scenarios don't specify *when* the outcome is expected. A 20% decline over 3 months feels different from 20% over 18 months.

**Improvement:** Add time horizons to each scenario (e.g., "likely within 2–3 quarters if tariffs escalate").

### Shortcoming 5: Price Sensitivity Lacks Historical Backing
The per-unit price sensitivity estimates (e.g., "+1x EV/EBITDA ≈ 7%") are reasonable approximations but lack specific historical validation for each stock.

**Improvement:** Where possible, reference the stock's own historical price reactions to ratio changes (e.g., "In Q3 2023, ZBRA's 2pp margin miss caused a 12% decline").

### Shortcoming 6: Overlap Between Ratios Is Estimated, Not Calculated
We estimate that overlapping effects account for 35–50% of the raw sum, but this discount factor is approximate.

**Improvement:** Use regression or factor analysis on historical data to estimate the independent contribution of each ratio change. For practical purposes, the 50–65% discount remains a reasonable rule of thumb.

# Part 5: Precedent Drawdown Methodology

## How We Apply Precedent Analysis

1. **Find 3–5 comparable episodes** — same stock's history + peer stocks in similar situations
2. **Measure peak-to-trough** for each episode
3. **Map catalyst severity** — is the current catalyst more or less severe than the precedent?
4. **Adjust for differences** — leverage, valuation starting point, competitive dynamics, balance sheet
5. **Determine if the stock has "overshot" or has "room to match"** — the critical ranking question

## Risk Archetypes Discovered Across 31 Stocks

| Risk Type | Stocks | What Drives Downside | How It Resolves |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Narrative / Sentiment** | DUOL, FRSH | Growth deceleration, AI disruption fears | Time — market reprices to fundamentals |
| **Commodity Cycle** | VITL | Egg prices, avian flu, consumer spending | Supply/demand cycle — 6–12 months |
| **Tariff / Margin** | ELF, ZBRA, KTB, LAD, AZO | Tariff policy, input costs, pricing power | Policy clarity |
| **Competitive** | ZG, SE | New competitors, market share threats | 12–24 months for shifts to show in data |
| **Integration** | CELH, ESAB | Acquisition synergies vs. friction | 12–18 months for proof points |
| **Multiple Compression** | BROS, LOAR, CRH, PGR, FSV | Growth deceleration, mean reversion | Gradual — multiples compress as growth normalizes |
| **AI Existential** | UPWK, FRSH | AI replacing the core product/platform | 2–3 years — structural question |
| **Leverage** | EQPT, ZBRA, PRKS, LAD | Debt amplifies all outcomes | Earnings delivery — each quarter matters |
| **Geopolitical** | HSAI | Pentagon blacklist, US-China policy | Binary — policy decisions move stock 30%+ overnight |
| **Credit / Structural Oversupply** | ARE, OBDC, AGM | Credit quality, interest rates, supply/demand | 2–3 years |
| **Survival / Dilution** | INVZ | Cash burn, OEM ramp, capital raises | 5–6 quarters — binary outcome |
| **Safety Binary** | SLNO | FDA post-marketing data | 6–12 months — safety data determines everything |
| **FCF / Investment Year** | ACM, PCTY | FCF cuts, investment spending, growth deceleration | 12–18 months for investment payoff proof |
| **FX / Commodity** | TGLS | Currency, raw material costs | Macro-driven — 6–12 months |
| **Insurance Cycle** | PGR | Combined ratio normalization | 2–3 year soft market cycle |

## Key Finding: The "Known and Priced" Pattern

Stocks with the **least further downside** share a common characteristic: **the bad news is already known, priced, and reflected in depressed multiples.** (DUOL, VITL, FRSH, ARE, ELF, AZO, PCTY)

Stocks with the **most further downside** share the opposite: **the negative catalyst is unresolved or structural.** (ZBRA, UPWK, EQPT, PRKS, INVZ, SE)

The precedent analysis is what distinguishes these two groups — it reveals whether a stock has already "overshot" historical patterns or has "room to match" them.

# Part 6: Competitive Landscape as a Downside Dimension

**See `competitive_landscape.md` for the full 31-stock competitive analysis.**

The three quantitative frameworks (Scenarios, Stress Test, Precedent) analyze financial metrics but can miss **competitive dynamics** that drive stock declines. Sub-industry competitive position matters because:

1. **Competitive moat determines pricing power** — which directly affects whether margin compression scenarios play out. A monopoly (AGM) can't lose pricing power to competitors. A commodity marketplace (UPWK) can.

2. **The bear case for many stocks IS a competitive scenario** — FRSH's biggest risk isn't growth deceleration, it's AI-native startups replacing helpdesk software. PRKS's biggest risk isn't a recession, it's Epic Universe opening next door.

3. **Moat strength score should adjust the probability weights in Framework 1.** A stock with moat strength 0/5 should have higher probability assigned to bear/severe-bear scenarios than one with 5/5.

### How Moat Strength Maps to Framework 1 Probabilities

*All ratings: 5/5 = strongest moat, 0/5 = no moat.*

| Moat Strength | Suggested Bear Case Probability Adjustment |
|---------------|-------------------------------------------|
| 5/5 (monopoly/irreplaceable) | Bear scenarios get −10pp probability (more likely to muddle through) |
| 4/5 (strong switching costs) | No adjustment (standard weights) |
| 3/5 (moderate, replicable) | Bear scenarios get +5pp probability |
| 2/5 (low switching costs, disruptable) | Bear scenarios get +10pp probability |
| 1/5 (barely any moat) | Bear + severe bear should be 55%+ combined |
| 0/5 (existential competitive risk) | Bear + severe bear should be 60%+ combined |

### Step 4F: Quantifying Competitive Revenue Risk

Moat scoring (0-5) tells you WHETHER competition matters. But for stocks scoring 0-2, you must also quantify HOW MUCH revenue is at risk. The process:

1. Identify the top 2 most direct competitors (not largest — most likely to take THIS company's customers)
2. Estimate their growth trajectory and geographic/product overlap
3. Calculate: Competitor takes X pp of share → $Y revenue loss → at current multiple = Z% stock decline
4. Verify with evidence: is it already showing up in quarterly results?

**Revenue Risk Severity Scale:**
- **<3%** = Noise (PCTY, BROS today)
- **3–8%** = Monitor (ELF, SE, PRKS)
- **8–15%** = Core bear scenario (FRSH, SLNO)
- **15–25%** = Thesis-level risk (DUOL, CELH, HSAI)
- **>25%** = Existential (INVZ growth trajectory)

See `final_framework_for_downside.md` Step 4F and `competitive_landscape.md` for full analysis of all 12 at-risk stocks.

### Three Categories of Competitive Risk

**Competition INCREASES downside** (financial models underestimate): DUOL, UPWK, FRSH (AI structural), SE (TikTok Shop), BROS (7 Brew replication), PRKS (Epic Universe), CELH (Monster/Ghost)

**Competition is IRRELEVANT to downside** (all risk is macro/financial): AGM, CRH (monopoly positions), AZO (counter-cyclical), LOAR, ZBRA (locked-in enterprise)

**Competition IS the entire bear case**: INVZ (Hesai + camera-only), SLNO (safety → competitor entry), PRKS (Epic Universe direct impact)
