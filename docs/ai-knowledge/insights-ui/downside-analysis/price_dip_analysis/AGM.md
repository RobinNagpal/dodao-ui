# Federal Agricultural Mortgage / Farmer Mac (AGM) — Price Dip Analysis

*Time horizon: next 12 months (through Q1 2027). All prices and ratios as of March 2026.*

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

Farmer Mac (AGM) is a government-sponsored enterprise (GSE) that operates the only national secondary market for US agricultural real estate mortgages — effectively the "Fannie Mae of farming." Farm & Ranch mortgage loans (secondary market for agricultural real estate) are the largest segment. Rural utility cooperative loans (USDA-backed financing for rural electric and telephone cooperatives) are the second key product. Broadband and infrastructure loans represent a newer, fast-growing segment. The company posted record FY2025 results: net effective spread income hit $383M, outstanding business volume grew 13% to $33.4B, and core EPS rose 7% to $16.66.

The stock dropped roughly 24% from its all-time high of $210.78 to around $158 because the agricultural credit cycle is turning. Provisions for loan losses spiked to $32.9M in FY2025, nonaccrual loans climbed 41% year-over-year to 1.42% of total loans, and the farm economy outlook is darkening — only 52% of farm borrowers are expected to be profitable in 2025, falling below 50% in 2026 (the lowest since 2020). Nearly 70% of agricultural lenders say they are "very worried" about grain profitability.

## How Much Further Can It Fall?

The probability-weighted expected price is approximately $145, implying roughly 8% further downside from $158. The key variable is not competition (Farmer Mac has none) but how deep the agricultural credit deterioration goes. If provisions normalize, the stock is materially cheap. If farm bankruptcies spike, earnings could be cut in half.

## Competitive Position & Moat

Farmer Mac's moat is structural and absolute. The company holds a Congressional charter making it the sole GSE authorized to provide a secondary market for US farm mortgages. No competitor can replicate this without a literal act of Congress. This charter delivers a 50-100 basis point funding cost advantage over any hypothetical rival, creating an insurmountable pricing edge. In practical terms, there are zero direct competitors. Competitive displacement risk is minimal due to the regulatory monopoly moat.

The real risk is not competition but agricultural credit quality. With 52% of farm borrowers profitable (and falling), substandard agricultural finance loans at 3.52% (above the 3.3% watch level), and grain-sector stress threatening to spread into livestock and dairy, the credit cycle is the single variable that matters. The company's 13.3% Tier 1 capital ratio and 277 days of liquidity provide a substantial buffer, but if nonaccruals breach 2.5-3%, even the monopoly cannot prevent significant earnings erosion.

AGM's moat (5/5 — regulatory monopoly) is the strongest possible: Congressional charter with no competitors. The risk is entirely financial/credit, not competitive.

The dividend ($6.00/year, 3.45% yield) is a key sentiment signal — any cut or freeze would likely trigger another leg down.

## Stress Test / Sensitivity Matrix

**Base multiple justification:** 10x PE. AGM's 5-year average PE is approximately 10-11x, reflecting its GSE status (limited growth ceiling but reliable earnings). Current price of $158 implies $17 EPS at 9.3x — slightly below the base multiple, reflecting the credit concern discount.

**Valuation regime check:** AGM's PE averaged ~8-9x in 2016-2020 and expanded to ~10-12x in 2021-2025 as the company delivered record results and the broadband/infrastructure segment provided a growth narrative. The expansion is partially justified by improved business diversification and volume growth. For the severe case, 7x PE (below the pre-expansion average) reflects agricultural crisis multiples.
