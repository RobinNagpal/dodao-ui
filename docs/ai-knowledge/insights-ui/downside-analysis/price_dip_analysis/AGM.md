# Federal Agricultural Mortgage / Farmer Mac (AGM) — Price Dip Analysis

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

Farmer Mac (AGM) is a government-sponsored enterprise (GSE) that operates the only national secondary market for US agricultural real estate mortgages. Think of it as the "Fannie Mae of farming" — local lenders originate farm loans, then sell them to Farmer Mac, which packages and guarantees them. The company also finances rural utility cooperatives (USDA-backed) and a growing broadband/infrastructure lending segment. With a Congressional charter granting it monopoly status, Farmer Mac posted record results in FY2025: net effective spread income hit $383M, outstanding business volume grew 13% to $33.4B, and core EPS rose 7% to $16.66.

The stock dropped roughly 24% from its all-time high of $210.78 because the agricultural credit cycle is turning. Provisions for loan losses spiked to $32.9M in FY2025, nonaccrual loans climbed 41% year-over-year to 1.42% of total loans, and the farm economy outlook is darkening — only 52% of farm borrowers are expected to be profitable in 2025, falling below 50% in 2026 (the lowest since 2020). Nearly 70% of agricultural lenders say they are "very worried" about grain profitability. The franchise itself is performing at record levels, but the market is pricing in the risk that worsening farm credit eats into those earnings.

## How Much Further Can It Fall?

At ~$158, the stock trades at roughly 9.5x core earnings with a 3.45% dividend yield. The probability-weighted expected price across all scenarios is approximately $145, implying about 8% further downside from current levels. The key variable is not competition (Farmer Mac has none) but how deep the agricultural credit deterioration goes. If provisions normalize, the stock is materially cheap. If farm bankruptcies spike, earnings could be cut in half.

The bull and base cases together carry 55% probability and point to flat-to-significant upside. However, the bear and severe cases (45% combined) suggest meaningful downside remains. The GSE's implicit government backing limits bankruptcy risk to equity holders but does not protect against earnings compression. During COVID, AGM fell 54% before recovering in 12 months — the current 24% decline is milder, but the catalyst this time is a slow-moving credit cycle rather than a sudden liquidity shock, meaning recovery could take longer.

## Competitive Position & Moat

Farmer Mac's moat is not just strong — it is structural and absolute. The company holds a Congressional charter making it the sole GSE authorized to provide a secondary market for US farm mortgages. No competitor can replicate this without a literal act of Congress. This charter delivers a 50–100 basis point funding cost advantage over any hypothetical rival, creating an insurmountable pricing edge in agricultural lending markets. In practical terms, there are zero direct competitors.

The real risk to Farmer Mac is not competitive displacement but agricultural credit quality. With 52% of farm borrowers profitable (and falling), substandard agricultural finance loans at 3.52% (above the 3.3% watch level), and grain-sector stress threatening to spread into livestock and dairy, the credit cycle is the single variable that matters. The company's 13.3% Tier 1 capital ratio and 277 days of liquidity provide a substantial buffer, but if nonaccruals breach 2.5–3%, even the monopoly cannot prevent significant earnings erosion.

## What to Watch

The single most important metric is the trajectory of nonaccrual loans and provisions. If nonaccruals stabilize around 1.3–1.5% and provisions hold at $30–35M, the base case holds and the stock is fairly valued. If nonaccruals break above 2% with provisions exceeding $50M, the bear case is in play — watch for management guidance changes on these numbers in quarterly earnings calls.

Beyond credit metrics, monitor farm profitability surveys (USDA and agricultural lender sentiment reports), trade policy developments affecting ag exports (particularly tariff actions on grain), and whether credit stress remains isolated to grain or spreads to livestock and dairy. The dividend ($6.00/year, 3.45% yield) is a key sentiment signal — any cut or freeze would likely trigger another leg down.

## Complete Scenario Breakdowns

### Scenario 1: Ag Credit Stabilizes — Growth Continues (20% probability)
Farm profitability stabilizes. Provisions normalize to $15–20M. Nonaccruals peak and decline. Business volume continues toward $50B target. Core EPS grows to $18+. Price target: $195–220 (+23 to +39%).

### Scenario 3: Ag Credit Deterioration Accelerates (30% probability)
