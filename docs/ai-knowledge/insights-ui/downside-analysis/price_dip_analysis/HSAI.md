# Hesai Group (HSAI) — Price Dip Analysis

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

Hesai Group is the world's #1 automotive LiDAR company by revenue and shipments, holding approximately 33% global market share, and is the only profitable pure-play LiDAR company globally. ADAS LiDAR sensors for passenger vehicles account for roughly 60% of revenue, robotaxi/autonomous LiDAR roughly 25%, and non-automotive applications (robotics, smart cities) roughly 15%. Key products include the AT128 (128-channel ADAS), FT120 (solid-state), and ET25 (next-gen long-range). Over 80% of revenue comes from Chinese OEMs including BYD, Li Auto, and Geely, with 90%+ automated manufacturing.

HSAI posted FY2025 revenue of RMB 3.03B ($433M), up 46% YoY, and achieved its first full-year GAAP profitability (net income $62.3M). The company shipped 1.6M units in 2025 (tripled YoY) and guided 2-3M unit shipments for 2026. It dual-listed on NASDAQ and Hong Kong (raising $531M from the HK listing in September 2025). The primary competitor, Luminar, filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in December 2025.

The stock has fallen approximately 34% from its 52-week/all-time high of $30.85 to around $20.22 (from an all-time low of $3.55 in August 2024). Despite strong earnings, the stock dropped 14% in a single day on March 25 — the market is pricing geopolitical risk, not fundamentals. Key headwinds include the Pentagon "Chinese Military Companies" blacklist since October 2024 (lawsuit ongoing), US-China tariff escalation (47% tariffs), camera-only L3 autonomous driving from Tesla threatening the LiDAR TAM, and persistent China ADR risk discount. US revenue is less than 10%.

## How Much Further Can It Fall?

The probability-weighted expected price is approximately $17, implying roughly 16% further downside from $20.22. HSAI is fundamentally a geopolitical/multiple-driven stock, not a fundamental one — the business is performing well (46% growth, first-ever profitability, tripling shipments), but the stock price is controlled by what valuation multiple the market assigns, and that multiple is driven by the Pentagon blacklist, US-China tensions, and LiDAR TAM narrative.

The bull case requires L3 autonomy regulation to accelerate in China and Europe, driving multi-LiDAR configurations, with revenue hitting $700M+ and the Thailand factory unlocking non-Chinese OEM wins. The severe bear case envisions a US ban on Chinese-made LiDAR in Western markets combined with Tesla's pure-vision FSD achieving L3 certification in China, shrinking HSAI's addressable market to China and non-Western markets only. The net cash of $5.87/share (29% of current price) provides a hard floor — HSAI will not go bankrupt — but equity value above net cash could compress dramatically, with a true floor around $7-8.

## Competitive Position & Moat

HSAI holds the dominant #1 position in global automotive LiDAR with 33%+ market share and is the only profitable pure-play in the space. Its primary competitor Luminar filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in December 2025, and other Western LiDAR peers (Ouster, Innoviz) have seen -90%+ declines on average. The company has 90%+ automated manufacturing, proven scale (1.6M units shipped), and a fortress balance sheet ($917M net cash, 29% of market cap). A Thailand factory under construction for 2026-2027 could bypass US tariffs and access non-Chinese OEMs.

However, the geopolitical ban risk is existential for Western market expansion — the Pentagon blacklist and potential US ban on Chinese LiDAR in autonomous vehicles (similar to the proposed ban on Chinese software in connected vehicles) could permanently cap HSAI's addressable market. Camera-only L3 approaches from Tesla threaten the core LiDAR TAM thesis. RoboSense is a credible competitor aggressively undercutting on pricing. LiDAR ASP erosion is structural, following a consumer electronics cost curve. HSAI's -34% decline is remarkably mild compared to LiDAR peer average crashes of -90% and China ADR average crashes of -80%, reflecting that the business is fundamentally better (profitable, growing, dual-listed) than during its own -81% crash from IPO to all-time low.
