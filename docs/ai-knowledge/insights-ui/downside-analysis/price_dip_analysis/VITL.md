# Section 1: Analysis

## What the Company Does and Why It Dropped

Vital Farms is the #1 pasture-raised egg brand in the United States and the #2 egg brand overall, partnering with 600+ family farms built over 15+ years to produce third-party certified pasture-raised shell eggs (~95% of revenue, $720M+ in FY2025). The remaining ~5% comes from butter and other dairy, which is growing. The company has zero debt, $113M in cash, ROIC of 30%, and posted FY2025 revenue of $759.4M (+25.3% YoY) with $114M adjusted EBITDA (15% margin).

**Why the stock collapsed 75%:** Four forces converged — (1) egg price deflation as conventional supply recovered from avian flu, widening the premium gap back to $4+/dozen, (2) Q4 EPS miss ($0.35 vs $0.39 expected) and FY2026 guidance below consensus ($910M midpoint vs $938M), (3) founder Matt O'Hayer retired as executive chairperson in February 2026, and (4) scanner data weakness in January-February 2026 suggesting consumers are trading down from premium eggs. Simultaneously, VITL is mid-build on its $140-150M Vital Crossroads processing facility in Indiana, making FCF deeply negative despite a profitable P&L.

**Top revenue products:**
1. Pasture-raised shell eggs (~95% of revenue, $720M+ in FY2025, #1 US brand)
2. Butter and other dairy (~5% of revenue, growing)
3. Vital Crossroads facility — not revenue yet, but $140-150M capex investment for future capacity toward the $2B 2030 revenue target

## How Much Further Can It Fall

At $13.40, the stock trades at ~9.3x PE, 0.81x PS, 0.66x EV/Sales, and 4.4x EV/EBITDA. These are distressed multiples for a company growing 20%+ with zero debt. The probability-weighted expected price is ~$12.30, representing approximately 8% further downside.

The key insight from stress testing: VITL is a pure re-rating bet. Moving revenue or margins within the realistic range only swings the price +/-$2-3 at a fixed multiple. But moving from the current 6x EV/EBITDA to a fair 10x on the same fundamentals swings the price +75%. The multiple is doing almost all the damage, and further fundamental deterioration has limited incremental impact because the multiple is already at distressed levels.

The true floor is ~$7.90 (revenue miss + margin collapse + 4x distressed multiple), but reaching that requires genuine financial stress — difficult for a zero-debt company. Even at the worst realistic fundamental outcome ($830M revenue, 10% EBITDA margin) at the current 6x multiple, the stock only falls to $11.60 — just 13% below current.

## Competitive Position and Moat

VITL's moat rests on its 600+ family farm partnership network, built over 15+ years, with third-party certifications (Certified Humane, USDA Organic where applicable). This supply chain is extremely difficult to replicate — it took VITL over a decade to build, and competitors cannot shortcut the relationship-building and certification processes. Current household penetration is only ~2%, implying massive remaining runway.

**Competitive landscape:** Cal-Maine Foods (largest US egg producer) is the primary threat, beginning to launch pasture-raised product lines. Pete and Gerry's and Happy Egg compete in adjacent premium segments. Revenue at risk from competitive entry is minimal near-term because the farm partnerships and certifications create a structural barrier. The bigger risk is consumer trade-down to conventional eggs rather than competitive share loss within premium.

## What to Watch

The next 3-6 months will determine whether VITL's drop was an overreaction to a cyclical egg market or the beginning of a structural de-rating. Scanner data trends in Q1-Q2 2026 are the single most important variable — sequential improvement from weak January-February data validates Scenario 1/2, while continued weakness points to Scenario 3. Cash burn rate is the financial risk to monitor: with $113M cash and projected -$90M+ FCF in FY2026, the runway is ~12-15 months without slowing capex or adding a credit facility.

## Precedent Context

VITL's -75% drawdown is extreme for a growing, profitable, zero-debt company. This is commodity-cycle driven — the stock surged in 2024-2025 as avian flu decimated conventional supply (driving consumers to premium brands), then crashed as supply recovered. Unlike leverage-driven declines (where financial distress can accelerate), VITL's zero-debt balance sheet puts a floor under the stock. At 4.4x EV/EBITDA, the company is already priced at distressed levels typically seen in declining businesses, not 20%+ growers.

The capex cycle ($140-150M for Vital Crossroads in FY2026, up from $82M in FY2025) creates a timing mismatch: the P&L shows a profitable, growing business, but FCF is deeply negative (-$48M in FY2025, projected -$90M+ in FY2026). The market is treating VITL as if the capex will become a stranded asset, but the company's track record of building farm partnerships and the facility's strategic positioning for Midwest expansion suggest otherwise.
