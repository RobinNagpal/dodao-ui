## Vital Farms (VITL) — Price Dip Analysis

**Current price:** ~$13.40 (March 31, 2026)
**52-week high:** $53.13
**52-week low:** $12.80
**Already down:** ~75% from high, ~56% YTD in 2026

Vital Farms posted FY 2025 revenue of $759.4M (+25.3% YoY), net income of $66.3M, diluted EPS of $1.44, and adjusted EBITDA of $114M (15% margin). The company guided FY 2026 revenue of $900–920M (+19–21%), EBITDA of $105–115M, and capex of $140–150M (for the Vital Crossroads facility in Indiana). The stock collapsed after a Q4 EPS miss ($0.35 vs $0.39 expected), lower-than-expected 2026 guidance ($910M midpoint vs $938M consensus), founder retirement, and emerging egg price deflation.

**Business context:** VITL is the leading pasture-raised egg brand in the U.S. with 600+ family farms. Revenue is ~95% shell eggs. The company has zero debt, $113M cash, and a $100M buyback authorization. The stock surged in 2024–2025 as avian flu decimated conventional egg supply (driving consumers to premium brands), then crashed as supply recovered and egg prices deflated. Capex is spiking to $140–150M in 2026 (from $82M in 2025) for the new processing facility, making FCF deeply negative despite a profitable P&L.

## The Vital Farms Puzzle: Great Business Metrics, Terrible Stock

VITL has an unusual profile:
- **Strengths:** Zero debt, 30% ROIC, 10.5x inventory turnover, 1.73x asset turnover, 2.16 current ratio, 15% EBITDA margin. The operational metrics are excellent.
- **Weaknesses:** Negative FCF (-$48M in FY 2025) due to massive capex cycle. Negative buyback yield (SBC dilution). The stock is priced like the business is broken, but the P&L says otherwise.

**Why the disconnect:** The market sees four things — (1) egg price deflation destroying the demand tailwind, (2) $140–150M in capex creating negative FCF for 2+ years, (3) founder departure signaling potential strategic drift, and (4) scanner data weakness suggesting consumers are trading down from premium eggs as prices normalize.

## Downside Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Egg Market Normalizes Gradually — Guidance Met, Stock Stabilizes (20% probability)

**What happens:** Egg prices settle into a "new normal" — conventional eggs come down from their avian flu highs but stabilize at levels 15–20% above pre-2022 averages due to permanently reduced flock sizes and ongoing HPAI risk. This keeps the price gap between conventional and pasture-raised eggs manageable (~$3–4 premium per dozen instead of the compressed $1–2 gap seen during peak eggflation).

VITL's retail execution issues (out-of-stocks, inconsistent shelf replenishment) get resolved by Q2 2026 as the company works with retailers on store-level staffing and planogram compliance. Scanner data improves from the weak January–February numbers as spring brings seasonal egg demand uplift.

The Vital Crossroads facility in Seymour, Indiana progresses on schedule. Capex of $140–150M is absorbed without needing external financing thanks to the $113M cash position plus operating cash flow (~$50–60M). The facility positions VITL for the $2B 2030 revenue target by adding capacity in the Midwest, closer to growing retail demand.

Revenue comes in at the midpoint of guidance (~$910M). EBITDA of $110M at 12% margin (lower than 2025's 15% due to promotional spending and breaker market exposure). The stock re-rates modestly as the market realizes the 9x PE and 4.4x EV/EBITDA are too cheap for a 20% grower.

**Why only 20%:** The market is extremely skeptical right now. Scanner data weakness in January–February 2026 is a hard data point that's difficult to dismiss. The out-of-stock issues suggest VITL may be losing shelf space to competitors during the critical egg supply recovery period. For this scenario to play out, VITL needs scanner data to visibly improve by Q2 — and that's competing against a flood of conventional egg supply coming back online.

### Scenario 2: Egg Deflation Bites but Business Adapts — Mild Miss (30% probability)

**What happens:** Egg prices deflate more than expected as conventional supply recovers fully from avian flu. The industry enters an oversupply phase in H1 2026 — cold storage facilities hit capacity (already happening), and excess eggs get diverted to the breaker market (liquid eggs, food service) at steep discounts.

VITL's premium positioning provides some insulation, but not immunity. The price gap between conventional eggs ($2.50/dozen) and VITL pasture-raised ($6–7/dozen) widens back to $4+, causing some consumer trade-down. Same-store velocity dips 3–5% as price-sensitive shoppers who "discovered" VITL during eggflation switch back to cheaper options.

Revenue comes in at $870–890M (below the $900M low end of guidance). The miss is modest — VITL still grows ~15% YoY, but misses the 19–21% guided range. EBITDA comes in at $95–100M as promotional spending increases to defend shelf space against aggressive conventional egg promotions.

The Vital Crossroads capex continues, keeping FCF deeply negative (~-$70M to -$90M). The $113M cash position gets drawn down to $40–50M by year-end. The $100M buyback gets paused after only $10–15M in repurchases because the board decides to preserve cash for the facility build.

Analysts cut 2027 estimates. Morgan Stanley maintains its $24 target but Jefferies (already cutting) goes lower to $16–18. The stock drifts down 10–20% but doesn't collapse because the underlying business is still profitable and growing.

**Why 30%:** Egg price deflation is the consensus bear case and it's already playing out. Stifel explicitly called this "the bear thesis for VITL." The breaker market exposure and promotional activity headwinds are already in the Q1 2026 outlook. This scenario is essentially "the current trajectory continues" without getting dramatically worse.

### Scenario 3: Consumer Trade-Down Accelerates + Capex Trap (35% probability)

**What happens:** The U.S. consumer weakens in H2 2026. Tariff-driven inflation on imported goods (not eggs, but everything else — electronics, clothing, auto parts) squeezes household budgets. Consumers cut discretionary food spending, and premium eggs are one of the first casualties. The calculus becomes stark: "Why pay $7 for pasture-raised eggs when conventional eggs are $2.50 again?"

VITL experiences its first negative same-store velocity quarter since its growth phase began. Scanner data shows not just slowing growth but actual unit volume declines of 5–8% at existing retailers. New distribution gains (new stores, new regions) partially offset this, but organic growth at existing stores goes negative.

Revenue comes in at $830–860M (vs. $900–920M guided). EBITDA drops to $80–90M as gross margin compresses from 37.6% toward 33–34% — a combination of lower volume leverage, higher promotional spending to defend velocity, and breaker market losses on excess supply.

The worst part: VITL is mid-construction on Vital Crossroads, spending $140–150M on a facility designed for the $2B 2030 revenue target. If demand growth is slowing, this capex starts to look like overbuilding. The market draws the Peloton comparison — investing heavily in capacity just as the demand tailwind reverses.

Cash position drops below $30M by Q3 2026. FCF is -$100M+ for the year. VITL may need to slow construction, raise a small revolving credit facility, or cut the buyback entirely. The $2B 2030 target starts looking aspirational rather than achievable — requiring ~26% CAGR from a decelerating base.

Founder Matt O'Hayer retired as executive chairperson in February 2026. The market questions whether CEO Russell Diez-Canseco (now also chairman) can navigate a downturn without the founder's strategic judgment. The "executive sells 20k shares amid social media backlash" headline from February adds to the governance concern narrative.

**Why 35% (most likely negative scenario):** Consumer trade-down from premium food brands during economic stress is well-documented. VITL's growth was turbocharged by avian flu — an external event that made premium eggs relatively cheap (small price gap vs. conventional). With that gap widening back to $4+, the natural demand level for $7 eggs is being tested for the first time. Meanwhile, VITL is locked into a $140–150M capex commitment that can't be easily paused mid-construction. This creates a classic "growth company capex trap" — spending to build capacity while demand decelerates.

### Scenario 4: Avian Flu Resurges — Supply Shock Rescues the Bull Case (15% probability)

**What happens:** HPAI (Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza) resurges in fall 2026, as it has cyclically since 2022. Conventional egg producers, who restocked flocks aggressively in H1 2026, get hit again. 20–30 million hens are culled. Conventional egg prices spike back to $5–6/dozen.

VITL's distributed farm network (600+ small farms, pasture-raised with lower flock density) is structurally more resilient to avian flu — none of its farms have had positive HPAI cases. When conventional eggs become expensive and scarce again, VITL's premium narrows. The "eggflation trade" returns.

Scanner data rockets back. Same-store velocity jumps 15–20%. Revenue accelerates to the high end of guidance or above. EBITDA margins expand because volume leverage is the most powerful margin driver in food processing — selling more eggs through the same facilities at stable pricing.

The Vital Crossroads capex suddenly looks prescient — VITL is building capacity ahead of renewed demand. The $2B 2030 target regains credibility.

**Why 15%:** Avian flu has been cyclical and unpredictable — there's no way to rule out another outbreak. Each of the last 4 years has seen significant HPAI events. VITL's structural advantage (distributed small farms vs. concentrated industrial operations) is real and proven. However, the market won't price this in until it happens, and betting on a pandemic as a catalyst is inherently low-probability for any given quarter. This is a "tail event that helps" rather than a base case.

## What Makes VITL Different from ZBRA and DUOL

| Factor | ZBRA | DUOL | VITL |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Balance sheet | Leveraged | Fortress | Strong (zero debt, but cash being spent on capex) |
| Why it's down | Macro/tariff headwinds | Growth deceleration | Commodity cycle reversal |
| Nature of risk | Financial (leverage) | Narrative (multiple) | Cyclical (egg prices) |
| Recovery catalyst | Tariff clarity + earnings beat | DAU inflection + AI payoff | Egg supply shock or consumer confidence recovery |
| Valuation floor | Earnings power | Brand + cash | Tangible assets + farm network |
| Wild card | Credit downgrade | AI disruption by Google/OpenAI | Avian flu outbreak |

**Key insight:** VITL's situation is the most commodity-driven of the three. The stock rose on a supply shock (avian flu) and is falling as supply normalizes. Unlike ZBRA (leverage risk) or DUOL (narrative risk), VITL's fate is tied to something largely outside management's control — the biological cycle of avian influenza and the elasticity of consumer demand for premium eggs at a $4+ price gap. The capex cycle adds a timing risk: VITL is building for $2B in 2030 revenue, but if the path to $2B isn't linear (demand dips before recovering), the company burns through its cash cushion mid-construction.

## Key Catalysts and Timing (VITL)

- **Q1 2026 earnings (May/June):** Scanner data and same-store velocity trends. A sequential improvement from the weak Jan–Feb data = Scenario 1/2. Continued weakness = Scenario 3.
- **Conventional egg price trajectory (monthly USDA data):** Track wholesale egg prices. Below $2/dozen conventional = maximum premium gap pressure on VITL.
- **HPAI outbreak monitoring (USDA APHIS reports):** Any new outbreak affecting >5M birds = potential Scenario 4 catalyst.
- **Cash burn rate (quarterly):** With $113M cash and -$48M FCF in FY2025, accelerating to -$90M+ in FY2026, the cash runway is ~12–15 months without slowing capex. Watch for credit facility announcements.
- **Vital Crossroads construction milestones:** On schedule = long-term bull case intact. Delays or pauses = market reads as demand uncertainty.
- **Buyback execution:** $100M authorized. If VITL buys back stock aggressively at $13, that's 7–8% of market cap — very accretive. If paused to preserve cash, that's a bearish signal.
