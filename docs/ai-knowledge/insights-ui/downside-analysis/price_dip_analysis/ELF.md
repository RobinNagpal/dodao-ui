# e.l.f. Beauty (ELF) — Price Dip Analysis

# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

e.l.f. Beauty is the #1 value cosmetics brand in the U.S., known for disrupting prestige beauty at drugstore prices. The company sells color cosmetics (face, lip, eye — roughly 70% of revenue), skin care products (roughly 20%), and the acquired Naturium brand (roughly 10%). Products are primarily manufactured in China (approximately 75% of sourcing) and sold through mass retail channels including Target, Walmart, and Amazon, with accelerating international expansion into the UK, Canada, and Italy.

ELF posted FY2025 revenue of $1.3B (+28.3% YoY), net income of $112M, EPS of $1.92, and gross margin of approximately 71%. The company guided FY2026 net sales growth of 22-23%, with second-half growth of 31-33% and Adj EBITDA of $323-326M. Note: ELF has a fiscal year ending in March, so FY2026 covers through March 2027.

The stock has fallen approximately 61% from its 52-week high of $150.99 to around $59.35. The crash resulted from a confluence of factors: a 55% China tariff rate hitting 75% of production (approximately $50M annualized cost impact), gross margin compression of 165 basis points, a PEG ratio of 2.89 too rich for tariff headwinds, and a broader consumer discretionary selloff. With approximately 58M shares outstanding, market cap is roughly $3.4B and net debt is approximately $400M.

## How Much Further Can It Fall?

The probability-weighted expected price is approximately $52, implying roughly 12% further downside from $59. ELF's downside is fundamentally tariff-margin driven — with 75% China sourcing concentration, tariff changes flow almost directly to EBITDA margin. The stress test shows that at $1.55B revenue, moving EBITDA margin from 17% to 23% at 16x EV/EBITDA swings the stock from $68 to $103 (+51%), while revenue sensitivity is smaller. Margin matters more than revenue for ELF, and margin is the variable most directly hit by tariffs.

The bull case requires tariff rates to stabilize at 55%, price increases to stick without slowing volume, and Naturium to scale — producing a recovery to $75-85. The severe bear case envisions a U.S. recession combined with supply chain disruption: revenue goes flat to +5%, gross margin drops below 65%, and Debt/EBITDA spikes above 5x, pushing the stock to $25-32. ELF's 2017-2018 crash of -73% during a strong economy shows the stock can fall much further if the growth narrative breaks entirely.

## Competitive Position & Moat

ELF holds the fastest-growing position in mass cosmetics, offering a prestige-at-drugstore-prices value proposition that undercuts competitors by 50-70%. This positioning has driven 28%+ revenue growth and meaningful market share gains across color cosmetics, skin care, and the Naturium acquisition. The brand has demonstrated genuine pricing power historically, and its digital-first approach resonates with younger consumers.

However, beauty is a fickle category where consumer preferences shift rapidly. Ultra-cheap imports and private-label alternatives represent a structural threat, particularly if tariff-driven price increases erode ELF's core value positioning — at some point, a $14 lipstick loses its "value" appeal when it was $8 two years ago. The 75% China manufacturing concentration is the most acute vulnerability among major beauty companies, and supply chain diversification takes 18-24 months minimum. International expansion (UK, Canada, Italy) provides a margin of safety as a less tariff-exposed growth vector.

Precedent analysis shows ELF's current -61% decline matches Estee Lauder's 2022-2025 decline (-65%) almost exactly — both beauty companies hit by China-related headwinds. ELF's own 2017-2018 crash (-73% from $34 to $9) demonstrates the stock can overshoot to the downside. Ulta Beauty's -50% decline in 2019 with a 12-month recovery is the optimistic template, but Ulta had no China tariff exposure. Precedent-implied further downside is -5 to -20% from current levels, with the Estee Lauder "no recovery" scenario as the risk case.

## What to Watch

The key variable is whether tariff costs can be passed through without destroying ELF's value positioning. Q1 FY2026 earnings (July/August 2026) provide the first read on tariff impact and pricing power — a gross margin miss below 68% validates the bear scenario. Supply chain diversification progress is critical: management has discussed shifting production out of China, but the 18-24 month minimum timeline means ELF is stuck with China production for at least 4-6 quarters regardless of policy changes. International expansion metrics offer the best upside signal.

## Detailed Scenario Breakdowns

### Scenario 1: Tariffs Stabilize + Market Share Gains Continue (15% probability)

Tariff rates hold at current 55% levels and do not escalate further. ELF successfully passes through price increases to consumers, and the price hikes stick because the brand's value proposition still undercuts prestige competitors by 50-70%. Revenue grows 22-23% as guided. Gross margin recovers toward 70%+ as supply chain adjustments and pricing actions take effect. International expansion (UK, Canada, Italy) contributes incremental growth. Adj EBITDA hits $323-326M guidance. Market rewards execution with EV/EBITDA of 18-20x.

Only 20% probability because the market has already priced in significant tariff damage. For the stock to recover, ELF needs to demonstrate that price increases do not slow volume growth — and with 75% China sourcing, any tariff escalation instantly re-prices the stock downward.
