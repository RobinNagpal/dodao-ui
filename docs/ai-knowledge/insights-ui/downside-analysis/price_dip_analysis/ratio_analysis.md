## Financial Ratios — Reference Guide

Ranges, unit definitions, and expected price sensitivity for each ratio.

**How to read this document:**
- **Good / Okay / Bad** ranges are general benchmarks for mid-cap industrial-tech companies (adjust for sector).
- **1 Unit** is the smallest meaningful move for that ratio — the increment that typically triggers analyst revisions or investor re-rating.
- **Price sensitivity** is the approximate % stock price change expected when the ratio moves by 1 unit, all else equal. These are directional estimates based on historical market behavior, not exact formulas.

---

## Valuation Ratios

### PE Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 15x |
| Okay | 15–25x |
| Bad | > 25x |

**1 Unit = 1x PE multiple** (e.g., PE moves from 20x to 21x)

**Price sensitivity:** +1x PE on the same earnings = **~4–5% price increase.** (Math: 1/20 = 5%. At PE 20, each 1x move is ~5% of price. At PE 30, each 1x move is ~3.3%.)

**Direction:** Higher PE = higher price (if earnings are constant). But rising PE without earnings growth is bad — it means the stock is getting more expensive without justification.

---

### Forward PE

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 12x |
| Okay | 12–20x |
| Bad | > 20x |

**1 Unit = 1x forward PE multiple**

**Price sensitivity:** Same math as trailing PE — **~4–5% per 1x at PE ~20.** Forward PE matters more because it reflects expectations. A 1x drop in forward PE (from analyst estimate revisions) typically causes **5–7% price decline** because it signals deteriorating outlook.

---

### PEG Ratio (PE-to-Growth)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 1.0 |
| Okay | 1.0–2.0 |
| Bad | > 2.0 |

**1 Unit = 0.1 PEG** (e.g., PEG moves from 0.8 to 0.9)

**Price sensitivity:** A 0.1 increase in PEG = **~2–4% price decline** (or less upside potential). PEG is a derived ratio — it moves when PE changes or growth estimates change. A PEG crossing above 1.0 (from 0.95 to 1.05) is a psychological threshold that can trigger **5–8% re-rating.**

---

### PS Ratio (Price-to-Sales)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 1.5x |
| Okay | 1.5–3.0x |
| Bad | > 3.0x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x PS** (e.g., PS moves from 2.0 to 2.5)

**Price sensitivity:** A 0.5x increase in PS on the same revenue = **~15–20% price increase** (direct math). PS moves slowly for stable-revenue companies. A 0.5x compression (from multiple re-rating) typically reflects **15–20% price decline** and usually happens during earnings misses or margin deterioration.

---

### PB Ratio (Price-to-Book)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 2.0x |
| Okay | 2.0–4.0x |
| Bad | > 4.0x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x PB** (e.g., PB moves from 2.5 to 3.0)

**Price sensitivity:** A 0.5x move in PB = **~15–20% price change.** PB is most relevant for asset-heavy or financial companies. For tech companies with large goodwill, PB is less meaningful and moves have smaller behavioral impact (~8–12%).

---

### P/TBV Ratio (Price-to-Tangible Book Value)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 3.0x |
| Okay | 3.0–6.0x |
| Bad | > 6.0x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x P/TBV**

**Price sensitivity:** Similar to PB — **~10–15% per 0.5x.** Less watched by the market for tech companies. Meaningful only when tangible book is a significant portion of total assets.

---

### P/FCF Ratio (Price-to-Free Cash Flow)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 15x |
| Okay | 15–25x |
| Bad | > 25x |

**1 Unit = 1x P/FCF** (e.g., P/FCF moves from 12x to 13x)

**Price sensitivity:** +1x P/FCF at the 12–15x level = **~6–8% price increase.** This is one of the most important ratios for value investors. A 1x compression in P/FCF (from FCF growth) is viewed very positively. Crossing below 10x often triggers value-buyer interest and **5–10% upside.**

---

### P/OCF Ratio (Price-to-Operating Cash Flow)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 12x |
| Okay | 12–20x |
| Bad | > 20x |

**1 Unit = 1x P/OCF**

**Price sensitivity:** Similar to P/FCF — **~5–7% per 1x.** Slightly less sensitive than P/FCF because OCF doesn't account for capex. Investors prefer P/FCF, so P/OCF moves have ~80% of the price impact.

## Enterprise Value Ratios

### EV/Sales

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 2.0x |
| Okay | 2.0–4.0x |
| Bad | > 4.0x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x EV/Sales** (e.g., moves from 2.0 to 2.5)

**Price sensitivity:** A 0.5x move in EV/Sales = **~15–20% price change.** This ratio is most useful for comparing companies with different capital structures. A compression below 2.0x often signals deep value.

---

### EV/EBITDA

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 10x |
| Okay | 10–15x |
| Bad | > 15x |

**1 Unit = 1x EV/EBITDA** (e.g., moves from 13x to 14x)

**Price sensitivity:** +1x EV/EBITDA at the 12–15x level = **~7–8% price change.** This is the most widely used valuation ratio for M&A and leveraged buyouts. Crossing above 15x is often a sell signal for value investors. Dropping below 10x frequently attracts private equity interest.

---

### EV/EBIT

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 12x |
| Okay | 12–18x |
| Bad | > 18x |

**1 Unit = 1x EV/EBIT**

**Price sensitivity:** +1x EV/EBIT = **~5–7% price change.** Slightly less followed than EV/EBITDA but more conservative (includes D&A). A 1x move matters more at lower absolute levels (e.g., 10x to 11x is ~10% vs. 20x to 21x is ~5%).

---

### EV/FCF

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 12x |
| Okay | 12–20x |
| Bad | > 20x |

**1 Unit = 1x EV/FCF**

**Price sensitivity:** +1x EV/FCF = **~5–7% price change.** Combines the capital-structure neutrality of EV with the cash-generation focus of FCF. Highly regarded by institutional investors. Compression below 12x is a strong buy signal for cash-flow-focused funds.

## Profitability Ratios

### ROE (Return on Equity)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 15% |
| Okay | 10–15% |
| Bad | < 10% |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point (pp)** (e.g., ROE moves from 12% to 13%)

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp ROE = **~2–3% price increase.** ROE improvements signal better capital efficiency. Crossing above 15% is a positive inflection that can drive **5–8% re-rating** as the company enters "quality" screens. A sustained drop below 10% can trigger **10–15% decline** over a quarter.

---

### ROA (Return on Assets)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 8% |
| Okay | 5–8% |
| Bad | < 5% |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point**

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp ROA = **~1.5–2.5% price change.** Less directly watched than ROE but important for asset-heavy companies. ROA below 5% for multiple quarters signals the asset base isn't productive, which can lead to **8–12% decline** as investors question acquisition strategy.

---

### ROIC (Return on Invested Capital)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 12% |
| Okay | 8–12% |
| Bad | < 8% (below cost of capital) |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point**

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp ROIC = **~2–4% price change.** ROIC is the gold standard for measuring value creation. The critical threshold is ROIC vs. WACC (~8–10%). Crossing above WACC from below can trigger **8–12% re-rating.** Falling below WACC signals value destruction and can cause **10–15% decline.**

---

### ROCE (Return on Capital Employed)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 15% |
| Okay | 10–15% |
| Bad | < 10% |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point**

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp ROCE = **~2–3% price change.** Similar to ROIC in importance. European and UK investors weight ROCE more heavily. Crossing above 15% signals a high-quality business and can attract long-term institutional capital.

## Leverage Ratios

### Debt/Equity

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 0.5 |
| Okay | 0.5–1.0 |
| Bad | > 1.0 |

**1 Unit = 0.1x D/E** (e.g., moves from 0.7 to 0.8)

**Price sensitivity:** +0.1x D/E = **~1–2% price decline.** Leverage changes are slow-moving but sentiment-impactful at thresholds. Crossing above 1.0x can trigger **3–5% decline** as credit risk concerns emerge. Dropping below 0.5x is viewed positively but has muted price impact (~1–2% upside).

---

### Debt/EBITDA

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 2.0x |
| Okay | 2.0–3.0x |
| Bad | > 3.0x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x Debt/EBITDA** (e.g., moves from 3.0 to 3.5)

**Price sensitivity:** +0.5x Debt/EBITDA = **~3–5% price decline.** This is the primary leverage metric watched by credit analysts and rating agencies. Crossing above 3.0x can trigger credit downgrades, which cause **5–10% equity price decline.** Dropping below 2.0x is a strong positive signal.

---

### Debt/FCF

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 2.0x |
| Okay | 2.0–3.5x |
| Bad | > 3.5x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x Debt/FCF**

**Price sensitivity:** +0.5x Debt/FCF = **~2–4% price decline.** Tells you how many years of FCF it takes to pay off debt. Crossing above 4.0x raises concerns about debt sustainability and can cause **5–8% decline.**

---

### Net Debt/Equity

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 0.5 |
| Okay | 0.5–1.0 |
| Bad | > 1.0 |

**1 Unit = 0.1x**

**Price sensitivity:** Same as Debt/Equity — **~1–2% per 0.1x.** Net debt ratios are preferred over gross debt ratios because they account for cash. If net debt is significantly lower than gross debt, it signals strong liquidity and reduces sensitivity.

---

### Net Debt/EBITDA

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 1.5x |
| Okay | 1.5–3.0x |
| Bad | > 3.0x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x**

**Price sensitivity:** +0.5x Net Debt/EBITDA = **~3–5% price decline.** The most watched leverage metric overall. Investment-grade thresholds are typically below 2.5x. Crossing above 3.0x can trigger rating agency reviews and **5–10% decline.**

---

### Net Debt/FCF

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | < 2.0x |
| Okay | 2.0–3.5x |
| Bad | > 3.5x |

**1 Unit = 0.5x**

**Price sensitivity:** +0.5x = **~2–4% price decline.** Similar dynamics to Debt/FCF but adjusted for cash holdings.

---

## Efficiency Ratios

### Asset Turnover

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 1.0x |
| Okay | 0.5–1.0x |
| Bad | < 0.5x |

**1 Unit = 0.1x asset turnover** (e.g., moves from 0.66 to 0.76)

**Price sensitivity:** +0.1x asset turnover = **~2–3% price increase.** Asset turnover improves when revenue grows faster than assets. It is a slow-moving ratio and rarely causes immediate price reactions, but a sustained trend (improving for 3+ quarters) signals operational improvement and can drive **5–10% re-rating** over time.

---

### Inventory Turnover

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 6.0x |
| Okay | 3.0–6.0x |
| Bad | < 3.0x |

**1 Unit = 1.0x inventory turnover** (e.g., moves from 4.0 to 5.0)

**Price sensitivity:** +1x inventory turnover = **~2–4% price increase.** Faster turnover means less capital tied up in inventory and lower obsolescence risk. For hardware companies, a drop below 3.0x can signal demand weakness and cause **5–8% decline.** A rise above 6.0x signals strong demand.

---

## Liquidity Ratios

### Quick Ratio

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 1.0 |
| Okay | 0.7–1.0 |
| Bad | < 0.7 |

**1 Unit = 0.1 quick ratio** (e.g., moves from 0.5 to 0.6)

**Price sensitivity:** +0.1 quick ratio = **~1–2% price change.** Liquidity ratios are slow-burn metrics — they don't cause daily price moves but matter during stress. A quick ratio below 0.5 combined with rising debt can trigger **5–10% decline** on credit downgrade fears. Improvement from 0.5 to 1.0 over several quarters is a quiet positive.

---

### Current Ratio

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 1.5 |
| Okay | 1.0–1.5 |
| Bad | < 1.0 |

**1 Unit = 0.1 current ratio** (e.g., moves from 0.97 to 1.07)

**Price sensitivity:** +0.1 current ratio = **~1–2% price change.** The critical threshold is 1.0. Crossing below 1.0 (current liabilities exceed current assets) is a red flag that can trigger **3–5% decline.** Crossing above 1.5 signals comfort and attracts conservative investors.

---

## Yield Ratios

### Earnings Yield (inverse of PE)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 7% |
| Okay | 4–7% |
| Bad | < 4% |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point** (e.g., earnings yield moves from 4% to 5%)

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp earnings yield (from earnings growth, not price decline) = **~3–5% price increase** as investors bid up the stock to compress yield back down. When earnings yield falls below the risk-free rate (~4.5% for 10Y Treasury), stocks become less attractive on a yield basis, which can cause **5–8% rotation** into bonds.

---

### FCF Yield

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 7% |
| Okay | 4–7% |
| Bad | < 4% |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point**

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp FCF yield = **~4–6% price increase.** FCF yield is the most actionable yield metric for institutional investors. Crossing above 8% puts a stock on deep-value screens. Crossing above 10% often triggers activist interest or buyout speculation, which can drive **10–15% upside.**

---

### Buyback Yield

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 3% |
| Okay | 1–3% |
| Bad | < 1% (or dilutive — negative) |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point**

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp buyback yield = **~1.5–3% price increase.** Buybacks reduce share count, directly increasing EPS. A buyback yield above 3% is material and signals management confidence. Dilution (negative buyback yield) from stock-based compensation can cause **2–4% headwind** to price annually.

---

### Total Shareholder Return (Buyback + Dividend Yield)

| Range | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Good | > 5% |
| Okay | 2–5% |
| Bad | < 2% |

**1 Unit = 1 percentage point**

**Price sensitivity:** +1pp total return = **~2–3% price increase.** Total shareholder return combines dividends and buybacks. For non-dividend payers like ZBRA, this equals buyback yield. Crossing above 5% signals aggressive capital return and attracts income-oriented investors.
