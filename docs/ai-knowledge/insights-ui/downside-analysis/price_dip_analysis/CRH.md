# Section 1: Analysis

## What This Company Does & Why the Stock Dropped

CRH is the world's largest building materials company and the #1 US producer of aggregates (crushed stone, sand, gravel) and asphalt. The business model is vertically integrated: quarries feed asphalt plants, which feed paving crews. Heavy materials are only economical to transport about 50 miles, turning each quarry into a local monopoly. New quarry permits take 7-10+ years thanks to NIMBY opposition, making the asset base nearly irreplaceable. CRH's top revenue products are aggregates and asphalt (local monopoly materials, largest segment), cement and readymix concrete (infrastructure-critical products), and integrated solutions/paving services (downstream construction services). FY2025 delivered record results — revenue $37.4B (+5%), EBITDA $7.7B (+11%), EBITDA margin 20.5%, and free cash flow $5.0B (+18%).

The stock fell approximately 23% from its $131.55 all-time high to around $102. The decline was driven by three factors: EPS guidance of $5.60-6.05 came in slightly below the $6.09 consensus — a minor miss but enough to crack a 22x+ forward PE that was stretched for a cyclical. Tariff risk surfaced, with 10% tariffs estimated to shave $150-200M from operating profit. The aggressive M&A pace (38 deals, $4.1B in 2025) pushed total debt to $19.7B and net debt/EBITDA to 2.29x, raising questions about balance sheet discipline. This is a valuation reset, not a business breakdown.

## Competitive Position & Moat

CRH's moat is physical, not financial. Quarries are fixed assets rooted in geology — you cannot move a mountain of limestone, and regulators make it nearly impossible to permit a new one (7-10+ years). This gives CRH pricing power that competitors cannot erode through innovation or scale. The company is the #1 US aggregates and asphalt producer with the largest vertically integrated network in North America.

The top 2 direct competitors are Vulcan Materials (VMC, #2 US aggregates) and Martin Marietta (MLM, #3). Both have similar local monopoly dynamics in their respective quarry locations, but CRH is larger and more vertically integrated, running the full chain from quarry to finished road surface. Competition is geographic rather than head-to-head — each quarry's 50-mile economic radius creates non-overlapping territories. Competitive displacement risk is minimal due to the physical/location moat.

The real risk is not competition but cycle exposure and policy. Infrastructure spending drives demand, and while IIJA provides years of funded projects, a severe recession could delay private-sector construction. Carbon regulations are a longer-term headwind for cement production. The elevated debt load from M&A ($19.7B, 2.29x net debt/EBITDA) warrants monitoring.

CRH's moat (5/5 — physical/location monopoly) is among the strongest possible: irreplaceable quarry assets with multi-year permitting barriers.

## Stress Test / Sensitivity Matrix

**Base multiple justification:** 11x EV/EBITDA. CRH's 5-year median EV/EBITDA is approximately 10-12x. Building materials peers (Vulcan 14-16x, Martin Marietta 13-15x) trade at premiums due to higher margins and less leverage. Current price of $102 implies ~$7.8B EBITDA at 11x.

**Valuation regime check:** CRH's EV/EBITDA averaged ~8-9x in 2016-2020 (pre-US listing, lower margins) and ~10-13x in 2021-2025 (US listing premium, record margins, IIJA tailwind). The expansion is partially justified by improved margins (20.5% vs. ~15% pre-2020), US listing re-rating, and infrastructure spending tailwind. For the severe case, 9x EV/EBITDA (above the pre-expansion average, reflecting the structural improvement) is appropriate.

**Key variables:** FY2026 EBITDA x EV/EBITDA Multiple. Assumes net debt ~$15B, ~710M shares diluted.

**Formula:** Price = ((EBITDA x Multiple) - Net Debt) / Shares
