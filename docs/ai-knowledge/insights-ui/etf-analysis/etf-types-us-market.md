# Types of ETFs in the US Market

A comprehensive catalog of every major ETF type available to US investors, organized by structure, strategy, and asset class.

---

## By Management Style

### Passive (Index-Tracking) ETFs
Passive ETFs replicate a predefined index by holding all or a representative sample of its constituents, with no human discretion in security selection. They offer the lowest expense ratios in the industry (often 0.03%-0.10%) because the portfolio manager's job is mechanical replication, not research. Examples include SPY (S&P 500), VTI (Total Stock Market), and BND (Total Bond Market) — the building blocks of most retail portfolios.

### Active ETFs
Active ETFs employ portfolio managers who make discretionary buy/sell decisions to outperform a benchmark, not merely replicate it. They carry higher expense ratios (typically 0.30%-0.75%) but offer the potential for alpha generation, tactical positioning, and downside management that passive funds cannot provide. The category exploded after the 2019 SEC ETF Rule, with notable examples like ARKK (ARK Innovation), JEPI (JPMorgan Equity Premium Income), and AVUV (Avantis US Small Cap Value).

### Semi-Transparent (Non-Transparent) Active ETFs
These active ETFs disclose holdings quarterly rather than daily, protecting the manager's strategy from front-running while still trading on an exchange. They use proprietary structures like Precidian's ActiveShares, Fidelity's proxy portfolio model, or Blue Tractor's shielded alpha to provide enough pricing information for market makers without revealing the full portfolio. Examples include FZROX-linked Fidelity active ETFs and American Century's AVGE, appealing to active managers who previously avoided ETFs due to transparency concerns.

## By Index Construction Strategy

### Market-Cap Weighted ETFs
These track indices where each stock's weight is proportional to its total market capitalization, meaning the largest companies dominate the portfolio. This is the most common and liquid ETF structure, naturally tilting toward winners as rising stocks gain weight. The approach is self-rebalancing and low-turnover but can become dangerously concentrated — as seen when the top 7 stocks reached 30%+ of the S&P 500.

### Equal-Weight ETFs
Every constituent receives the same portfolio weight regardless of market cap, which is reset at each rebalance (typically quarterly). This gives materially more exposure to mid-cap and smaller large-cap names, effectively creating a size and value tilt relative to cap-weighted peers. RSP (Invesco S&P 500 Equal Weight) is the flagship example, and these funds tend to outperform in broad rallies but underperform when mega-caps lead.

### Fundamental-Weight ETFs
Stocks are weighted by economic fundamentals — revenue, earnings, dividends, book value, or cash flow — rather than market price, creating a structural value tilt. The approach was popularized by Research Affiliates (RAFI) and is based on the thesis that cap-weighting overweights overvalued stocks and underweights undervalued ones. Examples include PRF (Invesco FTSE RAFI US 1000) and FNDX (Schwab Fundamental US Large Company), which rebalance toward cheaper stocks as valuations diverge from fundamentals.

### Rules-Based / Smart Beta ETFs
Smart beta ETFs follow transparent, rules-based indices designed to capture specific factor premiums (value, momentum, quality, low volatility, size) without active management discretion. They sit between pure passive and active — the strategy is systematic and predetermined, but the index itself is engineered to outperform cap-weighted benchmarks over full market cycles. Examples include MTUM (iShares MSCI USA Momentum), QUAL (iShares MSCI USA Quality), and VLUE (iShares MSCI USA Value), each targeting a single academically documented factor.

### Multi-Factor ETFs
These combine two or more factor exposures (e.g., value + quality + momentum) into a single fund, aiming for smoother performance since individual factors cycle in and out of favor. The construction can be "mixing" (blend single-factor sleeves) or "integration" (require each stock to score well on multiple factors simultaneously) — the latter tends to produce more differentiated portfolios. GSLC (Goldman Sachs ActiveBeta US Large Cap) and LRGF (iShares US Equity Factor) are prominent examples, offering diversified factor exposure in a single ticker.

## By Asset Class

### Broad Equity ETFs
These provide exposure to an entire equity market or market segment (total market, large cap, mid cap, small cap) in a single fund. They are the most widely held ETFs globally and serve as core portfolio allocations for both retail and institutional investors. VTI (Vanguard Total Stock Market), IVV (iShares Core S&P 500), and IJR (iShares Core S&P Small-Cap) are canonical examples.

### Sector ETFs
Sector ETFs isolate a single GICS sector (Technology, Healthcare, Financials, Energy, etc.), allowing investors to overweight or underweight specific parts of the economy. They are used for tactical bets, sector rotation strategies, and hedging sector-specific risks in a concentrated portfolio. The Select Sector SPDR series (XLK, XLV, XLF, XLE) and Vanguard sector funds (VGT, VHT, VFH) are the most liquid options.

### Industry and Sub-Industry ETFs
These go one level deeper than sector ETFs, targeting specific industries like semiconductors (SOXX), biotechnology (XBI), homebuilders (XHB), or cybersecurity (CIBR). They carry higher concentration risk than sector funds but allow precise exposure to narrow economic themes with direct business-model relevance. Liquidity varies widely — semiconductor and biotech ETFs trade millions of shares daily, while niche industry funds may have wide spreads.

### International Developed Market ETFs
These provide exposure to equities in developed economies outside the US — primarily Europe, Japan, Australia, and Canada. They offer currency diversification and access to companies and sectors underrepresented in US markets (European luxury goods, Japanese industrials, Australian mining). EFA (iShares MSCI EAFE), VEA (Vanguard FTSE Developed Markets), and IEFA (iShares Core MSCI EAFE) are the largest, with the key difference being whether they include or exclude Canada and South Korea.

### Emerging Market ETFs
Emerging market ETFs invest in equities from developing economies — China, India, Taiwan, Brazil, South Korea (in MSCI classification), and others. They offer higher growth potential but come with political risk, currency volatility, regulatory uncertainty, and varying levels of corporate governance. VWO (Vanguard FTSE Emerging Markets), IEMG (iShares Core MSCI Emerging Markets), and EEM (iShares MSCI Emerging Markets) dominate, with important index differences around China A-shares inclusion and South Korea classification.

### Single-Country ETFs
These concentrate all exposure in one country's equity market, from large economies (EWJ for Japan, FXI for China large-cap, EWG for Germany) to frontier markets (VNM for Vietnam, KSA for Saudi Arabia). They are used for targeted geographic bets, hedging country-specific risks, or accessing markets where direct stock purchase is difficult for US retail investors. Currency risk is a major consideration since returns combine local equity performance with exchange rate movements.

### Bond / Fixed Income ETFs
Fixed income ETFs hold portfolios of bonds — government, corporate, municipal, or a blend — providing income and diversification from equities. They democratized bond investing by making a traditionally opaque, dealer-driven market accessible in $1 increments with intraday liquidity. AGG (iShares Core US Aggregate Bond), BND (Vanguard Total Bond Market), and LQD (iShares Investment Grade Corporate Bond) are the core options, while TLT (20+ Year Treasury), HYG (High Yield Corporate), and MUB (Municipal Bond) serve more specific needs.

### Treasury and Government Bond ETFs
These hold exclusively US Treasury securities of specific maturities, offering the lowest credit risk available and direct exposure to interest rate movements. They are used for duration management, safe-haven positioning, and as portfolio ballast during equity drawdowns. SHY (1-3 Year), IEF (7-10 Year), and TLT (20+ Year) let investors target specific points on the yield curve, while TIPS ETFs (TIP, STIP) provide inflation-protected exposure.

### Municipal Bond ETFs
Muni bond ETFs hold debt issued by state and local governments, with interest income that is exempt from federal income tax (and sometimes state tax for in-state bonds). They are primarily valuable for investors in high tax brackets where the after-tax yield exceeds comparable taxable bonds. MUB (iShares National Muni Bond), VTEB (Vanguard Tax-Exempt Bond), and state-specific options like NYF (iShares New York Muni) and CMF (iShares California Muni) are the key funds.

### TIPS (Inflation-Protected) ETFs
These hold Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, whose principal adjusts with CPI, providing a real (inflation-adjusted) return guarantee backed by the US government. They serve as a direct hedge against unexpected inflation, which is the single largest uncompensated risk for long-term bond investors. TIP (iShares TIPS Bond), SCHP (Schwab US TIPS), and STIP (iShares 0-5 Year TIPS) cover different duration profiles.

### Commodity ETFs
Commodity ETFs provide exposure to physical commodities or commodity futures — gold, silver, oil, natural gas, agriculture, or broad commodity baskets. Physically-backed funds (GLD, IAU for gold; SLV for silver) hold the actual commodity in vaults, while futures-based funds (USO for oil, DBA for agriculture) roll futures contracts and are subject to contango/backwardation drag. Understanding the structure (physical vs. futures) is critical because it fundamentally changes the return profile and tax treatment.

### Gold and Precious Metals ETFs
Gold ETFs are the most popular commodity funds, used as inflation hedges, currency debasement protection, and portfolio diversifiers with low equity correlation. GLD (SPDR Gold Shares) and IAU (iShares Gold Trust) hold physical gold bullion, while GLDM offers a lower-cost alternative. Beyond gold, SLV (silver), PPLT (platinum), and GLTR (broad precious metals) provide related but distinct commodity exposures.

### Real Estate / REIT ETFs
REIT ETFs hold portfolios of Real Estate Investment Trusts, providing exposure to commercial real estate (offices, data centers, cell towers, warehouses, apartments) with the liquidity of a stock. REITs are required to distribute 90%+ of taxable income as dividends, making these funds high-yield but tax-inefficient in taxable accounts. VNQ (Vanguard Real Estate), IYR (iShares US Real Estate), and XLRE (Real Estate Select Sector SPDR) are the broadest options, while specialized funds target data centers, cell towers, or residential properties.

### Currency ETFs
Currency ETFs allow investors to take positions on foreign exchange rates without opening a forex account, holding short-term foreign-denominated deposits or currency forwards. They are used for hedging international exposure, speculating on currency movements, or diversifying away from US dollar risk. FXE (euro), FXY (yen), FXB (British pound), and UUP (US Dollar Index bullish) are the most traded, though bid-ask spreads can be wide on less popular currency pairs.

### Cryptocurrency ETFs
Crypto ETFs hold digital assets (directly or via futures), providing regulated, brokerage-account access to cryptocurrency without managing wallets or private keys. Spot Bitcoin ETFs (IBIT, FBTC, GBTC) were approved in January 2024, followed by spot Ethereum ETFs (ETHA, FETH) later that year, transforming crypto from an alternative asset into a standard portfolio allocation option. Futures-based crypto ETFs (BITO) launched earlier but suffer from roll costs that erode long-term returns versus spot holdings.

## By Strategy and Objective

### Dividend ETFs
Dividend ETFs select stocks based on dividend yield, dividend growth, or dividend consistency, targeting investors who want regular income or believe dividend-paying stocks offer superior risk-adjusted returns. They range from high-yield (SPYD, HDV) to dividend growth (VIG, DGRO, SCHD), with the latter emphasizing companies that consistently increase dividends rather than those with the highest current yield. The choice between yield and growth strategies produces meaningfully different sector exposures — high yield tilts toward utilities and energy, while growth tilts toward industrials and healthcare.

### Growth ETFs
Growth ETFs hold companies with above-average revenue and earnings growth expectations, typically trading at higher valuations (P/E, P/S) than the broad market. They outperform during economic expansions and low-rate environments but are vulnerable to multiple compression when rates rise or growth disappoints. VUG (Vanguard Growth), IWF (iShares Russell 1000 Growth), and SPYG (SPDR S&P 500 Growth) are the core options, each using slightly different growth definitions.

### Value ETFs
Value ETFs target stocks trading at discounts to fundamental measures (low P/E, low P/B, high dividend yield), based on the long-documented value premium in academic finance. They tend to outperform during economic recoveries and inflationary periods but can underperform for extended stretches when growth and momentum dominate. VTV (Vanguard Value), IWD (iShares Russell 1000 Value), and SPYV (SPDR S&P 500 Value) define "value" differently, leading to meaningful portfolio differences despite the shared label.

### Low Volatility / Minimum Variance ETFs
These ETFs construct portfolios to minimize overall price volatility, either by selecting the least volatile stocks or by optimizing portfolio-level variance through covariance analysis. They provide a smoother ride and tend to outperform on a risk-adjusted basis over full cycles, though they systematically underperform in strong bull markets when high-beta stocks lead. USMV (iShares MSCI USA Min Vol) and SPLV (Invesco S&P 500 Low Volatility) are the largest, with important methodological differences — USMV optimizes at the portfolio level while SPLV simply picks the 100 lowest-volatility stocks.

### Momentum ETFs
Momentum ETFs select stocks that have exhibited the strongest recent price performance (typically 6-12 month returns), exploiting the well-documented tendency of winning stocks to continue winning over intermediate horizons. They have high turnover and are prone to sharp drawdowns during momentum reversals (when last year's winners suddenly crash), making them more volatile than the broad market despite strong long-term factor premiums. MTUM (iShares MSCI USA Momentum) is the dominant fund, though its semi-annual rebalance means it can be slow to react to momentum shifts.

### Quality ETFs
Quality ETFs select companies based on profitability, earnings stability, balance sheet strength, and corporate governance metrics — essentially filtering for well-run businesses. They tend to be the most defensive of factor ETFs, outperforming during economic downturns when weaker companies face financial stress, while keeping pace in expansions. QUAL (iShares MSCI USA Quality), DGRW (WisdomTree US Quality Dividend Growth), and SPHQ (Invesco S&P 500 Quality) use different quality definitions but share the theme of favoring financially healthy companies.

### Income-Focused / Covered Call ETFs
These ETFs generate income by holding a stock portfolio (or index position) and systematically selling call options against it, collecting option premium in exchange for capping upside potential. They have become enormously popular since 2020, with JEPI (JPMorgan Equity Premium Income) and XYLD (Global X S&P 500 Covered Call) distributing yields of 7-12% annually — far above traditional dividend funds. The trade-off is well-defined: you sacrifice participation in strong rallies for consistent monthly income, making these suitable for income-dependent investors but suboptimal for long-term capital growth.

### Buffer / Defined Outcome ETFs
Buffer ETFs use options strategies to provide a known range of outcomes over a specific period (typically one year) — protecting against the first 9-15% of losses while capping gains at a predetermined level. They were pioneered by Innovator and First Trust, with funds resetting annually (e.g., Innovator S&P 500 Buffer ETF — January Series, BJAN). Investors must understand the "outcome period" concept: buying mid-period means inheriting the current buffer/cap levels, not the original ones — a nuance that many retail investors miss.

### Thematic ETFs
Thematic ETFs target specific investment themes or megatrends — artificial intelligence (BOTZ, ROBT), clean energy (ICLN, QCLN), genomics (ARKG), space exploration (UFO), cybersecurity (HACK), or the metaverse. They appeal to investors with strong convictions about secular trends but carry concentration risk, high turnover as theme definitions evolve, and a history of launching near peak hype and underperforming subsequently. Due diligence on the index methodology is especially critical here, as the definition of "AI company" or "clean energy company" varies wildly across providers.

### ESG / Sustainable Investing ETFs
ESG ETFs screen or weight companies based on environmental, social, and governance criteria, either excluding controversial sectors (negative screening) or tilting toward high ESG scorers (positive screening/integration). The spectrum ranges from light-touch (ESGU, which excludes only controversial weapons and tobacco) to aggressive (KRMA, VEGN) that reshape the portfolio substantially. Performance differences versus non-ESG benchmarks are modest in most periods, but sector exclusions (fossil fuels, defense) can create meaningful tracking differences during energy rallies or geopolitical events.

## By Structure and Mechanics

### Leveraged ETFs
Leveraged ETFs use derivatives (swaps, futures) to deliver 2x or 3x the daily return of an underlying index — TQQQ delivers 3x the daily Nasdaq-100 return, SSO delivers 2x the daily S&P 500 return. Due to daily reset and compounding ("volatility decay"), they can dramatically underperform the simple multiple of the index's return over periods longer than one day, especially in choppy markets. They are designed for single-day tactical trading, not buy-and-hold investing — a critical distinction that many retail investors misunderstand.

### Inverse ETFs
Inverse ETFs deliver the opposite of an index's daily return (-1x) or a leveraged inverse return (-2x, -3x), allowing investors to profit from declining markets without short-selling. Like leveraged ETFs, they reset daily and suffer from compounding effects that make long-term holding unreliable — an inverse S&P 500 ETF held for a year will not return the exact negative of the S&P 500's annual return. SH (ProShares Short S&P 500) and SQQQ (ProShares UltraPro Short QQQ) are common examples, used primarily for short-term hedging.

### Currency-Hedged ETFs
These ETFs hold international stocks or bonds but use currency forwards to neutralize the impact of exchange rate movements on returns, isolating the pure local-market equity or bond return. They are valuable when the US dollar is strengthening (which drags down unhedged international returns) and costly when the dollar is weakening (missing the currency tailwind). HEDJ (WisdomTree Europe Hedged Equity), DXJ (WisdomTree Japan Hedged Equity), and HEFA (iShares Currency Hedged MSCI EAFE) are the main options.

### ETFs of ETFs (Multi-Asset / Allocation ETFs)
These hold other ETFs as their underlying positions, creating a complete portfolio in a single ticker — blending stocks, bonds, commodities, and sometimes alternatives according to a target allocation. They range from target-risk (AOR — iShares Core Growth Allocation, holding 60/40 stock/bond ETFs) to tactical (actively managed allocation ETFs that shift weights based on market conditions). They are the simplest all-in-one solution but add a layer of fees (though many waive the underlying ETF fees to avoid double-charging).

### Actively Managed Transparent ETFs
Unlike semi-transparent active ETFs, these fully disclose holdings daily while still employing active management — the manager's stock picks are visible to anyone each morning. This full transparency benefits from tight bid-ask spreads and reliable NAV tracking but exposes the manager's strategy to front-running and free-riding. Dimensional Fund Advisors (DFAC, DFAT), Avantis (AVUV, AVDV), and Capital Group's American Funds ETFs operate this way, generally accepting the transparency trade-off in exchange for the ETF wrapper's tax efficiency.

### Exchange-Traded Notes (ETNs)
ETNs are unsecured debt obligations of a bank that promise to pay the return of an index, minus fees — they hold no underlying assets. They offer perfect tracking (no sampling error, no dividend drag) and favorable tax treatment for certain strategies (commodities, VIX), but carry the credit risk of the issuing bank — if the bank fails, investors may lose everything, as Lehman Brothers ETN holders did in 2008. VXX (iPath Series B S&P 500 VIX Short-Term Futures) and AMJ (JPMorgan Alerian MLP Index ETN) are notable examples.

## Specialized and Niche Categories

### Volatility ETFs
Volatility ETFs provide exposure to the VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) through VIX futures contracts, rising sharply during market panics and decaying steadily during calm periods. They are among the most dangerous products for retail investors — long VIX ETFs (VXX, UVXY) lose 50-80% of their value annually in normal markets due to futures contango and roll costs. They are designed exclusively for short-term hedging or speculation, and multiple products in this category have been terminated or reverse-split dozens of times.

### Merger Arbitrage / Event-Driven ETFs
These ETFs hold stocks involved in announced mergers and acquisitions, capturing the spread between the current trading price and the deal price at close. They deliver bond-like returns (3-6% annually) with low market correlation, funded by the risk that some deals fail — when a merger collapses, the target stock drops sharply. MNA (IQ Merger Arbitrage ETF) and MRGR (ProShares Merger ETF) are the primary options, offering a strategy that was previously accessible only through hedge funds.

### Options Strategy / Put-Write ETFs
Beyond covered calls, some ETFs systematically sell put options to generate income, effectively getting paid to agree to buy stocks at lower prices. Put-write strategies tend to outperform in flat-to-modestly-rising markets and provide a cushion in mild downturns (the premium collected offsets some losses), but suffer in sharp selloffs when puts move deep in-the-money. PUTW (WisdomTree CBOE S&P 500 PutWrite Strategy) is the primary example, offering a different risk/return profile than covered call funds.

### Preferred Stock ETFs
Preferred stock ETFs hold hybrid securities that pay fixed dividends, sit above common stock in the capital structure, and behave more like bonds than equities. They offer yields of 5-7% with less price volatility than common stock but significant interest rate sensitivity — they drop when rates rise, similar to long-duration bonds. PFF (iShares Preferred & Income Securities), PGX (Invesco Preferred), and PFFD (Global X US Preferred) are the largest, primarily holding bank and financial institution preferreds.

### Convertible Bond ETFs
Convertible bond ETFs hold bonds that can be converted into a predetermined number of shares of common stock, providing bond-like downside protection with equity-like upside participation. They tend to capture 50-70% of equity upside with 30-50% of the downside, making them attractive in uncertain markets where the investor wants equity exposure with a floor. CWB (SPDR Bloomberg Convertible Securities) and ICVT (iShares Convertible Bond) are the main options.

### MLP (Master Limited Partnership) ETFs
MLP ETFs hold shares of publicly traded partnerships, typically in the energy midstream sector (pipelines, storage, processing), offering high yields from stable, fee-based infrastructure revenues. The ETF structure simplifies the notoriously complex K-1 tax reporting that individual MLP ownership requires, issuing a standard 1099 instead. AMLP (Alerian MLP ETF) and MLPA (Global X MLP ETF) are the largest, though investors should note that C-corp-structured MLP ETFs pay corporate taxes internally, reducing the yield compared to direct MLP ownership.

### Floating Rate / Bank Loan ETFs
These ETFs hold senior secured bank loans with interest rates that reset every 30-90 days based on a reference rate (SOFR), providing natural protection against rising interest rates. They sit near the top of the corporate capital structure (senior secured), offering better recovery rates in default than high-yield bonds, but liquidity can dry up during credit stress. BKLN (Invesco Senior Loan) and SRLN (SPDR Blackstone Senior Loan) are the primary funds, popular during rate-hiking cycles.

### Ultra-Short / Money Market Alternative ETFs
Ultra-short bond ETFs hold investment-grade fixed income with durations under one year, serving as higher-yielding alternatives to money market funds with slightly more price volatility. They occupy the space between savings accounts and short-term bond funds, offering daily liquidity and brokerage-account convenience. ICSH (BlackRock Ultra Short-Term Bond), MINT (PIMCO Enhanced Short Maturity), BIL (SPDR Bloomberg 1-3 Month T-Bill), and SGOV (iShares 0-3 Month Treasury Bond) are widely used for cash management.

### Target Maturity / Defined Maturity Bond ETFs
These bond ETFs hold bonds that all mature in the same year, then liquidate and return capital — functioning like individual bonds with ETF diversification. They allow investors to build bond ladders, match liabilities to specific time horizons, and hold to maturity to eliminate interest rate risk (if held to the target date). Invesco BulletShares (e.g., BSCR for 2027 corporate, BSMR for 2027 muni) and iShares iBonds (e.g., IBDQ for 2025 corporate) are the two main series.

### Equal Sector Weight ETFs
Rather than weighting sectors by market cap (which creates heavy tech/healthcare tilts), these ETFs assign equal weight to each of the 11 GICS sectors, rebalancing periodically. This systematically reduces concentration in the currently dominant sector and forces a "buy low, sell high" discipline at the sector level. RSP implicitly achieves some sector equalization through stock-level equal weighting, while EWSC and similar products target sector-level balance explicitly.

### Dividend Aristocrats / Kings ETFs
These ETFs track companies with exceptional long-term dividend growth records — Dividend Aristocrats (25+ consecutive years of dividend increases within the S&P 500) or Dividend Kings (50+ years). They represent the most financially disciplined companies in the market, as maintaining decades of unbroken dividend growth through recessions and crises requires consistent profitability and conservative capital allocation. NOBL (ProShares S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats) is the flagship fund, holding ~65 companies that have survived every economic downturn without cutting dividends.

### Clean Shares / Commission-Free Platform ETFs
These are ETFs specifically designed for distribution on advisory platforms, with no embedded commissions, trail fees, or revenue-sharing arrangements — offering the lowest possible total cost to the end investor. Major brokerages (Schwab, Fidelity, Vanguard) offer proprietary ETF lineups with zero or near-zero expense ratios as loss leaders to attract assets to their platforms. Examples include Fidelity ZERO funds, Schwab's core ETFs (SCHB, SCHX), and Vanguard's flagship funds — the competitive race to zero has made ultra-low-cost indexing universally accessible.

## Quick Reference: Choosing the Right ETF Type

| Investor Goal | Most Relevant ETF Types |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Core portfolio building | Broad Equity, Bond, Market-Cap Weighted |
| Regular income | Dividend, Covered Call, Preferred Stock, MLP |
| Inflation protection | TIPS, Commodity, Real Estate/REIT |
| Reduce portfolio volatility | Low Volatility, Buffer/Defined Outcome, Bond |
| Tactical sector bet | Sector, Industry, Single-Country |
| Factor-based investing | Smart Beta, Multi-Factor, Quality, Momentum, Value |
| International diversification | Developed Market, Emerging Market, Currency-Hedged |
| Short-term trading/hedging | Leveraged, Inverse, Volatility |
| Cash management | Ultra-Short, Target Maturity, Money Market Alternative |
| Thematic conviction | Thematic, ESG, Cryptocurrency |
