# ETF Market Scenarios — Sector and Asset-Class Shocks

Thirty-one recurring market scenarios that meaningfully move specific ETF categories — both downside shocks and upside rallies. The list is filtered for events that (a) have happened more than once historically and (b) are structurally likely to recur. One-off events tied to specific policy regimes or unique crises have been excluded in favor of scenarios that an analyst will pattern-match against again. Each entry covers the scenario, its underlying cause, a historical analog, and which ETF categories / specific ETFs historically win or lose.

---

### 1. Technology Sector Stagnation / Crash
**Underlying cause:** Valuation bubble bursts, growth expectations unwind, or rising rates compress high-multiple stocks. Tech is the sector most sensitive to both liquidity cycles and disruption risk.
**Historical analog:** 2000–2002 dot-com bust — the Nasdaq Composite fell 78% from its March 2000 peak, and the Nasdaq-100 fell 83%. QQQ dropped from ~$120 to ~$20; it took 15 years for the index to reclaim the 2000 high. XLK fell 70%+. Most pure-play internet and software names went bankrupt or lost 90%+.
**Winners:** Defensive sectors — XLP (staples), XLU (utilities), XLV (health care); gold (GLD, IAU); long-duration Treasuries (TLT) as rates fall; value (VTV, IVE); dividend (SCHD, VYM).
**Losers:** Broad tech (XLK, VGT, QQQ); software (IGV); internet (FDN); innovation thematics (ARKK, ARKW); semiconductors (SOXX, SMH); leveraged tech (TQQQ, SOXL) — pure-play unprofitable growth names fall hardest.

### 2. Oil & Gas Crash
**Underlying cause:** Demand collapse (recession, pandemic, EV substitution) or supply surge (OPEC+ market-share war, US shale overproduction).
**Historical analog:** 2014–2016 (WTI fell from $107 to $26, a 76% decline driven by the Saudi quota war) and April 2020 (WTI briefly traded negative at -$37 as demand collapsed). XLE fell ~50% in each episode. USO suffered both the price drop and severe futures contango. Multiple E&P dividend cuts and bankruptcies across the sector.
**Winners:** Consumer discretionary (XLY) and airlines/transports (JETS, IYT) benefit from cheaper fuel; refiners (CRAK) outperform E&Ps as crack spreads widen; oil-importing EMs (INDA).
**Losers:** Energy sector (XLE, VDE, IYE); E&P (XOP); oil services (OIH, IEZ); MLPs (AMLP, MLPX); commodity futures ETFs (USO, DBO — hit twice by price and contango); Canada and energy-heavy EMs (EWZ).

---

### 3. Utilities Boom
**Underlying cause:** Surging electricity demand (AI datacenter buildout, EV adoption, re-shoring manufacturing) combined with falling rates that make utility dividend yields more attractive versus Treasuries.
**Historical analog:** 2024 utilities rally — XLU rose ~25%, the best sector performance of the year, as the AI datacenter power-demand narrative took hold. Power generators with nuclear or capacity exposure (CEG, VST, NRG) doubled or tripled. The sector shifted from a sleepy bond-proxy trade to a growth-adjacent story.
**Winners:** Utilities (XLU, VPU, IDU, FUTY); nuclear/uranium (URA, URNM, NLR); clean energy (ICLN, QCLN, TAN); infrastructure (PAVE, IFRA); grid-focused plays (GRID). Within utilities, generators (XLU heavy in CEG/VST) beat regulated water/gas names.
**Losers:** Low opportunity cost during the rally — but fixed-rate bond proxies (TLT, LQD) underperform if the rally is driven by rising power-demand growth rather than falling rates.

### 4. Banking / Financial Crisis
**Underlying cause:** Credit cycle peak, rising defaults, bank balance-sheet stress, duration mismatches, or liquidity runs on uninsured deposits.
**Historical analog:** 2008–2009 GFC (XLF fell 55%; several names went to zero) and March 2023 regional bank crisis (SVB, Signature, First Republic all failed within days). KBE fell ~30% in three weeks in 2023; regional bank ETF KRE fell ~40%. The 2023 crisis was driven by rate-shock-induced held-to-maturity losses combined with social-media-accelerated deposit runs.
**Winners:** Long-duration Treasuries (TLT, VGLT, ZROZ) on flight-to-safety; short-duration Treasuries (SHY, BIL); gold (GLD, IAU); mega-cap quality (QUAL, MGK) — depositors flee to scale; defensive sectors (XLP, XLU, XLV).
**Losers:** Financials (XLF, VFH); regional banks (KRE, KBE, IAT) fall hardest; broker-dealers (IAI); preferreds (PFF) with heavy bank issuer mix; CRE-exposed REITs (VNQ, office REITs); high-yield (HYG, JNK) as spreads blow out.

### 5. Inflation Shock & Long-Duration Bond Rout
**Underlying cause:** Supply-chain disruptions, fiscal stimulus, or accommodative monetary policy produce unexpected CPI spikes; central banks are forced into aggressive hiking that crushes duration.
**Historical analog:** 2022 — fastest Fed hiking cycle in 40 years (0% to 5.25% in 15 months). TLT fell 31%, AGG fell 13% (worst bond year in history), MBB fell 12%. Bonds failed their diversifier role — stocks and bonds fell together for the first time in decades. 60/40 allocation funds had their worst year since 2008.
**Winners:** TIPS (TIP, SCHP, VTIP); commodities (DBC, PDBC, GSG); energy (XLE, VDE); value (VTV, IVE, VLUE); ultra-short duration (BIL, SGOV); floating-rate loans (BKLN, SRLN); gold (GLD).
**Losers:** Long-duration Treasuries (TLT, ZROZ, EDV) — the epicenter; aggregate bond (AGG, BND); long credit (LQD, VCLT); MBS (MBB); long-duration growth (VUG, QQQ, ARKK); REITs (VNQ); 60/40 funds (AOR, AOM).

### 6. Emerging Markets Currency Crisis
**Underlying cause:** Strong US dollar plus Fed tightening exposes EM countries with fiscal deficits or current-account weakness; capital flight triggers currency collapse and equity selloffs.
**Historical analog:** 1997 Asian Financial Crisis (Thai baht, Indonesian rupiah, Korean won fell 40–80% in months). 2013 Taper Tantrum (EMB fell 9% in three months). 2018 Turkey/Argentina crisis (local markets down 50%+). VWO typically falls 20%+ in each episode; EM local-currency bond funds (EMLC) can fall 15%+.
**Winners:** USD (UUP); US Treasuries (IEF, TLT, SHY); US large-cap quality (SPY, QUAL); defensive staples (XLP); gold (GLD) when crisis turns systemic.
**Losers:** EM equities (VWO, EEM, IEMG, SCHE); EM local-currency bonds (EMLC, LEMB) hit hardest; EM dollar bonds (EMB) milder but still down; frontier markets (FM); single-country EMs (TUR, EWZ, RSX/defunct); commodity EMs (EWZ); EM small caps (EWX).

### 7. Value Regime / Growth Underperformance Cycle
**Underlying cause:** Rising interest rates compress growth-stock multiples while value stocks (financials, energy, industrials) benefit from higher rates and inflation passing through to earnings.
**Historical analog:** 2000–2006 post-dot-com period — value outperformed growth cumulatively by 50%+. 2022 — value (VTV) outperformed growth (VUG) by 20%+ as rates rose from 0 to 5%. Value strategies that had been ignored for a decade (2010–2020) suddenly delivered the factor premium academics had been defending.
**Winners:** Value factor (VTV, IVE, VLUE, RPV); financials (XLF, KBE); energy (XLE, VDE); industrials (XLI); dividend (SCHD, VYM, DVY); equal-weight (RSP); small-cap value (AVUV, IWN, VIOV).
**Losers:** Growth factor (VUG, IVW, MGK, SPYG); large-cap tech (XLK, QQQ); mega-cap growth (MGC); innovation thematics (ARKK, ARKW, ARKG); long-duration biotech (XBI); small-cap growth (IWO, VBK).

### 8. Commercial Real Estate / REIT Crash
**Underlying cause:** Rate shock raises cap rates and compresses property values; refinancing becomes painful; structural demand shifts (work-from-home killing office demand) compound the stress.
**Historical analog:** 2008–2010 VNQ fell 68% as the mortgage crisis triggered a cascade. 2022–2024 commercial real estate crisis (office REITs down 60%+, regional banks with heavy CRE loan books stressed). CRE debt refinancing remains a slow-burning concern as 2020–2021 zero-rate loans mature.
**Winners:** Short-duration fixed income (SHV, BIL, SGOV); quality large caps (SPY, QUAL); defensive sectors (XLP, XLV). Within REITs, data-center/industrial names (held in VNQ via PSA, DLR, PLD) and health-care REITs (VTR) hold up better than office/retail.
**Losers:** Broad REITs (VNQ, IYR, SCHH, RWR); office/retail-heavy REITs (BXP as a holding); mortgage REITs (REM, MORT) fall hardest — double hit from credit and duration; regional banks with CRE loans (KRE, IAT); homebuilders indirectly (XHB).

### 9. Gold / Precious Metals Boom
**Underlying cause:** Loss of confidence in fiat currencies, real yields turn negative, geopolitical uncertainty, central bank accumulation, or debasement fears from large fiscal deficits.
**Historical analog:** 2008–2011 gold rose from $700 to $1,900 (+170%) post-GFC. 2020 pushed gold to a then-record $2,075. 2023–2025 gold surged 40%+ past $2,700 on central bank buying (China, India reserve diversification) and dollar hedging. GLD, IAU, and GLDM all logged multi-year highs.
**Winners:** Physical gold ETFs (GLD, IAU, GLDM, SGOL, BAR); gold miners (GDX, GDXJ, SGDM) — operational leverage makes miners outperform the metal 2–3x; silver (SLV, SIVR); silver miners (SIL, SILJ); broad precious metals (GLTR); platinum/palladium (PPLT, PALL).
**Losers:** USD (UUP); long-duration bonds in real terms (TLT real yield collapses); growth equities in the stagflation variant; USD-hedged international funds (HEDJ type).

### 10. Cryptocurrency Crash
**Underlying cause:** Leverage unwinds, stablecoin failures, major fraud revelations, regulatory crackdowns, or simple bubble exhaustion after parabolic moves.
**Historical analog:** 2018 crypto winter (Bitcoin fell from $20K to $3K). 2022 was worse — Luna/Terra collapse in May, Three Arrows Capital bankruptcy, FTX fraud in November (Bitcoin fell from $69K to $16K, -77%). Grayscale GBTC traded at a 50% discount to NAV. Crypto-proxy stocks (COIN, MSTR, MARA) fell 80%+.
**Winners:** Mostly contained — broad market and safe-haven ETFs unaffected. Quality (QUAL); large-cap value (VTV); short Treasuries (BIL, SHV); gold (GLD) picks up some "hard money" flows diverted from crypto.
**Losers:** Spot bitcoin ETFs (IBIT, FBTC, GBTC, BITB, ARKB); spot ether ETFs (ETHA, ETHE, FETH); crypto-equity proxies (crypto miner ETFs WGMI, BITQ; BITO futures-based); blockchain thematics (BLOK, BLCN); ARK funds with heavy COIN/crypto allocations.

### 11. Small-Cap Recession / Underperformance
**Underlying cause:** Rate hikes hurt smaller companies disproportionately (higher floating-rate debt costs, less pricing power, recession sensitivity). Russell 2000's heavy regional-bank weighting adds financial-sector exposure.
**Historical analog:** 2022–2023 small caps (IWM) underperformed large caps (SPY) by 20%+ as rates rose and regional banks (held in IWM) fell. 2008 IWM fell 41%, worse than the S&P 500's 37% decline. Small-cap value (IWN, AVUV) typically holds up better than small-cap growth in these cycles.
**Winners:** Large-cap (SPY, VV, IVV); mega-cap (MGK, MGC); quality factor (QUAL, SPHQ); low volatility (SPLV, USMV); dividend (SCHD); within small caps, small-cap value (AVUV, IWN) outperforms small-cap growth.
**Losers:** Broad small-cap (IWM, VB, IJR, SCHA); small-cap growth (IWO, VBK); microcaps (IWC, IJT); S&P SmallCap 600 (SLY, IJR); high-leverage small-cap tilt funds.

### 12. Residential Housing Crash
**Underlying cause:** Over-leverage in mortgage lending, subprime credit excess, speculative buying, or affordability collapse from sharply rising rates. Distinct from commercial real estate stress — this is single-family housing.
**Historical analog:** 2006–2012 US housing bust — national home prices fell 30%; Arizona, Nevada, and Florida fell 50%+. Homebuilder ETF XHB fell 80% peak-to-trough. Mortgage-backed securities blew up and triggered the GFC. Affordability-driven cycles recur whenever rates rise faster than wages.
**Winners:** Short Treasuries (SHY, BIL); gold (GLD); defensive sectors (XLP, XLU, XLV); rental-focused operators (single-family rental REITs AMH/INVH held in VNQ benefit from displaced buyers). International developed (EFA) decoupled in regional housing busts.
**Losers:** Homebuilders (XHB, ITB, PKB); mortgage REITs (REM, MORT); MBS (MBB, VMBS, CMBS); regional banks with mortgage books (KRE, KBE); building products / materials (CAT and MLM heavy names); mortgage insurers (held in KIE).

### 13. Biotech Boom & Bust Cycle
**Underlying cause:** Booms from approval waves, IPO enthusiasm, and speculative early-stage names; busts from drug-pricing regulation, rate hikes hitting pre-revenue companies, or high-profile trial failures.
**Historical analog:** 2013–2015 biotech boom (IBB tripled). 2015–2016 bust — Hillary Clinton's drug-pricing tweet pushed XBI down 40%. 2021–2023 biotech bust (XBI fell 60%+) as rising rates killed the pre-revenue model and cash-burn biotechs faced existential pressure.
**Winners in bust:** Large-cap pharma (XPH, PPH, IHE) — profitable with pricing power; medical devices (IHI); broad health care (XLV, VHT).
**Losers in bust:** Small/equal-weight biotech (XBI) — cash-burn names fall hardest; cap-weighted biotech (IBB) milder; genomics (ARKG); cell & gene therapy thematics; clinical-stage-heavy funds. Order flips in a boom: XBI outperforms IBB because equal-weighting captures small-cap IPO winners.

### 14. Geopolitical Oil Price Spike
**Underlying cause:** War, embargo, or Middle East conflict disrupts supply; OPEC+ cuts quotas deliberately; shipping-chokepoint disruption (Strait of Hormuz, Suez, Red Sea) threatens flow.
**Historical analog:** 1973 OPEC embargo (oil +300%), 1990 Gulf War (oil doubled in 3 months), 2022 Russia invasion of Ukraine (WTI hit $130). XLE rose 60%+ in 2022 and was the best-performing sector that year. Defense and energy typically rally together in these shocks.
**Winners:** Energy (XLE, VDE, IYE, FENY); E&P (XOP, IEO); oil services (OIH, IEZ); integrated majors; defense (ITA, PPA, XAR); MLPs (AMLP); broad commodities (DBC, PDBC); oil-tracking ETFs (USO, BNO).
**Losers:** Airlines/transports (JETS, IYT) — fuel cost shock; consumer discretionary (XLY); refiners (CRAK) squeezed when crude rises faster than product prices; oil-importing EMs (INDA, EWW); trucking and logistics (XTN).

### 15. Semiconductor Cycle Downturn
**Underlying cause:** Inventory overbuild, softening end-demand (PCs, smartphones, autos), or export controls. The semi industry runs a 2–4 year capex cycle that repeatedly produces boom-bust patterns.
**Historical analog:** 2018–2019 semi downturn (SOXX fell 25% in late 2018 on crypto-mining demand collapse and memory glut). 2022 downturn (SOXX fell 40% as post-COVID pull-forward normalized). Recoveries are typically sharp — SOXX doubled from late 2022 to mid-2024 on AI-driven demand.
**Winners:** Software (IGV, VGT) — less cyclical than hardware; defensive sectors (XLP, XLU, XLV); broad market vs. tech (SPY beats XLK); cybersecurity (HACK, CIBR); within semis, design-software (ADBE/SNPS heavy) beats foundries and memory.
**Losers:** Semi ETFs (SOXX, SMH, XSD, PSI); leveraged semis (SOXL) — blow up in downturns; semi equipment (held in SOXX via AMAT, LRCX); memory-heavy exposure; Asia tech (EWT, KWEB) with heavy semi supply chains.

### 16. High-Yield / Credit Spread Blowout
**Underlying cause:** Recession fears, sector-specific stress (energy, retail, CRE), or liquidity crunch widens corporate credit spreads sharply — sometimes without a broad equity crash triggering it.
**Historical analog:** 2015–2016 energy HY stress (HY spread widened from 350bp to 850bp as oil crashed; HYG fell 15%). March 2020 briefly (HY spread hit 1,000bp before Fed intervention). HYG and JNK typically fall 10–20%+; bank-loan funds (BKLN) hold up better due to loan seniority and floating rates.
**Winners:** Treasuries (TLT, IEF, GOVT, VGIT); investment-grade quality short-duration (VCSH, SCHZ); bank loans / floating-rate (BKLN, SRLN, FLRN) — seniority and floating rates; T-bills (BIL, SGOV); gold (GLD).
**Losers:** High yield (HYG, JNK, SJNK, SHYG, USHY); fallen-angel funds (ANGL); EM hard-currency bonds (EMB); convertibles (CWB); preferreds (PFF, PGX); closed-end credit funds; long IG credit (LQD, VCLT) if crisis widens.

### 17. Stagflation
**Underlying cause:** Inflation stays elevated while growth slows — typically from supply-side shocks (oil, labor, deglobalization) combined with central banks unable to cut rates without reigniting inflation. The nightmare scenario for 60/40 portfolios.
**Historical analog:** 1973–1980 US stagflation — real S&P 500 returns were deeply negative after inflation; nominal bonds were destroyed by double-digit CPI. Gold rose 1,500% from $35 to $850. Commodities and hard assets outperformed paper assets for a full decade. Risk of recurrence whenever supply shocks combine with large fiscal deficits.
**Winners:** Commodities (DBC, PDBC, GSG); gold (GLD, IAU); energy (XLE, VDE); short-duration TIPS (VTIP, STIP); materials (XLB); MLPs (AMLP, MLPX); farmland / real assets; value dividend (SCHD); managed futures strategies.
**Losers:** Long nominal bonds (TLT, ZROZ, EDV); aggregate bond (AGG, BND); long credit (LQD); long-duration growth (VUG, QQQ); 60/40 balanced funds (AOR, AOM); target-date funds with heavy bond weights; REITs (VNQ) if driven by real-rate rise.

---

### 18. Agricultural / Food Price Shock
**Underlying cause:** Drought, war disrupting grain exports, government export bans, biofuel-driven demand, or extreme weather patterns create supply shortages across major food commodities.
**Historical analog:** 2007–2008 global food crisis (wheat +150%, rice +200%, triggered riots in 30 countries). 2022 Russia–Ukraine war disrupted Black Sea grain (those two countries = 30% of global wheat exports); wheat rallied 40%. Agricultural ETFs (DBA, WEAT, CORN) rally; agribusiness equities (MOO) gain.
**Winners:** Agricultural commodities (DBA, WEAT, CORN, SOYB, TAGS); agribusiness equities (MOO, VEGI, FTAG); fertilizer-heavy exposure (held in MOO via NTR, CF, MOS); farmland REITs; inflation hedges (TIP, gold).
**Losers:** EM consumer-heavy ETFs (food is a large share of EM CPI — INDA, FXI hit by inflation); food retailers / staples with input cost drag (XLP components like KR, COST squeezed); restaurant ETFs; packaged food companies without hedging.

### 19. Defense Sector Boom
**Underlying cause:** A major war, geopolitical escalation, or sustained defense-spending commitment (e.g., NATO 2% or 3% GDP targets) drives a multi-year revenue and backlog-growth cycle for defense contractors.
**Historical analog:** 2022 Russia invasion of Ukraine. ITA rose 25% in 2022 while the S&P 500 fell 18%. European defense stocks (Rheinmetall, BAE Systems) went up 2x–3x. PPA, XAR, and ITA entered multi-year uptrends as NATO spending accelerated and munitions restocking ramped up.
**Winners:** Aerospace & defense (ITA, PPA, XAR, DFEN); cybersecurity (HACK, CIBR, BUG); European defense exposure (EUAD, NATO-focused funds); industrials (XLI) with defense weight. Pure-play defense prime names (LMT, RTX, NOC, GD) anchor the rally.
**Losers:** Multinationals exposed to conflict regions; commercial aerospace suppliers during wartime supply-chain disruption; international ETFs with heavy affected-region exposure (RSX-type Russia funds wiped out in 2022).

### 20. Mega-Cap Concentration Reversal
**Underlying cause:** The top 5–10 stocks in the S&P 500 reach extreme weight (25–35%) during a growth cycle; when they multiple-compress simultaneously (earnings disappointment, antitrust, rate shock), they pull the whole cap-weighted index down even as most stocks hold up.
**Historical analog:** 2000 dot-com — top 10 stocks were 25%+ of the S&P; Cisco, Intel, Microsoft all fell 60–80%. The S&P took 7 years to recover. As of early 2026, the top 7 exceed 30% of the S&P 500 again — a similar concentration setup. Equal-weight funds (RSP) historically outperform cap-weighted funds during these reversals.
**Winners:** Equal-weight S&P (RSP); equal-weight sector ETFs (RYT, RSPT); mid-caps (IJH, MDY, VO); small-cap value (AVUV, IWN); dividend (SCHD, VIG); international (EFA, VEA, VXUS); factor diversifiers (VLUE, QUAL, USMV).
**Losers:** Cap-weighted S&P (SPY, VOO, IVV); Nasdaq (QQQ, QQQM); mega-cap growth (MGK, MGC); sector ETFs with concentration (XLK, XLC — heavy top-5 weights); target-date funds anchored to cap-weighted beta.

### 21. Yield Curve Inversion & Recession Onset
**Underlying cause:** Fed hikes short rates faster than long rates rise (or long rates fall), producing an inverted curve that has preceded every US recession in the past 50 years. Banks' net interest margins compress, credit creation slows, and risk assets eventually reprice.
**Historical analog:** 1990, 2000, 2007, 2019, and 2022 all saw curve inversions followed by recessions within 6–18 months. After 2007's inversion, the S&P fell 55% over 2008–09; XLF fell 80%. The 2022–2023 inversion preceded a 2024 growth slowdown. Banks (KBE, KRE) typically underperform during and after inversions; long-duration Treasuries (TLT) eventually rally as recession pricing kicks in.
**Winners:** Long-duration Treasuries (TLT, VGLT, ZROZ, EDV); defensive sectors (XLP, XLU, XLV); quality (QUAL, SPHQ); low volatility (SPLV, USMV); gold (GLD) — recession-pricing asset classes.
**Losers:** Banks (KBE, KRE, XLF, IAT); small caps (IWM, VB, IJR); high yield (HYG, JNK); cyclicals (XLI, XLB, XLY); regional-bank-heavy micro/small funds; early-cycle sector bets.

### 22. US Dollar Strength Cycle (DXY Surge)
**Underlying cause:** Fed tightening outpaces other central banks, US growth outperforms globally, or flight-to-safety pushes capital into dollar-denominated assets. A strong dollar crushes commodities, EM equities, and US multinationals' foreign earnings.
**Historical analog:** 2014–2016 DXY rose from 80 to 100 (+25%); commodities and EM were crushed — VWO fell 25%, oil fell 70%. 2022 DXY spiked to 114 on Fed hikes; EEM fell 22%, EFA fell 15%, gold fell 10% in USD terms. Multinationals with 40%+ foreign revenue (MSFT, AAPL, PG, KO) saw earnings headwinds from FX translation.
**Losers:** Emerging markets (VWO, EEM, IEMG, SCHE); developed international (EFA, VEA, VXUS); broad commodities (DBC, PDBC, GSG); gold in USD terms (GLD, IAU); EM debt (EMB, EMLC); multinationals with heavy foreign revenue; Japan unhedged (EWJ).
**Winners:** US small-cap value (AVUV, VIOV, IWN) — domestic revenue shields from FX; USD index ETFs (UUP); short-duration USD bonds (BIL, SHY, SGOV); USD-hedged international (HEFA, DBEF, HEDJ, DXJ); US-focused staples and value (VTV).

### 23. Fed Pivot / Rate Cut Cycle
**Underlying cause:** Fed shifts from hiking to cutting rates, usually in response to slowing growth, financial stress, or falling inflation. Duration rallies hard, growth outperforms value, gold rallies, emerging markets recover, and rate-sensitive sectors (REITs, utilities) rerate upward.
**Historical analog:** 2019 Fed pivot — after the December 2018 panic, the Fed cut three times in 2019. TLT rose 14%, growth (VUG) outperformed value by 15%, gold rose 18%. 2024 pivot — Fed cut 100bp starting September; duration rallied, REITs rallied, and small caps initially rallied. Rate-cut cycles recur every 5–10 years as the Fed responds to slowdowns.
**Winners:** Long-duration Treasuries (TLT, VGLT, ZROZ, EDV); long credit (LQD, VCLT); growth (VUG, QQQ, MGK); REITs (VNQ, IYR); utilities (XLU); gold (GLD); emerging markets (VWO, EEM); small caps (IWM) early in the cut cycle; mortgage REITs (REM).
**Losers:** USD (UUP); cash-heavy positioning and money-market funds (yields collapse); bank-loan floating-rate funds (BKLN, SRLN) lose coupon; TIPS breakevens can compress.

---

### 24. Trade War / Tariff Shock
**Underlying cause:** Broad-based tariffs disrupt global supply chains, raise input costs for importers, and create retaliation risk. Specific sectors (autos, electronics, retail, agriculture) face margin compression or demand loss.
**Historical analog:** 2018–2019 US–China trade war — Section 301 tariffs on $370B of Chinese imports; China retaliated with tariffs on US agriculture. Semis (SOXX) fell 25% in late 2018; soybean exporters hurt; retailers with China supply chains (BBY, TGT) sold off. 2025 tariff expansion under a new administration revived the theme and affected auto, steel, and consumer-electronics ETFs.
**Winners:** Domestic small caps (IWM, AVUV) — less import exposure; US steel/industrial (SLX, XLI with domestic-steel tilt); defense (ITA, PPA); domestic consumer staples (XLP with US-manufactured weight); gold (GLD) on uncertainty.
**Losers:** Semiconductors (SOXX, SMH); China equities (FXI, MCHI, KWEB, ASHR); broad EM (VWO, EEM); autos (CARZ) with global supply chains; retailers with Chinese sourcing (XRT); soybean / ag exporters (DBA); shipping / transports (IYT); EFA broadly.

### 25. Yen Carry Trade Unwind
**Underlying cause:** BOJ tightens unexpectedly or risk-off sentiment triggers rapid yen appreciation; leveraged short-yen positions funding global risk assets are forcibly unwound in hours, causing cascading selling.
**Historical analog:** August 5, 2024 — Nikkei fell 12% in a single day (worst since 1987 Black Monday) as a BOJ hike plus weaker US jobs triggered the yen to rally 8% in days. VIX spiked to 65 intraday. EM, mega-cap tech, and leveraged positions were all hit. This is a recurring risk whenever yen-funded carry trades become crowded — and they tend to get crowded in low-vol regimes.
**Winners:** Yen-long / unhedged Japan (EWJ benefits from yen rally in USD terms); US Treasuries (TLT, IEF) on flight-to-safety; VIX products (VXX) briefly; gold (GLD); USD-hedged defensive exposure.
**Losers:** USD-hedged Japan (DXJ, HEWJ) — hedge unwinds against you; mega-cap tech (QQQ, XLK); leveraged tech (TQQQ, SOXL); emerging markets (VWO, EEM); high-beta / momentum (MTUM); AI-concentrated thematic ETFs; high-yield (HYG).

### 26. Corporate Earnings Recession
**Underlying cause:** Corporate earnings decline 10%+ over 2–3 quarters without an official GDP recession — often driven by margin compression from wage costs, input inflation, or demand weakness in specific sectors. The index goes sideways or down even as the economy technically grows.
**Historical analog:** 2015–2016 earnings recession — S&P 500 EPS fell for five consecutive quarters, driven by the oil bust and a strong dollar hitting multinationals. The index went sideways for 18 months. 2022–2023 mega-cap tech earnings recession — FAANG EPS fell 20%+ while the broader economy grew. More stealthy than GDP recessions, but still a meaningful driver of drawdowns in sector-concentrated ETFs.
**Winners:** Quality factor (QUAL, SPHQ) — earnings-stable names; dividend growth (SCHD, VIG, NOBL); defensive sectors (XLP, XLV, XLU); TIPS (TIP) if inflation-driven; international (EFA) if earnings pain is US-specific.
**Losers:** Sector-concentrated ETFs mirroring the affected industry (XLE in 2015 oil bust, XLK in 2022–23 tech pain); cyclicals with margin squeeze (XLI, XLY); small-cap growth (IWO) — thinner margins; leveraged-equity ETFs amplify the drawdown.

### 27. Infrastructure Buildout Cycle
**Underlying cause:** Government-scale capital spending programs on transportation, power grid, water systems, or broadband — triggered by deferred maintenance, geopolitical competition, or climate adaptation. These cycles typically run 5–10 years once initiated and reshape the earnings outlook for industrials, materials, and engineering firms.
**Historical analog:** 1950s Eisenhower Interstate Highway Act reshaped the US transportation economy for decades. 2021–2022 trio of Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act ($1.2T) + CHIPS Act ($280B) + IRA ($370B) triggered a multi-year buildout. PAVE rose 60%+ in 2023–2024; CAT, MLM, VMC, and ETN rallied on backlog growth. Cement, aggregates, and engineering firms saw multi-year order-book expansion.
**Winners:** Infrastructure thematics (PAVE, IFRA, GII, NFRA, IGF); industrials (XLI, VIS); materials (XLB, VAW); US small/mid-cap industrials (AVUV with industrial tilt); utilities tied to grid buildout (XLU, GRID); copper (COPX) and aggregates exposure; engineering & construction pure-plays.
**Losers:** Long-duration bonds (TLT, ZROZ) if the spending is deficit-financed and inflationary; rate-sensitive REITs (VNQ) if yields rise; dollar-sensitive EMs if the buildout strengthens USD.

### 28. New Technology Adoption Wave (S-Curve Mania)
**Underlying cause:** A new general-purpose technology hits the steep part of its adoption S-curve, driving massive capex and earnings growth for early winners and adjacent infrastructure providers. The cycle repeats roughly every 8–15 years around a new platform shift.
**Historical analog:** 1995–2000 internet wave (Nasdaq 5x). 2007–2012 mobile wave (AAPL 10x+). 2015–2020 cloud wave (AMZN, MSFT, CRM). 2022–2025 AI wave — NVDA rose from $13 to $130 (10x), SOXX rose 130%, and the "AI infrastructure" trade dominated. SMH, SOXX, and picks-and-shovels ETFs (utilities, power generators) typically lead.
**Winners:** Semis (SOXX, SMH, SOXL); tech (XLK, VGT, QQQ, QQQM); AI-adjacent (BOTZ, IRBO, AIQ); cloud (CLOU, SKYY, WCLD); robotics (ROBO); innovation (ARKK, ARKW) in early phase; utilities/power on datacenter buildout (XLU, VST/CEG heavy); copper (COPX) on electrification.
**Losers in relative terms:** Legacy value (VTV) lags; sectors displaced by the new tech (traditional retail during e-commerce wave — XRT; legacy enterprise hardware during cloud wave); high-dividend utilities if money rotates into growth; EM lagging US tech concentration.

### 29. Commodity Supercycle
**Underlying cause:** Multi-year structural supply-demand imbalance. Under-investment in capacity during bust years meets surging demand from industrialization, geopolitical restocking, or energy transition. Typically lasts 8–15 years and lifts broad commodity indices, producers, and commodity-linked currencies together.
**Historical analog:** 2001–2008 commodity supercycle — China's industrialization drove oil from $20 to $147, copper 6x, and corn 3x. DBC peaked in 2008; GSG and DBA had massive rallies. The 1970s stagflation decade was an earlier supercycle. A new cycle may be building from 2020s energy-transition metals demand (copper, lithium, uranium) combined with years of capex cuts in traditional mining.
**Winners:** Broad commodities (DBC, PDBC, GSG, COMT); energy (XLE, VDE, XOP); materials (XLB, VAW); miners (PICK, XME, GDX); copper (COPX); uranium (URA, URNM); lithium (LIT); gold/silver (GLD, SLV, GDX); Latin America (ILF, EWZ) and Canadian resources (EWC).
**Losers:** Long-duration bonds (TLT, ZROZ) as inflation stays elevated; disinflation-dependent growth stocks (VUG); consumer discretionary (XLY) squeezed by input costs; oil-importing EMs (INDA, EWW).

### 30. Post-Recession Small-Cap / Cyclical Rally
**Underlying cause:** Once a recession bottoms, small caps and cyclicals (industrials, materials, consumer discretionary) rally hardest because they carry the most operational leverage to the earnings recovery. Valuations are typically at cycle-low multiples relative to large caps.
**Historical analog:** 2009–2013 IWM rose 180% from the March 2009 low, outperforming SPY by 50%. 2020–2021 IWM rose 140% from the March 2020 low. Early-cycle sectors — XLI, XLB, and XLY — consistently led the recovery. This pattern recurs reliably in every business cycle.
**Winners:** Small-caps (IWM, VB, IJR, SCHA); small-cap value (AVUV, IWN, VIOV); microcaps (IWC); industrials (XLI, VIS); materials (XLB, VAW); consumer discretionary (XLY); financials (XLF, KRE) on yield-curve steepening; high yield (HYG, JNK) recovers sharply; EM (VWO) on risk-on.
**Losers in relative terms:** Defensive sectors (XLP, XLU, XLV) lag; long-duration Treasuries (TLT) — money rotates out of safe havens; low-volatility (SPLV, USMV); gold (GLD) after initial bottom.

### 31. Emerging Markets Rally Cycle
**Underlying cause:** US dollar peaks and weakens, commodity cycle turns up, or China/India growth reaccelerates — these factors often align to drive multi-year EM outperformance over US equities.
**Historical analog:** 2003–2007 BRIC-era rally — VWO rose 300%+ vs. S&P 100%. 2009–2011 post-GFC EM rally driven by China stimulus. 2016–2017 EM rally (VWO +45% vs. SPY +25%) as the dollar weakened and China restimulated. The setup recurs every 5–10 years whenever the dollar weakens and commodities rally simultaneously.
**Winners:** Broad EM (VWO, EEM, IEMG, SCHE); single-country — China (FXI, MCHI, KWEB, ASHR); Brazil (EWZ); India (INDA, SMIN); frontier markets (FM); EM small caps (EWX); EM local-currency bonds (EMLC, LEMB); commodity-linked EMs; Latin America (ILF).
**Losers in relative terms:** US mega-cap tech (MGK, QQQ) underperforms; USD (UUP); USD-hedged international (HEFA, DBEF); long US duration if EM rally is driven by global reflation.
