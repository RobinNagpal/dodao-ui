# DIG (NYSEARCA) — Cost, Efficiency & Team

_The cost and efficiency profile for this ETF is mixed. The fund charges a standard `0.95%` expense ratio for a leveraged product and has survived since its `2007` inception, managing a viable `$103M` in assets. However, it trades with a slightly wide `0.12%` bid-ask spread and thin `$3.0M` average daily volume, adding execution friction to its daily-reset mandate. Combined with the steep embedded financing drag of 2x leverage, the all-in holding costs are extremely high. Overall, it is a functional short-term trading tool but an expensive one to execute in retail lot sizes._

## Report

The fund charges a `0.95%` expense ratio, which falls solidly in line with the `~0.80–1.15%` standard range for leveraged equity products. It manages `$103M` in assets, comfortably above the `$50M` closure-risk threshold but relatively small for a long-standing product. Liquidity metrics reflect its smaller footprint, with an average daily trading volume of `$3.0M` and a moderate `0.12%` bid-ask spread, making a retail round-trip slightly frictional. As a sector-focused vehicle, its core underlying exposure is highly concentrated, with Exxon Mobil and Chevron driving the S&P Energy Select Sector Index and comprising over `30%` of the fund's combined physical and swap weight.

The fund reports a `27%` portfolio turnover, sitting neatly within the `20–60%` norm for sector-based strategies, though for a daily-reset leveraged fund, physical turnover understates the true rebalancing activity happening inside its swap agreements. Because this is a 2x leveraged vehicle, the structural costs far exceed the sticker fee. Investors face an all-in single-year cost estimate consisting of the `0.95%` headline expense ratio, roughly `8–10%` in embedded overnight financing costs (assuming SOFR around `4–5%` multiplied by the 2x leverage factor), plus daily volatility drag. This creates a real hold cost that easily exceeds `~10–12%` annually in normal regimes, confirming the fund is strictly a short-term trading tool rather than a buy-and-hold asset.

ProShares is an established issuer of leveraged and inverse products, providing a reliable operational footprint for this complex strategy. The fund boasts a track record dating back to its `2007` inception, proving it can survive multiple market cycles and oil-price shocks without liquidating. Its lead managers bring a lengthy `12.5 years` of maximum tenure, ensuring consistent handling of the swap rolls and daily rebalancing, though manager tenure on mechanical leveraged funds is less critical to forward returns than underlying index volatility.

The fund's strengths include its battle-tested `2007` inception date and its reasonable `0.95%` fee relative to other 2x leveraged sector peers. However, its main risks are structural: the hidden `~8–10%` financing drag and a `0.12%` bid-ask spread on thin `$3.0M` daily volume make it a costly vehicle to trade and completely unsuitable to hold long-term. For retail investors wanting energy exposure without the steep cost stack, the unleveraged Energy Select Sector SPDR Fund (XLE) offers the exact same underlying index for just `0.09%`, sacrificing the 2x daily multiplier in exchange for significant fee savings and deep liquidity. Overall, this ETF's cost profile looks mixed because while its headline fee is standard for a leveraged fund, its moderate trading volume and high embedded swap costs make it an expensive instrument to handle.
