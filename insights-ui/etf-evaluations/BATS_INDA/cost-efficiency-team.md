# INDA (BATS) — Cost, Efficiency & Team

_The cost and efficiency profile for iShares MSCI India ETF is Mixed. While the fund offers unparalleled market access with a `0.00%` bid-ask spread and a massive `$6.78B` asset base, its `0.61%` expense ratio is uncompetitive for a passive index tracker. The underlying portfolio is managed efficiently with an expected `30%` turnover rate, and operations benefit from a steady `13.4 years` of manager tenure. Ultimately, retail investors are paying a premium for secondary-market liquidity rather than strategic outperformance._

## Categories

- **Bid Ask Spread And Trading Cost** _(result: Pass)_: Exceptional daily volume and institutional-grade market making compress trading frictions to effectively zero.
- **Expense Ratio Vs Competition** _(result: Fail)_: The fund operates a passive market-cap-weighted strategy but charges a fee more typical of older or actively managed emerging market products.
- **Expense Ratio Vs Expected Returns** _(result: Fail)_: Without an active management overlay, the fund's higher expenses mechanically guarantee it will trail cheaper passive peers tracking similar broad-market beta.
- **Management Quality And Track Record** _(result: Pass)_: A tier-one issuer and over a decade of operational history provide a highly stable operational foundation.
- **Tax Efficiency** _(result: Pass)_: The passive index methodology and structural wrapper avoid the tax pitfalls often associated with active emerging-market trading.

## Report

The fund's headline fee is substantially above the expected baseline for a modern, purely passive single-country tracker. However, its asset scale completely removes any closure risk, while an average daily traded value of `$197.3M` guarantees top-tier execution. The previously mentioned frictionless quoting means a retail round-trip is exceptionally cheap to execute, even if the long-term holding cost remains a drag. Because this is a targeted India Equity exposure, the portfolio is somewhat top-heavy; the largest three holdings (Reliance Industries, HDFC Bank, and ICICI Bank) combine for a `17.11%` weight, maintaining broad large-and-mid-cap coverage without excessive single-group concentration. The portfolio's annual trading activity falls neatly into the standard rebalancing expectations for a market-cap-weighted international strategy. Containing exactly `0` bond holdings and composed entirely of domestic stocks, the underlying Indian equities naturally carry a low dividend yield; consequently, total return is dominated by price appreciation and rupee-dollar currency fluctuations, baking in a structurally high domestic valuation premium. On the tax front, the passive replication approach and in-kind creation and redemption mechanism keep the fund tax-efficient, largely avoiding the structural access risks or tracking drift that plague weaker emerging-market wrappers. Issued by BlackRock, the ETF is backed by one of the largest and most established asset management footprints globally. The fund's launch on `Feb 02, 2012` provides a deep operational history spanning multiple emerging-market cycles and domestic policy shifts. The extended mandate continuity and stable management team eliminate key-person risk, giving retail investors high confidence in the operational machinery tracking the index. The fund's primary strengths are its flawless trading efficiency and deeply established issuer track record. The glaring red flag is the premium access fee for a completely passive basket. For a direct retail alternative, investors should consider the Franklin FTSE India ETF (FLIN), which charges a much cheaper `0.19%`; choosing the BlackRock fund instead means accepting a higher persistent fee drag purely to gain access to deeper options chains and heavier secondary-market volume. Overall, this ETF's cost profile looks mixed because its premier execution quality is offset by a structurally outdated expense ratio.
