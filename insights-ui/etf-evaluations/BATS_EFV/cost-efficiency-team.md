# EFV (BATS) — Cost, Efficiency & Team

_The cost and efficiency profile for EFV is Mixed. While the fund provides institutional-grade liquidity supported by a massive $28.9B asset base and a razor-thin 0.01% bid-ask spread, its 0.33% expense ratio is elevated compared to modern passive core alternatives. It operates highly efficiently internally, generating just 3.90% turnover over its nearly two-decade history since its 2005 inception. Ultimately, investors get flawless execution and a highly reliable track record, but they must accept a slightly higher ongoing holding fee to maintain the precise foreign value tilt._

## Categories

- **Bid Ask Spread And Trading Cost** _(result: Pass)_: Enormous daily volume ensures that retail investors face virtually zero implicit trading friction.
- **Expense Ratio Vs Competition** _(result: Pass)_: The 0.33% expense ratio is standard for established international style funds but higher than modern baseline trackers.
- **Expense Ratio Vs Expected Returns** _(result: Pass)_: The fund accurately delivers its targeted value exposure, though the moderate fee guarantees a persistent drag against cheaper core alternatives.
- **Management Quality And Track Record** _(result: Pass)_: Backed by BlackRock and boasting a nearly two-decade history, the fund offers institutional-grade stability.
- **Tax Efficiency** _(result: Pass)_: The ETF wrapper protects against capital gains, but foreign dividend withholding taxes remain a factor.

## Report

The fund operates a passive strategy designed to track the MSCI EAFE Value Index, providing broad exposure to developed market equities outside the US and Canada that exhibit specific value characteristics. It carries a stated expense ratio of 0.33% (with a net prospectus ratio of 0.31%, indicating a slight fee waiver). While this fee is reasonable within the older cohort of international style-box ETFs, it sits noticeably above the ~0.05% to 0.10% baseline charged by modern, ultra-cheap broad international index funds. Backed by a massive $28.9B in AUM, the fund trades with exceptional liquidity; it averages 4.6M shares and roughly $125M in daily dollar volume, which drives the bid-ask spread down to 0.01%. For retail investors, this means that while the recurring holding cost is slightly elevated, the one-time execution friction for entering or exiting the position is practically zero. Because it employs a straightforward index-tracking methodology, the fund exhibits a very low portfolio turnover of just 3.90%, perfectly aligned with the low single-digit expectations for a broad-market tracker and ensuring minimal internal trading drag. In the foreign value segment, a significant portion of total returns comes from income rather than pure capital appreciation, which is reflected in the fund's robust 30-day SEC yield of 3.03%. From a tax perspective, the ETF wrapper efficiently suppresses capital gain distributions through its in-kind redemption mechanism. However, retail buyers should be mindful that these overseas dividends are structurally subject to foreign withholding taxes and currency exposure, meaning a taxable account will face more tax friction here than with a purely domestic equity fund. Issued by BlackRock's iShares, the ETF benefits from the institutional-grade backing, robust authorized participant network, and scale of the world's largest asset manager. The fund launched in August 2005, giving it a nearly two-decade operational history that proves its resilience across multiple major global market cycles. It is overseen by a team of four managers with the longest individual tenure standing at 13.5 years, which, while largely symbolic for a strictly passive index tracker, reflects a highly stable and well-resourced operation entirely free from closure or mandate-drift risks. The fund's primary strengths are its formidable asset base and deep secondary market liquidity, underscored by the tight 0.01% spread and reliable 3.03% yield. The main drawback is the 0.33% headline expense ratio, which acts as a persistent drag when compared against cheaper core international index funds. For a direct retail alternative, an investor could look to the Vanguard FTSE Developed Markets ETF (VEA, 0.05%), accepting the trade-off of abandoning the targeted value-factor methodology in exchange for a drastically lower fee on broad international exposure. Overall, this ETF's cost profile looks mixed; its flawless liquidity and execution efficiency are excellent, but they must be weighed against a fee that trails the curve in an era of aggressive passive fee compression.
